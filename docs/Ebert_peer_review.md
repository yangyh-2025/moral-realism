### DIMENSION C: COVERAGE GAPS

#### MAJOR gaps

**MAJOR-C.1 — Economic diplomacy absent.** Krueger's "Handelsstaat" thesis emphasizes economic power as substitute for military power. Dawes Plan section focuses on domestic ratification, not international economic diplomacy. Missing: 1924 London Conference, role of American bankers (Dawes, Young, Morgan), inter-allied debt-reparations nexus, trade agreements as diplomatic instruments.
WHY IT MATTERS: Economic diplomacy was arguably Weimar's most successful foreign policy under Ebert.
FIX: Expand Dawes Plan section or add economic statecraft subsection.

**MAJOR-C.2 — Ago von Maltzan and Foreign Office factional landscape underdeveloped.** Maltzan appears only in Rapallo "Pyjama Conference" passage. His broader role as architect of pro-Soviet faction, and the Eastern-vs-Western orientation debate within the Foreign Office, is absent.
WHY IT MATTERS: Ebert mediated between these factions. The institutional landscape clarifies his role as presidential arbiter.
FIX: Add paragraph contextualizing Foreign Office factional debate.

**MAJOR-C.3 — Colonial revisionism entirely absent.** Germany lost colonies at Versailles; colonial revisionism was an active Weimar foreign policy dimension.
WHY IT MATTERS: Even if Ebert personally de-prioritized it, a dimension of the foreign policy landscape is missing.
FIX: Brief acknowledgment that colonial revisionism existed as a policy dimension, noting it was not Ebert's priority.

#### MINOR gaps

**MINOR-C.1 — Brockdorff-Rantzau underdeveloped.** Led Versailles delegation; later ambassador to Moscow (1922-1928).

**MINOR-C.2 — SPD internal foreign policy debates underexplored.** Pacifist wing vs. governance wing tension, and Ebert's dual role as party chairman and head of state.

**MINOR-C.3 — 1924-1925 compressed.** Ebert's final year receives less treatment than 1919-1923.

**MINOR-C.4 — Stresemann-Ebert late-period collaboration thin.** Given Locarno's importance as Ebert's legacy.
