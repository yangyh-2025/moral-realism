## 1. FRIEDRICH EBERT AND THE VERSAILLES TREATY (1919)

### 1.1 The Decision to Accept Versailles

When the terms of the Treaty of Versailles were presented to the German delegation in May 1919, the Weimar government was thrown into an existential crisis. The treaty was almost universally condemned across the German political spectrum as a punitive "dictated peace" (*Diktat*). Friedrich Ebert, as Reichsprasident, found himself at the centre of the most agonising decision faced by any German leader since 1918.

**Key factors that drove the decision:**

- **Military impossibility of resistance:** Chancellor Philipp Scheidemann initially opposed signing. However, when the government consulted the High Command (*Oberste Heeresleitung*), Quartermaster-General Wilhelm Groener provided the crucial assessment: while the army might resist effectively in the east, military resistance in the west against French and American forces was impossible. Field Marshal Paul von Hindenburg concurred on the military assessment but notably added that, as a soldier, he would prefer "honourable annihilation" to a dishonourable peace -- thereby placing the political burden squarely back on the civilian government.

- **Political fracture and resignations:** Scheidemann, unable to reconcile himself to the treaty, resigned, leaving Germany without a government just three days before the armistice's expiration. Ebert approached Otto Landsberg, who explicitly refused to have anything to do with the treaty, even at the cost of the chancellorship.

- **Formation of the Bauer government and ratification:** Gustav Bauer, the former labour minister, agreed to form a new government. Matthias Erzberger, the Centre Party leader who had signed the armistice, became finance minister and was a leading proponent of signing. On **22 June 1919**, the Reichstag ratified the treaty by a vote of 237 to 138. Many prominent Social Democrats, including Scheidemann and Landsberg, were absent.

- **Final capitulation:** The German resolution attempted to exempt the "shame paragraphs" (the war guilt clause, Article 231) and the war crimes provisions. The Allies categorically refused. Just **19 minutes before the deadline expired**, the German delegation was instructed to inform the Allies that Germany accepted the treaty under duress. The signing took place on **28 June 1919** in the Hall of Mirrors at Versailles.

### 1.2 Ebert's Approach to International Norms and Post-War Order

Ebert and his ministers had staked their political legitimacy on the promise that a new, democratic Germany would receive a just peace based on Woodrow Wilson's Fourteen Points. The profound shock was that:

> "The Allies refused to recognize the fact that the November revolution had transformed the Reich into a state in which the people were sovereign... They persisted in regarding the Germany of Ebert and Scheidemann as in no essential regard different from the Junker-dominated Germany of 1914."

This refusal to distinguish between Imperial and Republican Germany was a devastating blow. Ebert's acceptance of Versailles was thus not a genuine embrace of the treaty's principles but an act of coerced necessity -- driven by three stark calculations:

1. **Avoiding the disintegration of the state:** A resumption of hostilities would trigger separatism (particularly in Bavaria) and Bolshevik-style uprisings. The Republic's limited forces could not simultaneously fight an external war and suppress internal insurrection.

2. **Acceptance under duress as the least worst option:** Germany had no power to refuse. The acceptance was explicitly made under protest.

3. **Preserving the unity of the Reich:** The government feared total occupation and dissolution more than a punitive treaty.

### 1.3 Relationship with the Military and Secret Rearmament

The foundation for Weimar's military policy was laid even before the Republic formally existed. In November 1918, Ebert made a secret pact with General Wilhelm Groener -- the **Ebert-Groener Pact**.

**Terms of the arrangement:**
- The military leadership would recognize the legitimacy of Ebert's government and place troops at its disposal
- In return, Ebert agreed to uphold the authority of the officer corps, maintain military discipline, and combat the radical left

**Long-term significance:** This pact created a pattern of mutual dependency between the fragile republican government and the deeply anti-republican military. It was through this bargain -- civilian political cover and state funding in exchange for military protection against internal revolution -- that secret rearmament became possible. The Reichswehr, under **General Hans von Seeckt** (Chief of Army Command from March 1920), cultivated an identity of being "above politics," loyal not to the constitution but to an abstract "Reich."

During the 1920 Kapp Putsch, Seeckt famously refused to order the Reichswehr to fire on the mutinous right-wing troops, saying: *"Reichswehr does not fire on Reichswehr."* This demonstrated the military's practical autonomy from civilian control.

### 1.4 Balancing National Interest with Cooperation

Ebert's approach reveals a consistent pattern: **public compliance with international norms was a tactical necessity forced by military weakness, not a principled commitment to a cooperative international order.** When the "peace of justice" promised by Wilson's Fourteen Points failed to materialise, the democratic leadership was left with no good options. The acceptance of Versailles became the foundational grievance of the right -- the "November Criminals" myth that, as historians Samuel W. Mitcham Jr. and Samuel Halperin have argued, was "the mainspring of all subsequent attacks on the republican regime," contributing directly to the rise of Hitler and the Nazi Party.

## 2. GUSTAV STRESEMANN'S FOREIGN POLICY ARCHITECTURE (1923-1929)

### 2.1 The Fulfillment Policy (*Erfullungspolitik*)

Gustav Stresemann served as Chancellor briefly (1923) and then as Foreign Minister from 1923 until his death in 1929. His *Erfullungspolitik* ("fulfillment policy") was based on the principle of outwardly complying with Versailles -- not out of genuine acceptance, but as a tactical means to:
- Improve relations with Britain and France
- Regain international trust
- Create conditions for peaceful revision of the treaty's harshest terms
- Restore Germany's Great Power status

Stresemann described his approach as distinguishing between "the possible and the impossible," pursuing what he called a policy of "finesse" -- skillfully combining negotiation and bluff from a position of weakness. He understood that Germany was in no military position to challenge the Allies by force, so he used Germany's still-considerable economic weight and strategic importance to negotiate concessions.

Earlier versions of *Erfullungspolitik* had been attempted under Chancellor Joseph Wirth (1921-1922) and Foreign Minister Walther Rathenau, but Stresemann perfected and executed it most successfully.

### 2.2 The Treaty of Rapallo (16 April 1922)

Signed during the Genoa Economic Conference by Foreign Minister Walther Rathenau (under Chancellor Joseph Wirth), the Rapallo Treaty between Germany and Soviet Russia renounced all mutual territorial and financial claims from World War I, restored full diplomatic relations, and established most-favored-nation trade status.

**The hidden dimension**: Rapallo provided diplomatic cover for intensive secret military collaboration that had already begun in 1919-1920. Key facilities established included:
- **Lipetsk**: Secret air training and testing facility where the Reichswehr trained hundreds of pilots. Companies like Junkers, Heinkel, and Dornier developed and tested military aircraft prototypes there.
- **Kazan**: Secret tank school where German engineers tested prototypes (disguised as "tractors" -- *Grossstraktor* and *Leichttraktor*), developed by Daimler-Benz, Krupp, and Rheinmetall.
- **Tomka**: Joint chemical warfare testing facility near Saratov.

The historiographical debate about whether Rapallo was *itself* a secret military pact remains live. Former Chancellor Joseph Wirth later insisted in exile that "the history of Rapallo has not yet been written" and that part of Rapallo involved "special work of a military-political nature." He claimed that **both the Foreign Office and the Chancellor were fully aware and supportive** of linking military cooperation with the treaty.

General Friedrich von Rabenau, Seeckt's biographer, privately admitted in a 1941 letter that his chapter on Rapallo was written with "the most extreme limitation and reserve" and that it "undoubtedly does not bring out many things which [Wirth] knows, things which I also know." Surviving administrative notes from Foreign Office files reference a 1923 memorandum titled *"Preparation for the Completion of the Rapallo Agreement"* that was marked for systematic destruction, mentioning combining "the total material according to its content together with the secret arrangement."

General Helm Speidel, who participated in these operations, confirmed that incriminating documents were **"regularly and systematically destroyed."** Historian Vasilis Vourkoutiotis (2007), examining both German and Russian archival sources, concluded that Rapallo "provided the necessary diplomatic camouflage" for secret military collaboration, making "common cause against the West, with implications to be felt in the decades to come."

### 2.3 The Locarno Treaties (October-December 1925)

The Locarno Treaties, negotiated in October 1925 and signed on **1 December 1925** in London, were the crowning achievement of Stresemann's policy.

**Key provisions:**
- Germany formally recognised its post-Versailles western borders with France and Belgium (accepting the loss of Alsace-Lorraine)
- The demilitarised Rhineland was confirmed; all signatories renounced the use of force and invasion, except in self-defence
- Britain and Italy guaranteed the western borders, protecting France against German aggression and Germany against another French occupation (as in the 1923 Ruhr crisis)
- Arbitration treaties were signed with Poland and Czechoslovakia, renouncing force

**What was deliberately left open:** Stresemann explicitly refused an "Eastern Locarno" guaranteeing Germany's borders with Poland and Czechoslovakia. This left the door open for future peaceful revision of Danzig, the Polish Corridor, and Upper Silesia. Locarno also stipulated that any violation of the demilitarised Rhineland was a "hostile act," though signatories were obligated to respond only to "flagrant violations" -- a subtle but significant weakening of Versailles.

For this achievement, Stresemann and French Foreign Minister Aristide Briand received the **Nobel Peace Prize in 1926**.

### 2.4 Joining the League of Nations (September 1926)

As part of the Locarno framework, Germany joined the League of Nations in **September 1926** with:
- **Great Power status** and a **permanent seat on the League Council**, giving it veto power
- Exemption from participating in collective military action against aggressors, due to Versailles disarmament limitations

This membership was a prerequisite for the Locarno Pact to come fully into operation. Stresemann used Germany's position to raise matters of German national interest, including the treatment of German minorities in Eastern Europe, war guilt, disarmament questions, Danzig, and the Saar -- issues he could now press against the Entente powers from within the institution.

### 2.5 The Dual Strategy: Public Cooperation, Private Revision

The single most revealing document is Stresemann's letter to the former Crown Prince Wilhelm, written on **7 September 1925**, on the eve of the Locarno Conference. In this letter, Stresemann outlined his three major foreign policy goals:

1. Resolution of the reparations question in a sense tolerable for Germany, and securing the peace essential for recovery
2. Protection of "the 10 to 12 million of our kindred who now live under a foreign yoke in foreign lands"
3. **Re-adjustment of Germany's eastern frontiers** -- recovery of Danzig, the Polish Corridor, and correction of the Upper Silesian frontier (with *Anschluss* of German-Austria as a background aspiration)

The most explosive passage:
> "The most important thing... is the liberation of German territory from foreign occupation. We must first get the strangler from our neck. Therefore German policy, as Metternich said of Austria after 1809, must in this respect consist first in showing finesse [*finassieren*] and avoiding fundamental decisions."

The word *finassieren* (French *finasser* -- to use cunning, trickery, or weaseling) proved explosive when published posthumously. Critics seized on it as proof that Stresemann's Locarno diplomacy was duplicitous -- a mask for unreconstructed nationalist revisionism.

**Stresemann's logic in the letter:**
- Renunciation of war against France (over Alsace-Lorraine) was "purely theoretical" since Germany lacked military force anyway
- The Security Pact (Locarno) would guarantee peace, secure British (and possibly Italian) guarantors for Germany's western frontier, and prevent future French invasions
- Entry into the League of Nations gave Germany a permanent Council seat with veto power, a platform to champion German minorities, and a forum to raise uncomfortable issues against the Entente powers
- Germany must first get the "strangler" (France) off its neck through evacuation of occupied territory, then methodically pursue eastern revision
- He warned against "coquetting with Bolshevism" or a German-Russian alliance, conscious of the danger of being caught between East and West

On his deathbed on **3 October 1929**, Stresemann reportedly said: **"We are again masters in our house."** Barely three weeks later, the Wall Street Crash triggered the Great Depression that helped doom the Weimar Republic.

In his Nobel Prize lecture (1927), Stresemann articulated his public philosophy:
> "The great men of a nation reach out to all mankind. They are unifying, not divisive; internationally conciliating and still great nationally."

## 3. THE SOVEREIGNTY AND MILITARY QUESTIONS

### 3.1 The Black Reichswehr and Secret Rearmament

The term "Black Reichswehr" (*Schwarze Reichswehr*) refers to illegal paramilitary formations operating under Reichswehr command -- composed of Freikorps members, nationalist leagues, and labour battalions (*Arbeitskommandos*). They were concentrated in garrisons, notably the Kustrin fortress near Berlin, and served as a manpower reserve beyond the 100,000-man Versailles limit.

These formations were also involved in political violence and "Feme murders" (vigilante killings of perceived traitors and informers). Seeckt tolerated them as a means to expand Germany's effective fighting strength, although he drew a line when a faction under Major Buchrucker launched an independent putsch at Kustrin in 1923, which the regular Reichswehr itself had to put down. This convinced Seeckt that only the "legal" path could lead to the reconstruction of military power.

**General Hans von Seeckt** was the central architect. He established a special task force known as **"Sondergruppe R"** (Special Group Russia), led by **Oskar von Niedermayer** (sometimes called "the German Lawrence of Arabia"), which handled the logistics of the secret military relationship with the Soviet Union, operating largely independently of the German Foreign Office.

### 3.2 Soviet Military Cooperation

The cooperation evolved through several stages:

- **Early contacts (1919-1921):** Karl Radek, a Bolshevik emissary, famously held a "political salon" from his Berlin prison cell (and later house arrest), where he received high-ranking German visitors to discuss military and economic collaboration. Seeckt also used the Ottoman war minister Enver Pasha as an emissary, while the Soviets sent Viktor Kopp to Berlin.

- **The 1926 Manchester Guardian scandal:** In December 1926, the *Manchester Guardian* published detailed revelations about this cooperation, reporting that Reichswehr officers travelled to and from Russia with false passports and that secret munitions exchanges were occurring. This scandal brought down the government of Chancellor Wilhelm Marx and led indirectly to Seeckt's resignation.

### 3.3 Military Force Limitations and Their Circumvention

The Versailles Treaty (Part V) limited the Reichswehr to 100,000 men and prohibited tanks, aircraft, submarines, heavy artillery, and a General Staff. The circumvention was systematic and multifaceted:

**The General Staff preserved in disguise:** The treaty specifically outlawed the German General Staff. Seeckt simply renamed it the ***Truppenamt*** (Troop Office) and ensured it retained all the functions of strategic planning and doctrine development.

**Submarine development abroad:** The German Navy secretly financed submarine design through a Krupp-owned front company in the Netherlands, the *Ingenieurskantoor voor Scheepsbouw* (IvS) in The Hague. IvS sold submarine designs and oversaw construction for Spain, Finland, Turkey, and Japan, whose specifications perfectly matched future German U-boat types.

**Naval aviation maintained covertly:** The Navy, under Captain **Walter Lohmann**, managed vast secret funds (over 100 million Goldmarks) from the illegal sale of ships meant for scrapping and from government slush funds. This money financed clandestine naval and aviation programs, including the *Severa* seaplane pilot division, speedboat development, and investment in film companies (*Phoebus Film AG*) as financial fronts. The **1927 Lohmann Affair** scandal exposed these machinations, forcing Defence Minister Otto Gessler's resignation, but the rearmament itself continued under his successor, Groener.

**Dual-use civilian structures:** The state-backed airline **Lufthansa**, formed in 1926, was deeply integrated with the military. Under its managing director, Erhard Milch, pilot training programs created a ready reserve of combat airmen. Civilian glider and flying clubs were promoted heavily to create a pool of potential military pilots, all while strictly denying any military purpose.

**Doctrinal development for future war:** The Reichswehr was reconceived as a *Fuhrerheer* (an army of leaders), where every soldier was trained to take on the responsibilities of a higher rank, providing a massive cadre of trained officers and NCOs for a future mass army. Maneuvers, wargames, and theoretical studies were conducted intensively, using mock-ups (cardboard tanks) in public but developing the revolutionary combined-arms and mobile warfare doctrines that would later become *Blitzkrieg*.

## 4. HISTORIOGRAPHICAL ASSESSMENT

### 4.1 Genuine Commitment to Peace vs. Tactical Pragmatism

This is the central debate. Two broad positions exist:

**Position 1: The "Good European" interpretation (dominant post-1945 through the 1960s):**
- Stresemann and Briand were visionaries of European cooperation
- The Locarno Treaties represented a genuine breakthrough in international reconciliation
- The Nobel Peace Prize was deserved recognition of sincere peacemaking
- The "spirit of Locarno" was real and represented a new approach to European security

**Position 2: The revisionist/realist interpretation (1950s onward, using Stresemann's private papers):**
- Stresemann was a committed German nationalist who used democratic means for nationalist ends
- His public conciliation was tactical -- designed to extract concessions from a position of weakness
- The Crown Prince letter reveals that his ultimate goals (eastern border revision, *Anschluss*, restoration of Great Power status) were fundamentally revisionist
- The difference between Stresemann and Hitler was one of *method*, not ultimate *aims*
- As one historian notes: "The difference between the two men was in method. Stresemann sought revision by common consent with the idea of giving his successes a sense of permanence. Hitler chose to achieve the changes by force."

**The balanced contemporary view** holds that both interpretations contain truth. Stresemann was genuinely committed to *peaceful* means -- which is a profound moral and practical difference from Hitler -- but his aims were revisionist and nationalist. He was a "pragmatic conservative" who believed Germany could regain its position through economic strength and diplomatic skill rather than war. As Jonathan Wright, Stresemann's biographer, has argued, the commitment to peaceful means was itself a significant normative choice, not merely tactical.

### 4.2 Evidence on Both Sides

**Evidence for genuine commitment to peace/cooperation:**
- Stresemann repeatedly and consistently chose negotiation over confrontation
- He accepted Germany's western borders as permanent (a huge concession from any German nationalist)
- He built genuine personal rapport with Briand, Austen Chamberlain, and other Western statesmen
- He steered Germany away from a purely revisionist alliance with the Soviet Union, writing in 1925: *"To celebrate a marriage with communist Russia would mean to go to bed with the killer of one's own fatherland"*
- His economic logic -- that German prosperity required European stability -- was sincere and correct
- The Locarno achievement was a real, binding commitment to peaceful dispute resolution

**Evidence for tactical pragmatism:**
- The Crown Prince letter's language of *finassieren* and systematic revision
- All three major foreign policy goals he listed were revisionist
- He refused to guarantee eastern borders, keeping revision options open
- Secret military cooperation with the USSR -- including Lipetsk, Kazan, and Tomka -- continued throughout his tenure
- Reichswehr illegal rearmament, managed by Seeckt and Gessler, was tolerated and funded by the state
- His deathbed words about being "masters in our house" reveal an underlying nationalist pride

### 4.3 The Continuity Question: Weimar to Third Reich

The question of whether Weimar foreign policy laid the groundwork for the Third Reich involves several dimensions:

**Argument for continuity:**
- The secret rearmament programs of the 1920s provided the technical and doctrinal base that the Nazis exploited after 1933
- Weimar's revisionist goals (eastern border revision, *Anschluss*, restoration of Great Power status) were shared by Hitler, even if the *means* differed
- Heinrich August Winkler's "Reich myth" thesis identifies an ideological continuity -- anti-democratic, authoritarian traditions that formed a "viable bridge between Hitler and educated Germany"
- Stresemann's very success in conditioning the Allies to make concessions in return for Germany's cooperation created precedents and expectations that Hitler later exploited
- The Versailles system's perceived illegitimacy was a grievance nurtured and weaponised throughout the Weimar period

**Argument for discontinuity:**
- The Foreign Office under Weimar (especially Stresemann) had real policy influence; under Hitler it was marginalised -- Hitler acted as "his own Foreign Minister"
- The *methods* were fundamentally different: Stresemann's multilateralism, negotiation, and institutional engagement vs. Hitler's unilateralism, coercion, and institutional destruction
- Weimar sought revision *within* an international framework; the Third Reich sought destruction of that framework entirely
- Eberhard Kolb's scholarship emphasises the rupture of 1933 -- a new regime with new methods and goals qualitatively different from anything in Weimar
- The Locarno spirit, Kellogg-Briand Pact, and League of Nations membership represented genuine integration into international institutions that Hitler explicitly repudiated
- The 1939 Hitler-Stalin Pact was tactically similar to Rapallo in some respects, but fundamentally different: Rapallo was a long-term strategic partnership with primarily economic objectives, not a tactical prelude to aggressive war and partition of Poland

**The scholarly consensus** has evolved toward a nuanced position: there were structural continuities (especially in the military and economic spheres) that the Nazis exploited, but the *political* break of 1933 was real. Stresemann's revisionism sought to restore Germany's position *within* a European order; Hitler's sought to destroy and replace that order with racial empire. The two projects are connected by shared grievances but separated by a qualitative difference in methods, goals, and normative frameworks.

| Historian | Position on Continuity |
|-----------|----------------------|
| **Eberhard Kolb** | Emphasises the rupture of 1933; Weimar diplomatic achievements were real; focuses on the institutional break; sees Stresemann's foreign policy as genuinely distinct |

| **Vasilis Vourkoutiotis** (2007) | Concluded Rapallo "provided the necessary diplomatic camouflage" for military collaboration, making "common cause against the West" |
| **Wolfram von Scheliha** (2007) | Challenged Vourkoutiotis: cooperation was strategic/economic, not an anti-Western alliance; Rapallo was fundamentally different from the 1939 Hitler-Stalin Pact |

## 6. ANALYTICAL CONCLUSION: IMPLICATIONS FOR THE MORAL-ABM MODEL

The Weimar case offers several critical insights for modelling how state leaders balance national interest with international cooperation:

1. **Cooperation as strategic instrument, not normative commitment:** Both Ebert and Stresemann participated in international institutions not because they believed in the normative order those institutions represented, but because cooperation was the most effective available means to advance national interests under conditions of military weakness. The ABM should model "cooperative behaviour" as potentially driven by either normative commitment OR strategic calculation -- these are observationally equivalent in the short term but diverge over time.

2. **The dual strategy is the norm, not the exception:** Stresemann's public cooperation / private revision model reflects a pattern that likely generalises to other constrained powers in hierarchical international orders. States comply publicly with norms they privately seek to revise. Coding this duality -- with separate public and private preference parameters -- is essential for model realism.

3. **Military constraint shapes the cooperation calculus:** Ebert and Stresemann both chose cooperation from a position of military weakness. The ABM should explicitly model how military capability (or its absence) constrains or enables revisionist behaviour. Weaker states have stronger incentives for instrumental cooperation.

4. **Temporal dimension matters:** Stresemann's strategy was explicitly sequential -- first remove constraints (occupation, reparations), then pursue revision. Models that collapse future intentions into present behaviour may misrepresent the dynamics. Agent time horizons and sequencing preferences should be explicit parameters.

5. **The continuity/discontinuity question maps onto agent-level parameters:** Whether a leader pursues revision *within* or *outside* the international order -- the key distinction between Stresemann and Hitler -- depends on parameters like respect for international law, preference for multilateralism, institutional engagement, and time horizon. These should be explicit agent attributes in the model, with different combinations producing qualitatively different foreign policy orientations from similar underlying revisionist preferences.

6. **The Ebert-Groener dynamic shows civil-military bargains shape compliance:** The Weimar case demonstrates that civilian leaders' willingness and ability to comply with international norms depends partly on their domestic civil-military bargain. Ebert traded military autonomy for regime survival. The ABM should consider modelling domestic institutional constraints (including civil-military relations) as factors that enable or constrain compliance with international obligations.
