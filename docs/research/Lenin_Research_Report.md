## 1. REVOLUTIONARY FOREIGN POLICY AND REJECTION OF TRADITIONAL DIPLOMACY

The October 1917 Revolution produced the most radical challenge to traditional diplomacy in modern history. The Bolsheviks rejected the fundamental premises of the Westphalian system -- national interest, sovereign equality, non-intervention, professional diplomatic corps -- in favor of transnational class warfare conducted by a revolutionary vanguard.

KEY EVIDENCE:

Lenin's Decree on Peace (November 8, 1917), adopted by the Second All-Russia Congress of Soviets, declared: "The government abolishes secret diplomacy, and, for its part, announces its firm intention to conduct all negotiations quite openly in full view of the whole people. It will proceed immediately with the full publication of the secret treaties."

The Decree defined annexation as: "every incorporation of a small or weak nation into a large or powerful state without the precisely, clearly and voluntarily expressed consent and wish of that nation, irrespective of the time when such forcible incorporation took place, irrespective also of the degree of development or backwardness of the nation forcibly annexed."

The Narkomindel (NKID) was created as a temporary Commissariat of Foreign Affairs under Trotsky, pending the expected world revolution. Soviet embassies abolished traditional ranks and protocol; in the Soviet mission in Italy, all staff "down to the porters, used the same dining room and ate the same food."

A "Janus-faced" dual diplomacy emerged: the NKID under Chicherin pursued traditional diplomatic recognition and Westphalian norms, while the Comintern conducted openly revolutionary agitation. Chicherin repeatedly petitioned the Politburo to separate Comintern activities from government diplomacy, complaining that Comintern rhetoric was "spoiling" relations with Germany and Turkey. Lenin supported some separation in 1921, directing that Soviet diplomats not engage in propaganda: "We have the Comintern for such purposes." But institutional separation was never complete. Politburo members sat on the Comintern Executive Committee, diplomats had Comintern ties, and embassies housed Comintern emissaries throughout the 1920s.

On December 26, 1917, Lenin and Trotsky signed a Sovnarkom decree appropriating two million rubles for the "revolutionary international movement," explicitly stating that Soviet power "bases itself on principles of international solidarity of the proletariat." This illustrates the early fusion of diplomacy with revolutionary agitation.

SCHOLARLY SOURCES:

Jon Jacobson, When the Soviet Union Entered World Politics (UC Press, 1994), argues that "whatever integration was attained was achieved more in theory than in actuality" -- the dialectic of revolution and coexistence was "clumsy" and often counterproductive. Jacobson's detailed analysis traces how the Bolsheviks developed "not one but two foreign policies" -- one coordinating revolutionary efforts through the Comintern, the other pursuing conventional diplomatic and commercial relations.

Lantsov and Lantsova (2017), "The October Revolution's Foreign Policy Program: Origins and Results," Vestnik RUDN. International Relations, Vol. 17, No. 2, pp. 233-245. Analyzes how Bolshevik leaders "initially viewed [the October Revolution] as an integral part of the world revolution" and that the "Leninist conception of the world revolution supposed a close link between the labor movement and national liberation movements in colonial and semi-colonial countries."

Benjamin Carlos Montoya (UNLV Master's Thesis), "Revolutionary Idealists to Revolutionary Statesmen: Bolshevik Foreign Policy, 1914-1922." Traces how Bolsheviks transformed from revolutionary emigres to statesmen who "incorporated socialist revolution into state power." By early 1921, Narkomindel conceded that "the attempt to run two organizations parallel and independent has proved a failure," leading to the "absolute subordination" of the Comintern to state interests.

R.C. Nation, Black Earth, Red Star: A History of Soviet Security Policy, argues that at Rapallo (1922), Soviet Russia "came to terms with the 'dilemma of coexistence'" by allying with Germany, breaking diplomatic isolation through traditional balance-of-power strategy.

Umut Bekcan, "The Aftermath of the Revolution: Necessary Foreign Policy of the Bolsheviks 1917-1925," Journal of the Faculty of Political Sciences (Ankara), examines how world revolution's failure and economic difficulties compelled Soviet leaders to base foreign policy on "protecting the revolution and gaining diplomatic recognition in the international arena."
ANALYTICAL PATTERNS:
The NKID was created as a temporary Commissariat, conditional on world revolution's arrival -- pragmatism overriding ideological purity from the outset (Pattern 1). The Decree on Peace represented maximum revolutionary ambition, calling for global revolution and abolition of secret diplomacy when the Bolsheviks controlled only a fraction of Russian territory (Pattern 2). The abolition of secret diplomacy was itself deployed as a selective weapon, exposing Entente secret treaties while the Bolsheviks had none to expose themselves (Pattern 3). The Janus-faced dual diplomacy -- NKID statecraft alongside Comintern agitation, funded by the December 26, 1917 two-million-ruble appropriation -- was born here as a deliberate institutionalized duality (Pattern 4). Stalin would later resolve this duality by liquidating one pole: Comintern dissolution in 1943, all foreign policy subordinated to direct state control (Pattern 5).

## 2. TREATY OF BREST-LITOVSK (MARCH 3, 1918)

CONTEXT: The Russian army had disintegrated -- trenches were nearly empty, soldiers were deserting en masse. Peace negotiations opened at Brest-Litovsk (in modern Belarus) in December 1917. The German terms were catastrophic: Russia would lose approximately 56 million people (one-third of its population), 32% of its best agricultural land, 54% of its industrial undertakings, 73% of its iron ore output, and 89% of its coal mines. Sovereignty over Finland, Estonia, Livonia, Courland, Lithuania, Russian Poland, Ukraine, and parts of Belarus was renounced. The Ottoman Empire was ceded Ardahan, Kars, and Batumi in Transcaucasia.

THE THREE FACTIONS IN THE BOLSHEVIK LEADERSHIP:

Faction 1: Immediate Peace (Lenin). Accept any terms at any cost -- a "breathing spell" (peredyshka) for regime survival. Lenin argued that with virtually no army remaining, continuation of war meant certain military collapse and the overthrow of the Bolshevik government. He characterized the Left's stance as suicidal romanticism -- "dying in a beautiful pose, sword in hand, saying: 'Peace is disgraceful, war is honourable.'"

Faction 2: Revolutionary War (Bukharin and the Left Communists). Reject any negotiated peace with imperialism on principle. Argue that signing with German imperialism would betray the international proletariat and demoralize the German socialist movement. Advocate for a revolutionary guerrilla war, believing the peasantry would rise up in a patriotic guerrilla war if invaded, and that the struggle would inspire uprisings in Germany and Austria-Hungary.

Faction 3: Neither War Nor Peace (Trotsky). Declare the war ended (demobilize the Russian army) but refuse to sign the formal peace treaty. Use the negotiations as a platform for revolutionary propaganda. Believe the German military might be incapable of launching a renewed offensive. If the Germans did attack, the demonstration would expose German imperialism to the world's workers. Trotsky explained his rationale: "I maintained that before we proceeded to sign the peace it was absolutely imperative that we should prove to the workers of Europe, in a most striking manner, how great, how deadly, was our hatred for the rulers of Germany."

THE DECISIVE MOMENT:

At a Petrograd party meeting on January 8, 1918: Lenin received 15 votes, Trotsky 16, Bukharin 32 -- the Left Communists commanded the largest faction.

On February 10, 1918, Trotsky announced "neither war nor peace" at Brest-Litovsk. The German delegation was initially stunned. On February 18, the Germans resumed their offensive (Operation Faustschlag), sweeping through the Baltic region and Ukraine almost unopposed. Trotsky's gamble had failed.

On February 23, 1918, facing a German ultimatum with even harsher terms than originally offered, Lenin threatened to resign from both the government and Central Committee if peace was not accepted. He warned: "If you do not do this, then you will sign the death warrant of the Soviet power in three weeks." Addressing Stalin's suggestion, Lenin stated: "Stalin is wrong when he says that we do not need to sign. We must sign these conditions."

The final Central Committee vote: 7 in favor, 4 against, 4 abstentions. Trotsky abstained to give Lenin his majority without personally endorsing the policy, preserving party unity. The treaty was signed on March 3, 1918, by a subordinate (Trotsky refused to sign personally).

LENIN'S RATIONALE:

"For the moment our revolution is more important than everything else; we must make it sure, cost what it may." Lenin viewed the treaty as a necessary tactical retreat: accepting immediate strategic losses for long-term state survival.

CONSEQUENCES:

First, the treaty was annulled within 8 months when Germany was defeated by the Western Allies (November 1918), vindicating Lenin's gamble. Germany was forced to renounce the treaty as a condition of the Armistice of Compiegne.

Second, annulment was formalized in Article 116 of the Treaty of Versailles.

Third, scholarship argues Brest-Litovsk became a blueprint for the Versailles peace -- its harshness convinced the Allies to impose punitive terms on Germany, creating the mutual "outcast" dynamic that directly led to the Treaty of Rapallo (1922).

Fourth, a supplementary agreement (August 1918) compelled Russia to pay 6 billion marks in reparations to Germany.

Fifth, the Left SRs (coalition partners in the government) walked out of Sovnarkom in protest and later assassinated the German ambassador, Count Mirbach, triggering an uprising.

Sixth, the treaty was a major catalyst for the Russian Civil War, alienating nationalists, patriots, and the Allied powers, who began their military intervention.

HISTORIOGRAPHY OF BREST-LITOVSK:

Classical interpretation -- John Wheeler-Bennett (Brest-Litovsk: The Forgotten Peace, 1938): Foundational text. Argues that Ludendorff was "the involuntary saviour of Bolshevism" because the "breathing spell" allowed Bolshevik consolidation. Traced Hitler's Lebensraum directly to Brest-Litovsk, calling the Nazi leader "the offspring of the Peace of Brest-Litovsk."

Classical interpretation -- George Kennan (Russia Leaves the War, 1956): Meticulous diplomatic history documenting Lenin and Trotsky's desperate last-minute feelers to Allied representatives (Raymond Robins and Bruce Lockhart) for military aid against Germany before the Congress of Soviets ratified the treaty. Kennan's work underscores the contingency of the moment and the Bolsheviks' pragmatic bid for survival.

Classical interpretation -- Richard Pipes (The Russian Revolution, 1990): Argues that while the treaty saved Lenin, the Allied victory in WWI ultimately "saved Soviet Russia" from German domination.

Revisionist interpretation -- Borislav Chernev (Twilight of Empire: The Brest-Litovsk Conference and the Remaking of East-Central Europe, 1917-1918, 2017): Challenges the narrative of Brest-Litovsk as a backward-looking, vindictive diktat. Argues the negotiations represented "the beginnings of self-determination, decolonization, and nation-state creation" -- the dawn of a new kind of 20th-century diplomacy. Emphasizes the clash of worldviews: ideological revolutionaries engaging in theatrics versus pragmatist German diplomats.

Revisionist interpretation -- Alexander Sagomonyan (2014): Russian historian who directly challenges the Western "betrayal" trope. Using archival evidence, argues that "it was the Allies who effectively deserted Russia." Lenin's last-minute offer -- via Robins and Lockhart -- to refuse ratification of the treaty if Britain and the US guaranteed military aid was ignored by both governments, leaving Lenin no choice but to sign and ratify.

Revisionist interpretation -- Ronald I. Kowalski, "Brest-Litovsk" in The Bolshevik Party in Conflict (1991): Detailed analysis of the intra-party struggle and the Left Communist faction.

Key source: Yuri Felshtinsky, Lenin, Trotsky, Germany and the Treaty of Brest-Litovsk: The Collapse of the World Revolution, November 1917-November 1918 (2012). Examines the intra-party debate across dedicated chapters on "The Problem of Revolutionary War," "Trotsky's Middle Way (Neither War nor Peace)," and "Lenin's Middle Way (The Respite)."
ANALYTICAL PATTERNS:
This is the purest example of pragmatism overriding ideology: Lenin accepted the most humiliating territorial losses in Russian history, calling it a peredyshka (breathing spell) for regime survival, threatening to resign to force acceptance (Pattern 1). Bukharin's "revolutionary war" represented maximum revolutionary ambition -- a guerrilla war to ignite German proletarian revolution, commanding the largest faction with 32 votes at the January 8, 1918 meeting (Pattern 2). The Decree on Peace's principle of "no annexations" was immediately violated by accepting German territorial demands that stripped Russia of one-third of its population (Pattern 3). The Brest-Litovsk debate temporarily suspended the dualism: the entire party apparatus converged on a single existential question of state survival, the exception that proved the rule of dual-track foreign policy (Pattern 4). The peredyshka concept became the intellectual foundation for Stalin's "socialism in one country" -- continuity of concept, rupture of meaning, as the temporary became permanent (Pattern 5).

## 3. THE COMINTERN (THIRD INTERNATIONAL): FOUNDING, PURPOSE, AND ROLE

FOUNDING:

The Communist International (Comintern), also known as the Third International, was founded at a congress held in Moscow from March 2-6, 1919, chaired by Lenin. Only approximately 52 delegates from 34 parties and organizations attended, most already residing in Russia as travel was extremely difficult during the Civil War. The sole German delegate, Hugo Eberlein, had been mandated by his party to oppose immediate formation of the new International, but he abstained rather than voting against it. The remaining delegates voted unanimously to establish the Comintern.

IMPETUS:

The Comintern was born from the collapse of the Second International (1889-1916), which had fractured during World War I. Most socialist parties in the belligerent nations had supported their own governments' war efforts -- a move Lenin denounced as betrayal and "social-chauvinism." At the 1915 Zimmerwald Conference, Lenin organized the "Zimmerwald Left" to advocate transforming the imperialist war into a transnational civil war. After the Bolsheviks seized power, Lenin moved quickly to establish a new, explicitly revolutionary international to counter efforts to revive the reformist Second International.

LENIN'S STATED PURPOSES:

Purpose 1: World proletarian revolution. The program called for "the overthrow of the international bourgeoisie and the creation of an international Soviet republic as a transition stage to the complete abolition of the State."

Purpose 2: "General Staff of the World Revolution." A centralized, disciplined body coordinating communist parties internationally, modeled on the Bolshevik Party's democratic centralism, with Lenin's principle that discipline must be "iron discipline bordering on military discipline."

Purpose 3: Break with reformism. Countering efforts to revive the Second International. The Comintern was designed to discredit and supplant reformist social democrats. As Lenin said, leadership in the revolutionary International "has passed for a short time...to the Russians."

Purpose 4: Defense of Soviet Russia. Lenin and other Bolsheviks genuinely believed that the survival of the Soviet state depended on the spread of revolution to advanced capitalist countries, particularly Germany. Without that, they feared being crushed by the military might of world capitalism, as the Paris Commune had been in 1871.

IMMEDIATE PRAGMATIC MOTIVES (1919):

Beyond world revolution, Lenin had immediate pragmatic reasons for creating the Comintern: breaking Soviet Russia's diplomatic isolation, countering the capitalist League of Nations (seen as an anti-Soviet alliance), and strengthening the Bolshevik negotiating position with the Western powers -- the Comintern's very existence served as a diplomatic lever.

THE TWENTY-ONE CONDITIONS (Second Congress, July-August 1920):

These strict membership requirements, formulated under Lenin's guidance, included: all propaganda must be genuinely communist and press organs controlled by reliable communists (Condition 1); reformists and centrists to be removed from all responsible party posts (Condition 2); parties must combine legal and illegal work (Condition 3); systematic propaganda in armed forces (Condition 4); systematic agitation in rural areas (Condition 5); complete and absolute break with reformism, naming specific individuals to be excluded -- Kautsky, Hilferding, Turati, Longuet, MacDonald (Condition 7); support for colonial liberation movements (Condition 8); systematic communist work in trade unions (Condition 9); parties organized on democratic centralism with "iron discipline bordering on military discipline" (Condition 13); periodic purges to remove petty-bourgeois elements (Condition 14); unconditional support for every Soviet republic (Condition 15); all Comintern decisions binding on member parties (Condition 17); parties must rename as "Communist Party of [Country] (Section of the Third International)" -- a political, not merely formal, requirement (Condition 18); party members rejecting the conditions must be expelled (Condition 21).

These conditions caused major splits in socialist parties across Germany (Independent Social Democratic Party, USPD), France (French Section of the Workers' International, SFIO), and Italy (Italian Socialist Party, PSI). Revolutionary majorities or minorities broke away to form communist parties that accepted the conditions, while reformist elements remained in social-democratic organizations.

EVOLUTION:

When world revolution failed to materialize -- the German and Hungarian revolutions were defeated by 1920-21 -- Soviet policy shifted. Under Stalin, the Comintern was openly wielded as a tool of Soviet state foreign policy. It backed the "Popular Front" against fascism in 1935 when the Soviet Union sought allies against Nazi Germany, then pivoted to condemn the "imperialist war" after the 1939 Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact, and pivoted again to anti-fascist resistance after Germany invaded the USSR in 1941. It was finally dissolved by Stalin in 1943 to allay Allied fears of communist subversion during World War II.

COMINTERN HISTORIOGRAPHY: THE ARCHIVAL REVOLUTION

Prior to 1987, Comintern studies suffered from a dual deficit. Western scholarship (particularly Theodore Draper in the early 1970s) had subsumed Comintern studies into Russian/Soviet studies, treating the International as "virtually an instrument of Soviet foreign policy" -- an epiphenomenon of Kremlin politics rather than an autonomous subject. Official Soviet historiography presented the Comintern as a grand, monolithic narrative built from official documents, "vehemently rejecting any linkage between policy considerations of the Soviet state and the policies and programmes of the Comintern." Both approaches converged on treating the Comintern as a single metanarrative, just from opposing political valences.

With glasnost and the opening of Comintern archives in Moscow (1987, fully after 1991), Russian historians led by F. I. Firsov (head of the Comintern archives), K. Shirinia, Alexander Vatlin, and Igor Krivoguz pioneered a self-critical reassessment. Key findings: the Comintern underwent a fundamental transformation after Lenin's death; after 1934-35, it emerged as an instrument of Soviet state control, with an "avalanche of terror, repression and liquidation" unleashed within its apparatus; parallel micro-discourses of dissent and alternative visions existed alongside the official line; and an alternative perspective, primarily represented by Bukharin, called for pluralism, diversity, and autonomy in Comintern-national section relations, as opposed to Stalin's ultra-centralism.

In the 1990s, British and Western historiography consolidated into two camps, as mapped by I. A. Suzdaltsev (Moscow). Traditionalists emphasize the Comintern as an instrument of Soviet foreign policy, controlled from Moscow, drawing on earlier Cold War-era frameworks. Revisionists (exemplified by Brigitte Studer, Travellers of the World Revolution: A Global History of the Communist International, 2020/2023) stress the agency of national sections, "entanglements" with labor and anti-colonial movements, and shift the scholarly frame toward social history of the Comintern through the life biographies of its employees and the daily practices that made transnational revolutionary networks possible.
ANALYTICAL PATTERNS:
The Comintern was conceived as the "General Staff of World Revolution" but from founding served immediate pragmatic goals: breaking diplomatic isolation, countering the League of Nations, strengthening Bolshevik negotiating leverage (Pattern 1). The Twenty-One Conditions (1920) represented revolutionary ambition at its most institutionally aggressive -- constructing a centralized global apparatus spanning dozens of countries under Moscow's command, permanently splitting the left between communist and social-democratic camps (Pattern 2). "Proletarian internationalism" was operationalized as obedience to Moscow: Condition 17 made all Comintern decisions binding, and Condition 18 required parties to rename as "Section of the Third International" (Pattern 3). The Comintern institutionalized the structural dualism: Chicherin repeatedly complained Comintern rhetoric was "spoiling" diplomatic relations, yet Politburo members sat on the Executive Committee and embassies housed Comintern emissaries throughout the 1920s (Pattern 4). Stalin's 1943 dissolution of the Comintern was the final resolution -- the revolutionary pole liquidated in favor of total state control. Clara Zetkin lamented it had become "a dead mechanism... swallowing orders in Russian and regurgitating them in different languages" (Pattern 5).

## 4. LENIN'S APPROACH TO INTERNATIONAL NORMS AND SOVEREIGNTY

Academic analysis reveals a three-layered approach to international norms and sovereignty, rather than simple rejection.

LAYER 1 -- THEORETICAL REJECTION:

Sovereignty and international law are bourgeois constructs that obscure class rule and must ultimately be transcended. The Soviet theory of sovereignty was "functionally related to the requirements of Soviet foreign policy" (Jones, 1990, Marxist-Leninist Doctrine and the Soviet Theory of Sovereignty). Marxist-Leninist doctrine transposed vertical class-struggle concepts (proletarian solidarity against capitalism, the inevitability of socialism's victory) onto the horizontal plane of the modern state system.

E. A. Korovin, the most systematic early Bolshevik theorist of international law, in his 1924 work The International Law of the Transitional Period, articulated several radical positions. He denied the existence of a single, universal international law binding on all states -- traditional international law was valid only insofar as the Soviet Union explicitly acknowledged it. He argued that during the transitional period before world revolution, three different systems of international law coexisted: Continental European international law, Anglo-American international law, and a special "transitory" international law governing relations with the Soviet Union. He rejected the traditional concept of the state as a uniform legal personality, holding that only the relevant organizations of the dominant class were subjects of international law -- a direct application of Marxist class analysis. He concluded that "an intercourse on the basis of intellectual unity (ideological solidarity) between countries of bourgeois and Socialist cultures cannot exist as a rule, and hence the rules of international law covering this intercourse become pointless" -- certain practical rules could apply in limited areas (postal communication, health regulation), but only as a temporary expedient.

LAYER 2 -- TACTICAL AFFIRMATION:

Self-determination and sovereignty are indispensable weapons for anti-imperialist struggle and must be defended vigorously in international forums. A Chinese academic analysis (Chinese Academy of Social Sciences) notes that while Marxist-Leninist theory describes the state as an organ of class rule and thus rejects the bourgeois conception of sovereignty as a class-neutral abstraction, this "certainly does not mean that in international relations national sovereignty...should be denied." On the contrary, sovereignty was upheld as "the nucleus of all the fundamental principles of international law" when it served anti-imperialist purposes.

Lenin himself, addressing existing international treaties on November 8, 1917, declared: "We reject all clauses on plunder and violence, but we shall welcome all clauses containing provisions for good-neighbourly relations and all economic agreements; we cannot reject these." This reveals the dual character of the Bolshevik approach from the outset: an ideological rejection of "reactionary" international law alongside a pragmatic acceptance of norms that served practical coexistence.

The three categories of nations in Lenin's scheme: advanced capitalist economies (Western Europe, U.S.) -- ripe for proletarian revolution; Eastern Europe (including Russia) -- intermediate; and "semi-colonial" peoples (China, Turkey, Persia, colonies) -- right to self-determination strategically supported.

LAYER 3 -- INSTRUMENTAL APPLICATION:

Norms are always conditional upon the judgment of the revolutionary vanguard, creating an inherent tension between proclaimed rights and actual practice. An eScholarship analysis argues that both Lenin and Wilson ultimately practiced an "internationalized conception of self-determination whereby an external force authorizes a people's political existence by enforcing that force's desires." The conclusion: "The people were never intended to be able to decide these issues for themselves."

PEACEFUL COEXISTENCE: ORIGINS (1917-1924):

Contrary to the common assumption that "peaceful coexistence" was a Khrushchev-era invention, the doctrine has its roots firmly in the earliest years of Soviet power.

The term was first used by Bukharin -- not Lenin -- as a gibe against Lenin's "breathing-space" (peredyshka) tactic. At the Seventh Special Party Congress in March 1918, Bukharin argued derisively that "peaceful coexistence between us, between the Soviet Republic and international capital, cannot be."

Lenin first spoke of "coexistence" in a September 1919 letter "To the American Workers," referring to granting concessions to capitalists "for the whole course of this period in which socialist and capitalist states will exist together." He formally used the phrase "peaceful coexistence" on February 18, 1920, in response to an American correspondent: "In Asia? As in Europe, peaceful coexistence (mirnoe sozhitel'stvo) with peoples, with workers and peasants of all nations awakening to a new life." Notably, Lenin carefully identified peaceful coexistence not with relations between states, but between peoples -- and only those peoples who displayed an acceptable revolutionary consciousness.

By late 1920, Lenin acknowledged the situation had moved beyond a mere "breathing space": "We have not only a breathing space; we have a new period when our fundamental existence in a network of capitalist states has been won." At the Eighth Congress of Soviets in late 1920, he nevertheless warned: "I did not for a moment believe in lasting trade relations with the imperialist powers: what we shall obtain will be simply a breathing space." For Lenin, "Concessions are a new form of war" (December 1920 speech).

The first international legal embodiment of peaceful coexistence came in 1921 through treaties with Persia (Iran), Afghanistan, and Turkey, which explicitly recognized "the right of each people to choose its own political destiny freely and without obstacles."

A subtle but significant terminological shift occurred between 1923 and 1927, when the Russian term for peaceful coexistence changed from mirnoe sozhitel'stvo to mirnoe sosushchestvovanie, marking a hardening of Soviet attitude toward the West as communist military and economic strength increased.

THREE PILLARS OF SOVIET INTERNATIONAL LEGAL DOCTRINE:

G. I. Tunkin, the dominant Soviet international law theorist from the 1950s, systematized the doctrine into three pillars, all traceable to 1917-1924. Socialist Internationalism governed relations among socialist/proletarian states. Equality and Self-Determination of Nations was directed against colonialism and international oppression. Peaceful Coexistence governed relations between states with different social systems. Tunkin argued all three initiated a "radical restructuring of international law" and its transformation into a new, modern international law.

POST-1945 CONTRADICTION:

The 2024 Oxford volume Socialism and International Law documents how socialist states advanced a "moderate anticolonial vision of self-determination" in UN resolutions supporting independence for former colonies while "generally opposing sub-national, secessionist, and Indigenous movements" -- self-determination for colonies of enemies, not for peoples within the socialist sphere.
ANALYTICAL PATTERNS:
Korovin's "three systems of international law" was a pragmatic intellectual accommodation -- carving out a "transitory" system allowing the Soviet state to function within existing frameworks while maintaining theoretical rejection (Pattern 1). The claim that ALL sovereignty norms were bourgeois superstructure destined for abolition was the maximum ambition position in international legal theory, making the USSR a legal outlier that simultaneously participated in and denied the foundations of international law (Pattern 2). This is THE foundational section for instrumental norms: self-determination was proclaimed as universal right but applied selectively -- endorsed for colonies of capitalist powers, denied for nationalities within the socialist sphere. Pelliconi (2024) traces the "cherry-picking application of self-determination" directly to Soviet origins (Pattern 3). The coexistence vs. inevitable-war paradox embodied structural dualism at the level of legal theory: "How could representatives of the capitalist powers respect the integrity of Soviet policy when the latter stated simultaneously that peaceful coexistence was possible but that war was inevitable?" (Jacobson) (Pattern 4). Tunkin's later codification of three pillars (1950s) -- Socialist Internationalism, Equality and Self-Determination, Peaceful Coexistence -- demonstrates how Lenin's provisional contradictions became institutionalized Stalinist doctrine (Pattern 5).

## 5. PUBLICATION OF SECRET TREATIES

PRE-REVOLUTIONARY COMMITMENT:

As early as April 1917, Lenin and the Bolshevik Party made the publication and abrogation of the Tsarist secret treaties a central demand of their foreign policy platform. They denounced the Provisional Government for not publishing the secret treaties concluded by Nicholas II with Britain, France, and other powers. Lenin characterized these treaties as "outrageously predatory," granting Russian capitalists "a free hand to rob China, Persia, Turkey, and Austria." The continuation of the war was imperialist on all sides, waged for the division of capitalist spoils. The Provisional Government's failure to publish the treaties proved its commitment to the same predatory imperialist war as tsarism.

POST-REVOLUTIONARY ACTION:

On November 8, 1917, Lenin addressed the Petrograd Soviet, declaring: "All the secret treaties must be immediately published in order to strengthen the confidence of the proletariat."

On November 22, 1917, Trotsky, as People's Commissar for Foreign Affairs, formally announced the publication. His statement, carried in Izvestiia on November 23, declared: "In publishing the secret diplomatic documents from the foreign policy archives of Tsarism and of the bourgeois coalition Governments... we are carrying out the undertaking which we made when our party was in opposition." He continued: "Secret diplomacy is a necessary tool for a propertied minority which is compelled to deceive the majority in order to subject it to its interests. The abolition of secret diplomacy is the primary condition for an honest, popular, truly democratic foreign policy."

From November 23, 1917 onward, documents were published serially in Pravda and Izvestiia. Between December 1917 and February 1918, seven volumes were published under the title Collection of Secret Documents from the Archives of the Former Ministry of Foreign Affairs.

CONTENT EXPOSED:

The published documents revealed: the 1910 plan for the partition of Asiatic Turkey (excluding Anatolia) between Russia, Great Britain, and France; Russian consent to a buffer state on the left bank of the Rhine between France and Germany, in exchange for a free hand over Poland; and 1909 Russo-German treaty proposals that would have virtually nullified the 1907 Russo-British Agreement.

GLOBAL IMPACT:

The publication sent shockwaves through the international system. Woodrow Wilson's Fourteen Points (January 1918), particularly Point 1 calling for "open covenants of peace, openly arrived at," was directly influenced by the Bolshevik action. Trotsky and Wilson together helped transform the norm of diplomatic transparency -- while secret diplomacy did not disappear, the era of entirely secret treaties between states was dealt a lasting blow.

Trotsky anticipated that Germany and Austria-Hungary would attempt to use the documents to cast their own diplomacy in a favorable light. He declared such attempts "doomed to pitiful failure" because subsequent publications would expose the Central Powers' diplomacy, and when the German proletariat gained revolutionary access to their own chancellery secrets, they would find "documents no whit inferior to those which we are about to publish."

HISTORIOGRAPHY OF THE SECRET TREATY PUBLICATION:

John Quigley, "Leon Trotsky and the Prohibition Against Secret Treaties" (available via Semantic Scholar), examines the legal-diplomatic legacy of Trotsky's action as a foundational moment in the emergence of treaty transparency norms in international law.

openDemocracy (2010) contextualizes Trotsky's publication alongside Wilson's Fourteen Points and WikiLeaks as part of a recurring historical pattern of "diplomatic transparency" as a weapon wielded by insurgent powers against established elites.

The publication was not purely idealistic -- it was also a strategic move to undermine the Allied war effort by exposing imperialist war aims, to rally anti-war sentiment globally, and to justify the Bolshevik separate peace negotiations at Brest-Litovsk.
ANALYTICAL PATTERNS:
The publication served multiple pragmatic goals: undermining the Allied war effort, rallying global anti-war sentiment, and providing ideological cover for the separate peace at Brest-Litovsk (Pattern 1). Publishing seven volumes of secret documents was an unprecedented act of revolutionary transparency, a direct assault on the operational foundations of great-power diplomacy at the risk of permanently foreclosing normal relations with the Entente (Pattern 2). Publishing others' secret treaties while the USSR would later maintain its own covert military cooperation at Rapallo (1922) represents the paradigmatic case of instrumental norm deployment -- transparency for adversaries, opacity for the revolutionary state (Pattern 3). The publication was executed through the Narkomindel (state diplomacy) but framed in thoroughly revolutionary terms (exposing imperialist predation), illustrating how the two faces of Soviet diplomacy converged when revolutionary and state interests aligned (Pattern 4). Stalin's return to secret diplomacy -- most dramatically the 1939 Molotov-Ribbentrop secret protocol -- represented a complete reversal of the Bolshevik transparency principle, restoring tsarist-style secret agreements as instruments of state policy (Pattern 5).

## 6. POLISH-SOVIET WAR (1919-1921)

LENIN'S MOTIVATION:

Export revolution to Europe by force. Peter Whitewood's 2023 monograph The Soviet-Polish War and Its Legacy: Lenin's Defeat and the Rise of Stalinism documents Lenin's determination to smash through Poland and link up with German revolutionaries. The Bolsheviks perceived Poland not as an independent actor but as a "catspaw" or proxy for the great capitalist powers -- primarily Britain and France. The Polish state was viewed as a "barrier" between the "two huge sources of revolution in the East and West" (Stalin's words). The shortest route to Berlin and Paris led through Warsaw.

KEY EVIDENCE OF REVOLUTIONARY INTENT:

Evidence 1: Tukhachevski's order (July 2, 1920), issued to the Western Front: "Soldiers of the workers' revolution -- turn your eyes to the west. The fate of world revolution is being decided in the west. Over the corpse of White Poland lies the road to worldwide conflagration. On our bayonets we will carry happiness and peace to working humanity. To the west, to decisive battles, to great victories!"

Evidence 2: Lenin's telegram to Stalin (July 23, 1920): Urged the need to "immediately begin a revolution in Italy" and to "Sovietise Hungary, and perhaps Czechoslovakia and Romania as well." The Red Army's South-Western Front was aimed at crossing the Carpathian Mountains to ignite revolution in these regions.

Evidence 3: A puppet government-in-waiting, the Provisional Polish Revolutionary Committee (Polrewkom), headed by Cheka chief Felix Dzerzhinsky and Julian Marchlewski, was established to take power once Warsaw fell. Polish workers and peasants overwhelmingly rejected communist agitation and rallied to defend their nation.

Evidence 4: Trotsky described the Sovietization of Poland and Ukraine as "the links binding Soviet Russia with future Soviet Germany" -- the first stage in building a "Union of European Proletariat Republics."

THE DEFEAT: BATTLE OF WARSAW (AUGUST 1920):

The Red Army's rapid advance -- at times covering 20 miles a day -- brought them to the gates of Warsaw by mid-August 1920. Tukhachevski's plan was to cross the Vistula River north of the city and attack from the rear. The Red Army reached the village of Izabelin, just 8 miles (13 km) from Warsaw. Foreign diplomats (except British and Vatican ambassadors) fled the capital.

On August 16, 1920, Polish commander Jozef Pilsudski launched a daring counteroffensive from the south, exploiting a weak gap between the Soviet Western and South-Western Fronts (guarded by the small Mozyrska Group of only 8,000 men). The attack achieved total surprise. The Red Army's lines were broken, three Soviet armies disintegrated, and tens of thousands of Red Army soldiers fled into East Prussia (interned by Germany). Soviet losses: estimated 15,000-25,000 killed, 65,000 captured, and 35,000 interned in Germany.

LENIN'S ADMISSION OF DEFEAT:

"The Polish war was the most crucial turning point not only for the politics of Soviet Russia but also for the world politics... In Europe, it was possible to seize everything. But Pilsudski and his Poles inflicted a gigantic, unprecedented defeat to the cause of the world revolution."

CONSEQUENCES (per Whitewood, 2023):

First, a perpetual fear of war emerged. Lenin, Stalin, and other senior Bolsheviks became convinced they had been defeated not by Poland but by the forces of the capitalist world -- Britain and France -- directing the war behind the scenes. This "capitalist encirclement" paranoia became a permanent feature of the early Soviet state's worldview. The massive war scare of 1927, centered on Poland, was a direct prelude to this.

Second, reinforced authoritarianism. The perceived international threats became the "all-important backdrop" to growing authoritarianism. The logic of capitalist encirclement provided a powerful rationale for internal repression, demanded iron discipline, and justified the ruthless crushing of internal enemies.

Third, rationale for Stalinist industrialization. The belief in inevitable foreign war served as a central justification for Stalin's "revolution from above" -- the breakneck industrialization and enforced modernization of the Soviet Union at the end of the 1920s.

Fourth, long-term legacy. Lenin afterward stated: "We will conquer Poland anyway, when it is time. Against Poland, we can unite the entire Russian nation and even make an alliance with the Germans..." -- an ominous foreshadowing of the 1939 Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact and the joint Nazi-Soviet invasion of Poland.

Fifth, British diplomat Lord Edgar Vincent d'Abernon called it "The Eighteenth Decisive Battle of the World," arguing that Poland had saved Western civilization from Soviet tyranny.

HISTORIOGRAPHY OF THE POLISH-SOVIET WAR:

Norman Davies, White Eagle, Red Star: The Polish-Soviet War, 1919-20 and 'the Miracle on the Vistula' (1972; Pimlico reissue 2003): The foundational English-language monograph, drawing on both Polish and Soviet sources. Davies frames the war as "the first occasion when the Red Army set out to revolutionise the whole of Europe" and one that "largely determined the course of European history for the next twenty years or more." His companion article "The Missing Revolutionary War" (Soviet Studies, vol. 27, no. 2, 1975, pp. 178-195) develops the "export of revolution" thesis in greater analytical depth.

Thomas Fiddick, "The 'Miracle of the Vistula': Soviet Policy versus Red Army Strategy" (Journal of Modern History, 1973, vol. 45, no. 4, pp. 626-643): Revisionist argument that directly answers Churchill's baffled question of "Why?" Fiddick argues Davies's work, while the best overall, "fails to note the conflict between Party policy and army strategy." The Red Army's defeat owed less to Polish brilliance than to internal Soviet strategic contradictions -- specifically how General Wrangel's White forces in Crimea diverted Soviet attention and resources southward at the critical moment.

Piotr S. Wandycz, "General Weygand and the Battle of Warsaw of 1920" (Journal of Central European Affairs, 19, January 1960): Argues persuasively that Pilsudski, not his French advisers (particularly General Weygand), deserves credit for the victory -- countering a persistent Western tendency to attribute Polish success to French military expertise.

Winston Churchill titled a chapter in The Aftermath (1929) "The Miracle on the Vistula," describing the Red Army's retreat from Warsaw as "sudden, mysterious and decisive." Much subsequent historiography reacts to this bewilderment.

Key primary sources: Jozef Pilsudski, Year 1920 (Pilsudski Institute, 1972) -- includes Tukhachevsky's "The March Beyond the Vistula" as an appendix; and Leon Trotsky, "The War with Poland: Is a Second Lesson Needed?" (speech of September 8, 1920, available at marxists.org).

Key source: Peter Whitewood, The Soviet-Polish War and Its Legacy: Lenin's Defeat and the Rise of Stalinism (Bloomsbury, 2023).
ANALYTICAL PATTERNS:
The war represents Lenin's most catastrophic failure of pragmatism: where Brest-Litovsk demonstrated ideology yielding to survival, the Polish campaign demonstrated ideology overriding strategic caution with devastating results. The oscillation was never resolved (Pattern 1). This is the quintessential case of revolutionary risk-taking: Tukhachevski's order -- "over the corpse of White Poland lies the road to worldwide conflagration" -- and Lenin's simultaneous planning for revolutions across five European countries reveal the Bolshevik "operational code" of pursuing ambitious objectives even with uncertain probability (Pattern 2). A puppet government (Polrewkom) headed by Cheka chief Dzerzhinsky was pre-installed to rule once Warsaw fell -- "self-determination" meant Soviet control, not genuine national choice (Pattern 3). The war fused military force and revolutionary ideology: the Red Army served simultaneously as conventional military and "armed vanguard of world revolution," with Tukhachevski's orders using Comintern-style rhetoric within a state military campaign (Pattern 4). The Polish defeat became the foundational trauma of Soviet state psychology: the leadership "drew the mistaken conclusion" (Whitewood, 2023) of hidden capitalist orchestration, institutionalized under Stalin as permanent "capitalist encirclement" justifying terror and industrialization. Lenin's later vow -- "We will conquer Poland anyway... even make an alliance with the Germans" -- directly foreshadowed 1939 (Pattern 5).

## 7. SELF-DETERMINATION RHETORIC VS. PRACTICE UNDER LENIN

THE 1917 DECLARATION:

The Declaration of the Rights of the Peoples of Russia (November 2/15, 1917), signed by Vladimir Lenin (as Chairman of the Council of People's Commissars) and Joseph Stalin (as People's Commissar for Nationality Affairs), enshrined four core principles:

Principle 1: The equality and sovereignty of the peoples of Russia.
Principle 2: The right of the peoples of Russia to free self-determination, even to the point of separation and the formation of an independent state.
Principle 3: The abolition of any and all national and national-religious privileges and disabilities.
Principle 4: The free development of national minorities and ethnographic groups inhabiting the territory of Russia.

INSTITUTIONAL ARCHITECTURE (1917-1924):

Narkomnats (People's Commissariat of Nationality Affairs), headed by Stalin, was created to serve as an intermediary between central Soviet organs and minority peoples. Between 1917 and 1923, approximately 17 autonomous republics and regions were established within the Russian Federation, plus five independent republics outside it. The 1924 Constitution codified the USSR as a federation of four republics (Russian SFSR, Ukraine, Belorussia, Transcaucasian Federation), with Article 4 granting each union republic the right of secession -- a right with no implementing mechanism until 1990.

ACTUAL PRACTICE REVEALED SYSTEMATIC CONTRADICTIONS:

Contradiction 1: Conditional application. Self-determination applied to the "toiling masses," not the bourgeoisie. Non-socialist independence movements were suppressed. The Bolsheviks supported Finnish and Polish independence (since those regions were already militarily lost) but fought to retain Ukraine, the Caucasus, and Central Asia.

Contradiction 2: Centralized party control. A Georgian Bolshevik noted in 1923: "Everyone knows what kind of independence this is. We have one party, one Central Committee, which decides everything unconditionally for every republic."

Contradiction 3: Korenizatsiia (nativization/indigenization) -- the 1923 resolutions formalized a policy of promoting national territories, languages, elites, and cultures. The USSR became what historian Terry Martin called the "world's first affirmative action empire" -- systematically promoting national consciousness among ethnic minorities. By the late 1920s, 66 of 191 official nationalities had books in their native languages, and 205 non-Russian newspapers circulated in 47 languages. Yet this was done within the framework of a strictly centralized Party.

Contradiction 4: Ethnic engineering. The 1924 Central Asian delimitation created distinct "national" territories (Uzbek, Turkmen, Tajik, Kyrgyz) where none had existed -- arguably "constructing" nations in a divide-and-rule strategy against pan-Turkic or pan-Islamic unity.

Contradiction 5: The secession time bomb. The USSR Constitution's secession clause, intended to reassure minorities and prevent separation, ultimately provided the legal basis for the Soviet Union's dissolution in 1991 -- an outcome Lenin never anticipated.

THE SCHOLARLY DEBATE: PIPES VS. SUNY/MARTIN

Richard Pipes (The Formation of the Soviet Union, 1954, and The Russian Revolution, 1990): The instrumentalist critique. Argues Lenin's self-determination slogan was primarily a tactical weapon to exploit minority grievances for political advantage during the Civil War, then discarded once Bolshevik control was secured. Self-determination was a "weapon" deployed against Tsarist and White opponents but denied when it threatened Bolshevik control. Pipes traced Lenin's nationalities theory through three phases: 1897-1913 (basic views), 1913-1917 (utilizing minority movements), and 1917-1923 (adapting theory to practical governance).

Ronald Grigor Suny and Terry Martin, A State of Nations: Empire and Nation-Making in the Age of Lenin and Stalin (Oxford University Press, 2001): The revisionist counterpoint. Suny argues the USSR was neither a simple continuation of the Tsarist empire nor a genuine federation, but a novel hybrid -- "an empire of nations" that simultaneously repressed nationalism and fostered national identities. Martin's "affirmative action empire" thesis (developed fully in The Affirmative Action Empire, 2001) argues the USSR was the first state in history to systematically promote national consciousness among ethnic minorities as a matter of policy. This was not purely cynical divide-and-rule but a genuine attempt to defuse nationalism by meeting it halfway -- paradoxically strengthening the very national identities that would eventually help dissolve the union. The volume's chapters, covering Ukraine, Central Asia, the Caucasus, and other regions, collectively document how the Soviet ethnofederal structure both constrained and enabled national mobilization.

THE GEORGIAN AFFAIR (1922) -- A DEFINING CASE:

Stalin proposed that Georgia enter the USSR through a Transcaucasian Federation (diminishing its status from direct membership to subordinate membership within a federation). Georgian Bolshevik leaders (Mdivani and Makharadze) resisted. The conflict escalated when Sergo Ordzhonikidze physically struck a Georgian opponent during a dispute. Lenin, initially siding with Stalin, reversed course after investigating.

In his last major political intervention before incapacitation (December 30-31, 1922), Lenin dictated "The Question of Nationalities or 'Autonomisation'" from his sickbed: "That Great Russian chauvinism played a role in this affair is apparent from the physical violence used by Ordzhonikidze... It appears that this whole 'autonomisation' idea was fundamentally mistaken and ill-timed. It was a truly Great Russian nationalist campaign." Lenin demanded Stalin and Dzerzhinsky bear "political responsibility" and warned that the "internationalist" oppressor-nation must practice "inequality in the opposite direction" to compensate for de facto inequality: "The internationalism of the oppressor or so-called 'great' nation... must consist not only in observance of the formal equality of nations but also in such inequality as would compensate for the inequality which obtains in actual practice."

On January 4, 1923, Lenin added: "Stalin is too rude, and this defect, although quite tolerable in our midst and in dealings among us Communists, becomes intolerable in a Secretary-General. That is why I suggest that the comrades think about a way of removing Stalin from that post."

However, Lenin's third stroke (March 10, 1923) incapacitated him permanently. At the 12th Party Congress (April 1923), Stalin's line triumphed decisively, Georgian "national deviationists" were purged from their posts, and most of the opposition leaders were later executed in the Great Purge of the 1930s.
ANALYTICAL PATTERNS:
Granting independence to Finland and Poland (already militarily lost) while reconquering Ukraine, the Caucasus, and Central Asia reveals the pragmatic limits of ideological self-determination (Pattern 1). The 1917 Declaration was a maximum ambition document -- granting secession rights "even to the point of separation and the formation of an independent state" went further than any existing international instrument, a gamble that the promise of self-determination would win minority support during the Civil War (Pattern 2). This is THE central case study for instrumental norm deployment: self-determination proclaimed as universal right but applied as conditional weapon -- for Finland/Poland, not Ukraine/Georgia, for capitalist colonies, not the socialist sphere. The Suny/Martin "empire of nations" thesis captures the paradox of simultaneously promoting and repressing nationalism (Pattern 3). Narkomnats handled the administrative architecture of nationalities within the Soviet state (autonomous republics, korenizatsiia), while the Comintern handled global anticolonialism -- parallel tracks for the national question, bureaucratic accommodation at home and revolutionary agitation abroad (Pattern 4). The 1922 Georgian Affair is the pivotal Lenin-Stalin rupture moment: Lenin's final political intervention condemned the "Great Russian nationalist campaign" and recommended Stalin's removal, but Stalin's victory at the 12th Party Congress (April 1923) set the template of formal ethno-federalism masking centralized party dictatorship (Pattern 5).

## 8. "SOCIALISM IN ONE COUNTRY" VS. WORLD REVOLUTION

LENIN'S OWN EVOLUTION (per Erik van Ree, "Socialism in One Country: A Reassessment," Studies in East European Thought, 1998, Vol. 50, No. 2, pp. 77-117):

Phase 1 (Pre-1917): Lenin and Trotsky both believed an isolated revolutionary Russia had no chance of survival. Lenin's Imperialism, the Highest Stage of Capitalism (1916) argued the socialist revolution would come as "an epoch of proletarian civil war against the bourgeoisie in the advanced countries combined with a whole series of democratic and revolutionary movements, including movements for national liberation, in the undeveloped, backward and oppressed nations."

Phase 2 (1917-1921): Lenin underwent a significant reversal. He first concluded that socialism could be built in isolated Russia, though it would remain "incomplete" without world revolution. He wrote in November 1920: "We have not only a breathing space; we have a new period when our fundamental existence in a network of capitalist states has been won."

Phase 3 (By 1923): Lenin's final writings (On Cooperation, Notes on Sukhanov) suggested the USSR possessed everything "necessary and sufficient" for building a complete socialist society, moving toward abandoning even the "incomplete" qualification. Van Ree's central thesis: Stalin and Bukharin's position in the 1925-26 debate was arguably more "orthodox-Leninist" than Trotsky's, who had initially accepted the notion of "incomplete socialism" but then reverted to the older pre-1917 position that capitalist restoration was inevitable without world revolution.

VAN REE'S KEY DISTINCTION:

Van Ree argues that traditional historiography conflates two very different claims. The "Theory of Restoration" (Trotsky's later view) holds that socialism in one country is impossible and doomed to collapse. The "Theory of Incomplete Socialism" (Lenin's later view) holds that socialism is possible but would remain imperfect/unfinished until international revolution secures it. Van Ree's reassessment is built on rigorously separating these two perspectives, concluding that Stalin's position was the logical continuation of late Leninism, not a betrayal of it.

VAN REE'S DEEPER GENEALOGY:

In "Socialism in One Country Before Stalin: German Origins," van Ree excavates a pre-Bolshevik German social-democratic discourse (Lassalle, Vollmar, Calwer, Balodis) that had already explored nationalist and autarkic versions of socialist construction -- anticipating Stalin's formula. This suggests "Socialism in One Country" was not a purely Stalinist invention but had roots in the broader Second International's struggles with nationalism and economic development.

THE CLASSICAL HISTORIOGRAPHY: CARR AND DEUTSCHER

Isaac Deutscher (The Prophet Armed, 1954; The Prophet Unarmed, 1959; The Prophet Outcast, 1963): Framed the debate as a clash between internationalist orthodoxy (Trotsky as defender of pure Marxist internationalism) and nationalist heresy (Stalin/Bukharin as pragmatic betrayers of revolutionary principles). Deutscher summarized Trotsky's view: "Russia's industrial poverty and backwardness would... prove formidable obstacles to the building of a Socialist economy; and only with the help of the Socialist West could these obstacles be broken and removed."

E.H. Carr (The Bolshevik Revolution, 1950; Socialism in One Country, 1958-1964): Documented the shift as a response to the failure of European revolution and the need for state consolidation. Carr portrayed Trotsky as an idealist whose theory failed the historical test, while Stalin was a realist who adapted Bolshevik ideology to the post-revolutionary geopolitical reality of enduring isolation.

THE POST-LENIN DEBATE (1924-1926):

- Internal contradictions: Stalin/Bukharin held they were soluble through the worker-peasant alliance and state planning. Trotsky held they were insoluble without international economic integration.
- Productive forces: Stalin/Bukharin held backwardness was surmountable. Trotsky held modern technology requires international division of labor and that a backward country alone faced insurmountable contradictions.
- External threat: Stalin/Bukharin argued inter-imperialist contradictions prevent united intervention. Trotsky argued imperialist powers would inevitably unite to crush the USSR.
- Ideological framing: Stalin/Bukharin claimed Lenin's On Cooperation proved the USSR possessed everything "necessary and sufficient." Trotsky called Stalin's theory a "reactionary utopia" expressing the conservative, self-interested mood of the emerging bureaucracy.

Stalin's position was formally ratified at the Fourteenth Party Conference (1925), explicitly recognizing the possibility of building "a complete Socialist society" in the USSR without "state aid" from more developed countries. Stalin used the theory to isolate Trotsky, Zinoviev, and Kamenev politically, painting them as "anti-Leninist" pessimists and "lackers of faith" in the Soviet working class.

Trotsky, in The Third International After Lenin (1928), countered that socialism "cannot be achieved in national isolation" and that national self-sufficiency would discredit the USSR as a revolutionary base for the international proletariat.

THE COEXISTENCE PARADOX:

A persistent tension existed in Soviet foreign policy, as formulated by Jon Jacobson: "How could representatives of the capitalist powers respect the integrity of Soviet policy... when the latter stated simultaneously that peaceful coexistence was possible but that war was inevitable?" For Lenin, peaceful coexistence was never a moral end or a permanent policy -- it was an instrumental, temporary tactic (peredyshka). Its transformation into a long-term strategic doctrine came only under Khrushchev.

LENIN'S REVOLUTIONARY DEFEATISM: THE HAL DRAPER CRITIQUE

Hal Draper, in "The Myth of Lenin's 'Revolutionary Defeatism'" (1953-54, published as a book in 1996), fundamentally challenges the canonical understanding. Draper argues that "revolutionary defeatism" was largely a myth constructed posthumously, and that Lenin's formulations on defeat were inconsistent, shifting, and deeply contradictory. Draper identifies at least four different formulations Lenin cycled through: the "lesser evil" (defeat of tsarism is a lesser evil since "tsarism is a hundred times worse than kaiserism"), "defeat facilitates revolution," "wish defeat in every country," and eventually "don't halt before the risk of defeat." By 1917, after the February Revolution, Lenin effectively abandoned the defeat-slogan, declaring "we were not defeatists."

Draper contrasts Lenin with Trotsky and Rosa Luxemburg, who held the same fundamental analysis of imperialism and opposition to both war camps but refused the "slogan of defeat," counterposing socialist revolution to any military outcome, without wishing for their own government's military defeat. Draper argues their "Third Camp" position was more theoretically coherent and politically sound.
ANALYTICAL PATTERNS:
Lenin's late writings (On Cooperation, 1923) show him moving toward pragmatic acceptance that the USSR possessed everything "necessary and sufficient" for building socialism, abandoning the qualification that it would remain "incomplete" without world revolution (Pattern 1). Trotsky's permanent revolution was the pure maximum ambition position -- insisting that socialism required international division of labor and that capitalist restoration was inevitable without world revolution, a position Lenin had already left behind (Pattern 2). "Socialism in one country" instrumentalized Marxism itself -- subordinating international proletarian solidarity to national state-building, transforming a temporary tactical retreat into a permanent doctrine of national self-sufficiency (Pattern 3). The debate resolved the Comintern/NKID tension decisively in favor of state interest: the Comintern was formally and permanently subordinated to Soviet national security, a subordination crystallized at the Third Comintern Congress (1921) and never reversed (Pattern 4). This IS the continuity/rupture debate in its purest form. Van Ree's reassessment (1998) shows Stalin's position was arguably more "orthodox-Leninist" than Trotsky's, since Lenin himself had evolved toward accepting the possibility of isolated socialist construction. Yet Lenin's NEP was a temporary holding action contingent on world revolution; Stalin transformed it into a permanent strategic doctrine irrespective of global conditions -- continuity of concept, rupture of meaning (Pattern 5).

## 9. TREATY OF RAPALLO (1922) -- BONUS SECTION

OVERVIEW:

The Treaty of Rapallo was signed on April 16, 1922, between Soviet Russia and Weimar Germany -- both "pariah states" of post-Versailles Europe. It was negotiated by German Foreign Minister Walther Rathenau and Soviet Foreign Minister Georgy Chicherin during the Genoa Economic and Financial Conference. The public treaty text addressed mutual renunciation of war claims, cancellation of debts, resumption of diplomatic relations, and most-favored-nation trade status. The public treaty itself contained no military provisions. The military dimension was entirely covert and denied by both governments for over a decade.

THE "PARIAH STATES" THESIS:

As Graebner and Bennett (The Versailles Treaty and Its Legacy, Cambridge University Press, 2011) state: "Germany and the Soviet Union entered the post-Versailles world as pariah states. Both had been denied roles in the Paris decisions of 1919; both had been ostracized thereafter from the main currents of European economic and diplomatic affairs."

The H-Net review of Vourkoutiotis describes the two nations as "the two pariah states of the postwar period... suspected of having formed an 'axis of evil'." Mangold-Will (University of Wuppertal, 2022) describes them as "two states internationally ostracized by the Western victorious powers." This shared pariah status -- both excluded from Versailles, both treated as international outcasts -- was the fundamental driver of the rapprochement.

RAPALLO AS VERSAILLES REVISIONISM:

For Morgan (The Historical Journal, Cambridge), Rapallo "cemented a German-Soviet partnership" offering multiple forms of advantage -- economic reconstruction, military evasion of Versailles, and political hopes ranging from neutrality pacts to "the grandiose ambition of jointly attacking and dismembering the fragile and presumptuous state of Poland."

For Mangold-Will (2022): "For Germany, the aim was to revise the Treaty of Versailles; for Russia, it was to obtain financial resources and technical support to rebuild or expand its military capacity. The common denominator was a perspective revision of Germany's eastern and Russia's western borders."

However, scholars are careful not to overstate the case. Von Scheliha (H-German review, 2007) emphasizes that unlike the 1939 Hitler-Stalin Pact, "no agreement was reached whatsoever upon a joint military action against Poland like that concluded in the secret protocol of 1939." The revisionism was strategic and long-term, not immediately offensive.

KEY SCHOLARLY WORKS AND DEBATES:

Vasilis Vourkoutiotis, Making Common Cause: German-Soviet Secret Relations, 1919-1922 (2007): Uses newly accessible Russian archives (RGASPI, RGVA) to trace secret contacts back before 1920; argues Rapallo was "diplomatic camouflage" for military cooperation that pre-dated the treaty itself. The monograph treats cooperation as extending from "joint lobbying against the Versailles treaty" to "mutual assistance in modernizing their armed forces and breaking the international isolation."

Sergei Gorlov: Demonstrates that the Soviet Politburo reviewed and approved virtually every detail of the military cooperation. The entire Soviet leadership -- not just Chicherin -- backed military cooperation with Germany as "one of the most important strategic courses of Soviet foreign policy at the time."

Eva Ingeborg Fleischhauer: Argues Rapallo had an inherently "offensive purpose" deployable at any time -- an interpretation contested by J. David Cameron, who counters that German policy aimed to "evolutionize" Bolshevik rule toward moderation through economic interdependence, not to forge a permanent anti-Western alliance.

Salzmann (Great Britain, Germany and the Soviet Union): Examines Rapallo's aftermath through 1934 and argues the "Rapallo myth" exceeded its real historical impact -- the treaty never caused lasting damage to European stability, and Weimar Germany never abandoned efforts to reconcile with the West (leading to the Locarno Treaties of 1925).

Bill Bowring (2017, Journal of the History of International Law, via Birkbeck): Focuses on Soviet legal theorist Yevgeniy Pashukanis's role in negotiating Rapallo and examines the secret military agreement through the lens of international law. Refers to both nations as "two defeated and to different extents pariah powers."

LENIN'S ROLE:

While Chicherin handled daily negotiations, Lenin's involvement was decisive. As early as August 1921, Chicherin informed Lenin that "the most influential persons up to the Chancellor [of Germany] were won to the project" of military cooperation. Lenin personally pushed for exploitation of "inter-imperialist contradictions" -- using Germany's resentment over Versailles to fracture the Western alliance. The Soviet leadership viewed military cooperation as one of the most important strategic courses of Soviet foreign policy, not merely a technical loophole for Germany.

SECRET MILITARY COOPERATION: THE THREE FACILITIES

Germany established covert military installations inside the USSR, circumventing the Versailles restrictions that limited the Reichswehr to 100,000 soldiers and banned tanks, military aircraft, submarines, and chemical weapons. General Hans von Seeckt, Chief of the German Army Command (Reichswehr), was the principal architect. He established Sondergruppe R (Special Group Russia), led by Oskar von Niedermayer, to manage the covert relationship. The German civilian Foreign Office was not fully informed initially -- Seeckt and the Reichswehr operated with considerable autonomy, a "Prussian tradition" the Foreign Office only gradually asserted control over.

Facility 1: Lipetsk Aviation School. Trained German pilots, tested military aircraft and aerial warfare doctrine. A Junkers aircraft factory was constructed alongside the flight school. This effectively built a German air force -- expressly forbidden by Versailles -- with both German and Russian pilots trained for military use.

Facility 2: Kama Tank School (near Kazan). Tested and trained with tanks and armored warfare doctrine (disguised as "light tractors"), giving the Reichswehr critical experience in mobile armored warfare later informing Blitzkrieg tactics.

Facility 3: Tomka Chemical Weapons Site (Volsk, Saratov region). Tested and developed poison gas and chemical warfare techniques, violating Versailles prohibitions on German chemical weapons development.

The cooperation lasted approximately ten years (mid-1920s to 1933), ending with Hitler's rise to power and his repudiation of the Soviet relationship. During this decade, the German military gained critical experience in mobile warfare, aviation, and armor doctrine. The Soviet Red Army gained access to German technical expertise, which contributed to its own modernization under the Five-Year Plans.

GERMAN PIVOT BACK WESTWARD: LOCARNO (1925)

Stresemann reportedly wrote that "to celebrate a marriage with communist Russia would mean to go to bed with the killer of one's own fatherland," suggesting Rapallo was a tactical maneuver, not a permanent orientation. The Locarno Treaties (1925) marked Germany's pivot back toward the Western powers, though secret military cooperation with the USSR continued regardless until 1933.
ANALYTICAL PATTERNS:
Secret military cooperation with a "bourgeois" German state authorized personally by Lenin represents pure pragmatism -- accepting the form of secret diplomacy the Bolsheviks had publicly condemned, to break isolation, acquire technology, and exploit "inter-imperialist contradictions" (Pattern 1). Rapallo was a revolutionary gamble betting on German revisionism against the Versailles order, wagering that shared pariah status would overcome ideological division more powerfully than ideology would drive the two states apart (Pattern 2). While the USSR publicly denounced secret treaties and had published the Tsarist archives, it maintained three covert military facilities (Lipetsk, Kama, Tomka) in flagrant violation of both Versailles and its own proclaimed principles -- instrumental norm deployment at its most brazen (Pattern 3). The NKID (Chicherin) handled the public treaty while the Reichswehr (Seeckt's Sondergruppe R) handled covert military cooperation -- dual tracks institutionally separated, with the German civilian Foreign Office not fully informed, replicating the NKID/Comintern split in the military sphere (Pattern 4). Rapallo's secret military cooperation directly prefigured the 1939 Molotov-Ribbentrop secret protocol. The pattern -- secret bilateral agreements with a revisionist power to undermine the Versailles order -- became the template. A crucial difference: Rapallo involved no concrete agreement on joint military action against Poland, unlike 1939 (Pattern 5).

## 10. CONCLUDING REFLECTION: THE FIVE PATTERNS ACROSS NINE TOPICS

The five cross-cutting patterns -- pragmatism versus ideology, revolutionary ambition and risk-taking, instrumental use of norms, structural dualism, and continuity/rupture with Stalinism -- do not operate as separate lenses but as interconnected dimensions of a single phenomenon: a revolutionary state's attempt to survive within, oppose, and ultimately transform an international order it rejected as illegitimate. Across all nine topics examined in this report, the patterns recur with remarkable consistency.

On pragmatism versus ideology: At Brest-Litovsk, Lenin surrendered a third of Russia's territory for a breathing spell. Under NEP, he granted sweeping economic concessions while tightening political control. At Rapallo, he authorized secret military cooperation with a bourgeois German state. In Asia, he traded revolutionary support for diplomatic normalization. Yet at Warsaw in 1920, pragmatism failed catastrophically, and ideological ambition overrode strategic caution. The pattern was not uniform but oscillating -- pragmatism when survival was at stake, maximalism when opportunity seemed within reach.

On revolutionary ambition and risk-taking: The Decree on Peace (1917) proclaimed the abolition of secret diplomacy from a position of military collapse. The Twenty-One Conditions (1920) attempted to construct a global centralized revolutionary apparatus. The Polish gamble (1920) aimed to export revolution to Germany, Italy, Hungary, Czechoslovakia, and Romania simultaneously. The Comintern dispatched professional revolutionaries from Berlin to Shanghai to Spain. Throughout, the Bolshevik "operational code" -- pursuing ambitious objectives even when probability of success was uncertain -- drove foreign policy toward audacious gambles that sometimes succeeded (Brest-Litovsk annulment, Rapallo) and sometimes failed catastrophically (Warsaw).

On instrumental use of norms: Self-determination was proclaimed as a universal right and then applied selectively -- granted to Finland and Poland (already militarily lost), denied to Ukraine and Georgia (strategically essential), endorsed for colonies of capitalist rivals (to weaken them), and suppressed within the socialist sphere. Secret treaties were published to expose Entente imperialism while the USSR maintained its own covert military cooperation at Rapallo. International law was rejected as bourgeois superstructure in theory and deployed as an anti-imperialist weapon in practice. The post-1945 pattern -- supporting anticolonial self-determination at the UN while opposing sub-national secession within the socialist bloc -- was not a later corruption but the operationalization of Lenin's original conditional framework.

On structural dualism: The NKID/Comintern split was the "most fundamental contradiction" (Jacobson) in early Soviet foreign relations. It was born in December 1917 with the simultaneous creation of the NKID and the two-million-ruble appropriation for world revolution. It persisted for 26 years through Chicherin's complaints, Lenin's procedural bans, Stalin's "polycratic" control system, and the Litvinov collective security pivot. It was resolved only by Stalin's liquidation of the Comintern in 1943 and Zhdanov's "two-camp" binary in 1947 -- after which the dualism that had defined Lenin's foreign policy was definitively eradicated in favor of monolithic state control.

On continuity and rupture with Stalinism: This is the most contested pattern in the historiography, and the evidence supports both interpretations. Continuity: the peredyshka concept became "socialism in one country"; the Rapallo secret cooperation became the Molotov-Ribbentrop secret protocol; the capitalist encirclement trauma from the Polish defeat became Stalin's permanent ideological justification for terror and industrialization; the Narkomnats administrative apparatus became the institutional framework for Stalin's nationalities policy. Rupture: Lenin's "Last Testament" explicitly sought Stalin's removal as General Secretary over the Georgian Affair; Lenin's NEP was a temporary holding action contingent on world revolution, while Stalin's "socialism in one country" was a permanent doctrine of national self-sufficiency; Lenin held revolutionary internationalism and pragmatic state diplomacy in creative tension, while Stalin collapsed that tension into total bureaucratic centralization; Lenin's authority rested on argument, Stalin's on coercion. The scholarly consensus (Cohen, Tucker, Smith, Le Blanc) is that despite the continuities, the period 1929-1933 marked a qualitative rupture -- the "birth period of Stalinism" -- that constituted "a radical departure from Bolshevik programmatic thinking."

The nine topics examined in this report, when viewed through the five patterns, reveal a Lenin whose foreign policy was neither the coherent strategic vision of Soviet hagiography nor the purely cynical manipulation of Cold War caricature. It was a set of provisional improvisations, held together by Lenin's personal authority and the shared Bolshevik expectation of imminent world revolution. When Lenin died in 1924 and world revolution definitively failed, the contradictions he had managed through tactical skill became the unresolved structural tensions that Stalin would resolve through violence and institutional liquidation. The Leninist template -- revolutionary state as revisionist actor, norms as instruments, dual-track institutionalization, pragmatic oscillation between retreat and advance -- survived the specific historical circumstances of 1917-1924 to become a permanent feature of the international system, adopted, adapted, and contested by revolutionary regimes from Asia to Latin America for generations to come.
