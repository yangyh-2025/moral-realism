# Joseph Stalin: Ideology, Realpolitik, and Paranoia in Soviet Foreign Policy (1924-1953)

## Overview

Joseph Stalin (1878-1953) presents the most challenging case for the ABM model's leader typology. He does not fit neatly into any single category: he was simultaneously an ideological Marxist-Leninist who genuinely believed in world revolution, a ruthless realpolitik practitioner who sacrificed communist parties abroad whenever Soviet state interests demanded, and a paranoid autocrat whose domestic terror apparatus systematically distorted his foreign policy information. His foreign policy covers the entire ABM simulation period: collective security against fascism (1934-1939), the Nazi-Soviet Pact (1939-1941), the Grand Alliance (1941-1945), and the early Cold War (1945-1953). Understanding Stalin requires understanding how ideology, state interest, and individual psychology interacted in a single decision-maker with essentially unlimited domestic power.

---

## 1. Stalin's Strategic Worldview

### "Socialism in One Country" (1924)

Stalin's first major doctrinal innovation was his break with Trotsky's theory of "Permanent Revolution." Trotsky argued that the Russian Revolution could only survive if revolution spread to advanced capitalist countries (especially Germany). Stalin argued that the USSR could and must build socialism within its own borders, even while surrounded by hostile capitalist states.

This had profound foreign policy implications:

- **The Comintern became subordinate to Soviet state interests.** Under Lenin, the Comintern (Communist International, founded 1919) was genuinely intended to coordinate world revolution. Under Stalin, it became an instrument of Soviet foreign policy. Communist parties abroad were ordered to support Soviet diplomatic positions, even when those positions contradicted their domestic political interests.
- **The USSR was a "besieged fortress."** Stalin's domestic propaganda emphasized capitalist encirclement and the constant threat of invasion. This was not purely paranoia -- the Western intervention in the Russian Civil War (1918-1920) had been real, and diplomatic isolation persisted through the 1920s.
- **Soviet security took precedence over revolutionary purity.** Stalin was willing to ally with capitalist powers (France 1935, the Western Allies 1941-1945), sign non-aggression pacts with fascist states (Germany 1939, Japan 1941), and suppress foreign communist revolutions (Greece 1944-49, China 1945-49, when Stalin advised Mao to accept a coalition government).

### The View of Capitalist Encirclement

Stalin genuinely believed that capitalist states would eventually attack the USSR. His 1927 analysis was typical:

> "The period of 'peaceful coexistence' is receding into the past, giving place to a period of imperialist attacks and preparation for intervention against the USSR."

This belief had real consequences: the forced collectivization of agriculture (1928-33) and the Five Year Plans for rapid industrialization were justified domestically as preparation for inevitable war. The human cost of collectivization (millions dead in the resulting famine, especially in Ukraine) was accepted as the price of survival against capitalist encirclement.

## 2. Collective Security Phase (1934-1939)

### The Turn to Collective Security

By 1933-34, Stalin recognized that Nazi Germany represented a qualitatively new threat. Hitler had openly declared his intention to destroy "Jewish Bolshevism" and conquer *Lebensraum* in the East. The Soviet response was a dramatic shift in foreign policy:

**Soviet Entry into the League of Nations (September 1934)**: The USSR joined the League, which it had previously denounced as a "league of imperialist bandits." Foreign Minister Maxim Litvinov became the leading advocate for collective security against fascist aggression.

**The Popular Front Strategy (1935)**: The Comintern's 7th Congress (July-August 1935) ordered communist parties worldwide to form alliances with socialist, liberal, and even conservative anti-fascist forces. Communist parties in France and Spain entered Popular Front governments.

**Franco-Soviet Mutual Assistance Pact (May 1935)**: A defensive alliance with France. The pact was weakened by lack of military coordination provisions and French reluctance (the French right preferred Hitler to Stalin).

**Soviet Support for Republican Spain (1936-1939)**: The USSR sent military advisors, aircraft, and tanks to the Spanish Republic. But Stalin also used the NKVD to suppress Trotskyists and anarchists within the Republican camp, and Soviet support was always limited by fear of provoking a general war.

### Why Collective Security Failed

From Stalin's perspective, the Western powers were not serious about stopping Hitler:

- **The Rhineland remilitarization (March 1936)**: No Western response
- **The Anschluss (March 1938)**: No Western response
- **Munich (September 1938)**: Britain and France forced Czechoslovakia to cede the Sudetenland to Hitler. **The USSR was excluded from the Munich Conference entirely** despite having a mutual defense treaty with Czechoslovakia.

Stalin concluded that the Western powers preferred Hitler to the USSR and might even be hoping to direct German expansion eastward. Litvinov, the face of collective security and a Jew, was replaced by Vyacheslav Molotov as Foreign Minister on 3 May 1939 -- a signal to Berlin that Moscow was reconsidering its options.

## 3. The Nazi-Soviet Pact (1939-1941)

### The Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact (23 August 1939)

The most dramatic reversal in modern diplomatic history. After years of denouncing fascism, Stalin signed a non-aggression pact with Hitler. The **secret protocol** divided Eastern Europe:

- Eastern Poland, Bessarabia (Romania), Finland, Estonia, Latvia: Soviet "sphere of influence"
- Western Poland, Lithuania: German "sphere of influence"

One week later (1 September 1939), Germany invaded Poland. On 17 September, the Red Army invaded from the east. Poland was partitioned for the fourth time in its history.

### Why Did Stalin Choose Hitler?

Five reasons, in rough order of importance:

1. **Anglo-French-Soviet negotiations had failed.** The tripartite military talks (June-August 1939) stalled on Poland's refusal to allow Red Army transit. British and French negotiators were low-level and seemed unserious.

2. **Munich had convinced Stalin that the West could not be trusted.** If Britain and France would sacrifice Czechoslovakia, they would sacrifice the USSR.

3. **Territorial gains.** The pact gave Stalin eastern Poland, the Baltic states, and Bessarabia -- a buffer zone that pushed the Soviet border hundreds of kilometers westward.

4. **Time.** Stalin believed the pact would buy 2-3 years for Soviet rearmament while the capitalist powers exhausted themselves fighting each other.

5. **Ideological flexibility.** For Stalin, the "socialist content" of foreign policy was always secondary to Soviet state power. An alliance with a fascist state was ideologically distasteful but operationally acceptable if it served Soviet interests.

### The Economic Relationship

The Nazi-Soviet economic agreements (1939-1941) made the USSR Germany's largest raw materials supplier. Soviet shipments of oil, grain, manganese, and other materials were crucial to the German war economy during the period when Germany was blockaded by Britain. Stalin reportedly personally supervised the punctuality of deliveries, believing that strict compliance would deter Hitler from attacking.

### The Intelligence Failure (1941)

Stalin received at least 84 separate intelligence warnings of the coming German invasion between January and June 1941. Churchill personally warned him in April 1941. Richard Sorge, the Soviet master spy in Tokyo, provided the exact invasion date in late May. Stalin dismissed all of them as British provocations designed to drag the USSR into war.

When the invasion came on 22 June 1941, Stalin suffered a nervous collapse. He retreated to his dacha for several days, reportedly telling colleagues: "Lenin left us a great inheritance and we have shit all over it." When Molotov and other Politburo members came to ask him to return, Stalin reportedly asked: "Have you come to arrest me?"

The failure was not primarily an intelligence failure but a psychological one: Stalin could not believe that Hitler would attack while Britain remained undefeated, because Stalin himself would never have made such a strategically irrational decision.

## 4. The Grand Alliance and Its Dissolution (1941-1947)

### Stalin as War Leader

Stalin recovered from his initial collapse and became an effective -- if brutal -- war leader:

- **Order No. 227 (28 July 1942)**: "Not a step back!" Deserters and "panic-mongers" were to be shot. Penal battalions fought the most dangerous sectors.
- **The patriotic turn**: Stalin appealed to Russian nationalism, not communism. The Russian Orthodox Church was rehabilitated. Officers' ranks and epaulettes were restored (they had been abolished as "tsarist" after 1917). The war became the "Great Patriotic War," not a war for world revolution.
- **Centralization**: Like Churchill and Roosevelt, Stalin centralized decision-making in a small war cabinet (the State Defense Committee, GKO). Unlike them, he had no legislature or public opinion to manage.

### The Wartime Conferences

| Conference | Date | Stalin's Achievements |
|-----------|------|----------------------|
| **Tehran** | Nov-Dec 1943 | Agreement on the cross-Channel invasion (Overlord) for 1944; Stalin agreed to enter the war against Japan after Germany's defeat; general agreement on moving Poland's borders westward |
| **Yalta** | Feb 1945 | Declaration on Liberated Europe (free elections -- ambiguous); agreement on UN voting procedures; secret agreement on Soviet entry against Japan (South Sakhalin, Kuril Islands, Port Arthur rights); Poland's western border on the Oder-Neisse line |
| **Potsdam** | Jul-Aug 1945 | Germany divided into four occupation zones; the Oder-Neisse line as Poland's provisional western border; German disarmament, denazification, and democratization |

### The Percentages Agreement (October 1944)

At a private meeting in Moscow, Churchill proposed to Stalin a division of influence in the Balkans, written on a scrap of paper:

- Romania: USSR 90%, Others 10%
- Greece: Britain (in accord with USA) 90%, USSR 10%
- Bulgaria: USSR 75%, Others 25%
- Yugoslavia: 50-50
- Hungary: 50-50

Stalin looked at the paper, made a blue tick with his pencil, and pushed it back. Churchill later wrote: "It was all settled in no more time than it takes to write down."

Stalin largely honored the agreement. When the Greek communists launched an insurrection after the war, Stalin -- contrary to the expectations of Tito and the Greek comrades -- provided no support. Greece was in the Western sphere; Stalin accepted this.

### The Warsaw Uprising (August-October 1944)

The Polish Home Army, loyal to the London-based Polish government-in-exile, rose against the Germans in Warsaw as the Red Army approached the Vistula. Stalin halted the Soviet advance and refused to allow Allied planes to use Soviet airfields for supply drops. The uprising was crushed after 63 days; 200,000 Poles died, mostly civilians.

Stalin's critics have called this deliberate -- waiting for the Germans to destroy the non-communist Polish resistance before the Red Army entered. Soviet accounts insisted it was military necessity: the Red Army was exhausted and overextended after the Bagration offensive. The truth is likely a combination: military difficulties provided a convenient justification for a politically desirable outcome.

### Why Did the Grand Alliance Collapse?

The standard narrative identifies Stalin's imposition of communist regimes in Eastern Europe as the cause. The reality is more complex:

1. **Poland was the breaking point.** For Stalin, control of Poland was non-negotiable -- "Through Poland, the German army has invaded Russia three times in the last thirty years." For the West, Polish self-determination was a moral commitment. Neither side could compromise.

2. **The atomic bomb changed Stalin's calculus.** The US tested the first atomic bomb on 16 July 1945, the day before Potsdam opened. Stalin already knew about the bomb through Soviet intelligence (Klaus Fuchs, the Rosenbergs). At Potsdam, Truman "casually mentioned" to Stalin that the US had "a new weapon of unusual destructive force." Stalin appeared unimpressed (he was not -- he accelerated the Soviet bomb program immediately).

3. **Ideological incompatibility:** The wartime alliance was a marriage of convenience against a common enemy. When that enemy was destroyed, there was nothing holding it together.

4. **Eastern Europe as Soviet security belt.** Stalin saw Soviet domination of Eastern Europe not as ideological expansion but as defensive necessity: buffer states that could never again serve as invasion routes. The West saw it as communist imperialism.

## 5. Early Cold War (1947-1953)

### The Sovietization of Eastern Europe

Stalin imposed communist regimes throughout Eastern Europe through varying methods:

- **Poland, East Germany, Romania, Bulgaria**: Direct Soviet military presence; coalitions were formed, then non-communist parties were eliminated
- **Czechoslovakia**: The Prague Coup (February 1948) -- communists seized power through a constitutional crisis rather than direct Soviet military intervention
- **Hungary**: Gradual communist takeover, completed by 1949

The Cominform was created in September 1947 to coordinate communist parties -- effectively replacing the Comintern (dissolved in 1943 to reassure the Western Allies).

### The Tito-Stalin Split (1948)

Josip Broz Tito, the Yugoslav communist leader, had liberated his country largely without Soviet help and maintained an independent foreign policy. In 1948, Stalin expelled Yugoslavia from the Cominform. Tito survived -- a unique case of a communist leader defying Stalin without being overthrown. The split demonstrated that:
- Even within the communist bloc, national interests could override ideological solidarity
- The Soviet model was not the only path to socialism
- Stalin's control was not absolute at the bloc's periphery

### The Berlin Blockade (1948-1949)

Stalin blockaded land access to West Berlin in June 1948, attempting to force the Western powers out of the city. The Western Allies responded with the Berlin Airlift, supplying the city by air for 11 months. Stalin lifted the blockade in May 1949 without achieving his objective. It was his most unambiguous Cold War defeat.

### The Korean War (1950-1953)

In early 1950, Stalin gave Kim Il-sung permission to invade South Korea. Why?

- **Geopolitical opportunity**: After the "loss of China" (communist victory, October 1949), the US was perceived as on the defensive in Asia. Secretary of State Acheson's January 1950 speech placed Korea outside the US "defensive perimeter."
- **Testing Western resolve**: A North Korean victory would demonstrate that the US would not fight for peripheral interests; a US intervention would tie down American forces in Asia.
- **Soviet non-involvement**: Stalin made clear the USSR would not directly intervene. This was a proxy war.

When the US-led UN force counterattacked and threatened the Chinese border, Stalin pressured Mao to intervene, providing Soviet air cover but not ground troops. The war settled into stalemate and ended three months after Stalin's death (July 1953).

## 6. The Ideology-vs-State-Interest Problem

### Was Stalin an Ideologue or a Pragmatist?

The evidence supports both readings:

**Evidence for ideology**:
- Stalin genuinely believed in Marxist-Leninist categories of analysis: imperialism, class struggle, the inevitable crisis of capitalism
- He saw the world through the lens of capitalist encirclement and the inevitability of war between capitalist powers
- His domestic policies (collectivization, industrialization, purges) were justified in ideological terms
- He invested heavily in the Comintern and international communist movements

**Evidence for pragmatism**:
- When ideology and state interest conflicted, state interest always won: the Nazi-Soviet Pact, suppression of the Greek communist insurgency, advice to the Chinese Communist Party to accept coalition government (1945)
- The Comintern was dissolved (1943) when it became inconvenient
- Stalin's nationalism ("Socialism in One Country") subordinated international revolution to Soviet state building
- He was willing to ally with anyone: Hitler (1939-41), the Western democracies (1941-45)

**Resolution**: Stalin's ideology provided the *framework* for understanding the world (capitalist encirclement, inevitable war, class analysis) but not the *operational rules* for specific decisions. In any given situation, he would choose the course that maximized Soviet state power. Ideology justified the choice post hoc.

This is a different kind of leader type than any of the ABM model's existing categories:
- Not `王道型` (Moral-Rule): Stalin had no interest in international norms or rules
- Not `霸权型` (Hegemonic): He did not seek to build a rules-based international order
- Not `强权型` (Power-Politics) in the Franz Joseph sense: prestige and honour were not drivers
- Not `昏庸型` (Inept): Stalin was a highly competent operator, albeit psychopathic

**Stalin is a distinct type**: the **ideological-realist** who uses universalist ideology instrumentally while pursuing traditional great-power security objectives. The ideology is neither irrelevant nor determinative -- it provides the language and framework for decisions made on state-interest grounds.

## 7. The Paranoia Dimension

A unique feature of Stalin's foreign policy was the systematic distortion of information by his domestic terror apparatus:

### The Great Purge and Its Foreign Policy Consequences

Between 1936 and 1938, Stalin eliminated approximately 1.5 million people through execution, imprisonment, or exile. The purges decimated the Soviet military and diplomatic corps:

- **The Red Army**: 3 of 5 marshals, 13 of 15 army commanders, 50 of 57 corps commanders, 154 of 186 division commanders were executed or imprisoned
- **The diplomatic corps**: Most experienced Soviet diplomats who had served abroad were recalled and shot as "foreign spies." Those who survived learned to tell Stalin what he wanted to hear.
- **The intelligence services**: The purge of experienced NKVD officers created an intelligence apparatus that confirmed Stalin's preconceptions rather than challenging them

This had direct foreign policy consequences:
- **1939-41**: Soviet diplomats and intelligence officers systematically understated German military capabilities and intentions because reporting honestly about the growing German threat might be interpreted as "defeatism" or "spreading panic"
- **The information bubble**: By 1941, Stalin had created an information environment where no one would tell him what he did not want to hear

### The "Doctors' Plot" (1952-1953)

In his final months, Stalin was manufacturing a new purge -- the "Doctors' Plot" -- alleging that Kremlin doctors (mostly Jewish) were conspiring to poison Soviet leaders. He was preparing mass deportations of Soviet Jews when he died on 5 March 1953.

The Docters' Plot illustrates how Stalin's domestic paranoia and foreign policy intersected: his growing anti-Semitic campaign would have shattered Soviet relations with Israel and the Western left, and potentially triggered a major Cold War crisis. His death prevented this.

## 8. Key Quotes

**On capitalist encirclement** (1934, 17th Party Congress):
> "The task is to overtake and surpass the advanced capitalist countries technically and economically... Either we do this, or they will crush us."

**On Hitler's intentions** (private, 1939 to Politburo):
> "We know what Hitler wants. He wants to fight the Western powers. We must not interfere. Let them weaken each other."

**On the Nazi-Soviet Pact** (to Molotov, August 1939):
> "Of course, it's all a game to see who can fool whom. I know what Hitler's up to. He thinks he's outsmarted me, but actually it's I who have outsmarted him."

**The percentages agreement** (to Churchill, October 1944):
> (Pushes paper back after ticking it) "It is all settled."

**On the atomic bomb** (to Kurchatov, the Soviet nuclear physicist, August 1945):
> "Provide us with atomic weapons in the shortest possible time. You know that Hiroshima has shaken the whole world. The balance has been destroyed. Provide the bomb -- it will remove a great danger from us."

**On Poland** (to Churchill and Roosevelt, Yalta):
> "For the Russian people, the question of Poland is not only a question of honour but also a question of security... Throughout history, Poland has been the corridor through which the enemy has passed into Russia."

**The "divisions" quote** (attributed, when asked about papal influence, 1935):
> "The Pope? How many divisions has he got?"

## 9. Calibration Notes for the ABM Model

| Parameter | Stalin | Notes |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| **Leader type** | Ideological-realist (doesn't fit existing 4 types cleanly) | Closest to `强权型` + `霸权型` hybrid, with distinctive features |
| **Ideology constraint** | Moderate: ideology provides framework but is overridden by state interest when they conflict | Unlike Wilson (whose moral framework constrained action), Stalin's ideology justified whatever action he took |
| **Trust of other actors** | Near-zero: all states are actual or potential enemies; allies are temporary instruments | "The only way to make a friend is to control him" |
| **Risk tolerance** | High but calculated: willing to gamble (Nazi-Soviet Pact, Korean War) when odds appeared favorable | Not reckless; did not directly confront the US militarily |
| **Information distortion** | Severe: subordinates systematically underreport threats and overreport successes to avoid purges | Unique to Stalin; the terror apparatus distorts decision inputs |
| **Time horizon** | Long: willing to wait decades for capitalism's internal contradictions to unfold | Believes in historical inevitability; patience is strategic |
| **Domestic constraint** | Near-zero: no legislature, no public opinion, no press freedom | The only domestic constraint is the inner circle's loyalty, which is maintained through terror |
| **Prestige motive** | Low: Stalin was not driven by personal honour or dynastic prestige | His ego was massive but expressed through power and control, not traditional prestige |
| **Moral constraint** | Near-zero: no indication of moral considerations in foreign policy decisions | "The death of one man is a tragedy. The death of millions is a statistic" (attributed) |

**Stalin's distinctiveness for the model**: He represents a leader type not fully captured by the current four categories -- a paranoid, ideologically-framed but operationally-pragmatic great power nationalist whose domestic terror apparatus systematically distorted his foreign policy information. The most salient feature for modeling purposes is the **information distortion mechanism**: Stalin's subordinates were incentivized to tell him what he wanted to hear, creating a systematically biased picture of the external world. This is fundamentally different from Franz Joseph (who had accurate information but chose to ignore it), Nicholas II (whose information was limited by his isolation), or Wilson (whose information was filtered through moral categories).
