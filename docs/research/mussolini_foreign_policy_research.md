# Benito Mussolini: Fascist Foreign Policy Between Ideology and Opportunism (1922-1945)

## Overview

Benito Mussolini (1883-1945) ruled Italy from 1922 to 1943 as *Il Duce* of the Fascist regime. His foreign policy presents one of the most challenging cases for leader-type classification in the ABM model. He combines elements of `强权型` (Power-Politics) -- obsession with prestige, willingness to use military force -- with `昏庸型` (Inept) characteristics -- catastrophic overestimation of national capabilities, systematic information distortion from propaganda feedback, and personalization of decision-making to the point of dysfunction. Unlike Franz Joseph, whose error was fatalistic honour-driven escalation, Mussolini's error was the belief that performative strength was actual strength -- he convinced himself of the illusion his own propaganda created.

---

## 1. Strategic Doctrine: "Peso Determinante" and "Mare Nostrum"

### "Decisive Weight" (Peso Determinante)

Mussolini's core strategic concept was that Italy -- too weak to dominate but too strong to ignore -- could swing between great powers to extract maximum concessions. This was not original to fascism: Italy had done exactly this in 1915, abandoning the Triple Alliance for the Entente in exchange for territorial promises (the Treaty of London). Mussolini sought to institutionalize this opportunistic posture.

The doctrine held that Italy should:
- Maintain sufficient military strength to be a meaningful ally or threat
- Avoid permanent commitments that would limit freedom of action
- Exploit moments of international tension to extract territorial concessions
- Present Italy as the arbiter of European crises

### "Mare Nostrum" (Our Sea)

The Mediterranean was the geographic focus of Mussolini's imperial ambitions. His rhetoric invoked Roman imperial glory: the Mediterranean as Italy's natural domain, "an Italian lake." This required:
- Confrontation with British naval power in the Mediterranean
- Control of the Adriatic (confrontation with Yugoslavia)
- Influence over the Balkans and North Africa
- Naval and air bases projecting Italian power across the sea

### The Cult of Romanita

Fascist ideology framed foreign policy in explicitly imperial terms. The fascist slogan "Believe, Obey, Fight" and the glorification of war as "the most noble and heroic of human activities" were not merely propaganda for domestic consumption -- Mussolini genuinely believed that war was the ultimate test of national vitality. His 1932 entry on fascism for the *Enciclopedia Italiana* (ghostwritten by Giovanni Gentile but signed by Mussolini) stated:

> "Fascism believes neither in the possibility nor the utility of perpetual peace. War alone brings up to their highest tension all human energies and puts the stamp of nobility upon the peoples who have the courage to meet it."

### Ideologue or Pragmatist?

The scholarly consensus (MacGregor Knox, Denis Mack Smith, R.J.B. Bosworth) is that Mussolini was **both** -- an ideological fascist who genuinely believed in the regenerative power of war AND a tactical opportunist who adjusted his methods to circumstances. The key insight: **he could not distinguish between rhetoric and reality.** Years of performative fascist propaganda convinced not only his audience but himself that Italy was a great military power.

## 2. Key Foreign Policy Decisions

### Corfu Incident (August-September 1923)

Italian general Enrico Tellini, part of a boundary commission, was murdered on Greek territory. Mussolini bombarded and briefly occupied the Greek island of Corfu. When the League of Nations demanded withdrawal, Mussolini threatened to leave the League and bomb Athens. Greece paid an indemnity. 

**Motivation**: Pure prestige -- Mussolini's first demonstration that fascist Italy would use force and disregard international institutions. It "worked" in the short term, reinforcing the lesson that aggression paid.

### Invasion of Ethiopia (October 1935-May 1936)

**Why Ethiopia?** 
- Revenge for Adwa (1896), when Ethiopian forces had defeated an Italian army -- the humiliation had festered for 40 years
- Imperial prestige: Italy lacked a "real" empire compared to Britain and France
- Domestic consolidation: a victorious war would unite the nation behind fascism
- Economic mirage: Ethiopia was believed (falsely) to be rich in resources

Italy deployed 500,000 troops (many colonial), used poison gas, and conquered Ethiopia in seven months. The League imposed sanctions (but not on oil). Haile Selassie's appeal to the League was ignored.

**Consequence**: The Ethiopian war destroyed the Stresa Front (the 1935 agreement between Italy, France, and Britain to contain Germany). Mussolini was isolated as an international pariah -- and drew closer to Hitler, the only major European leader who had supported his invasion.

### Spanish Civil War Intervention (1936-1939)

Mussolini sent over 50,000 troops (the Corpo Truppe Volontarie) to support Franco, along with substantial air and naval forces. Italian forces were involved in the bombing of Barcelona and Guernica.

**Motivation**: Ideological solidarity with fascism, strategic positioning in the Mediterranean, and prestige -- a fascist victory in Spain would demonstrate fascism's ascendance. But the cost was enormous and the strategic benefit marginal: Franco remained neutral in WWII, refusing to join the Axis.

### The Pact of Steel (May 1939)

Mussolini signed a full military alliance with Nazi Germany. Ciano (his Foreign Minister and son-in-law) opposed it, noting that Italy was not ready for war. Mussolini overrode him and committed Italy to fight alongside Germany in any war -- offensive or defensive, regardless of who started it.

**Why?** By 1939, Mussolini believed that Germany would win any European war. If Italy stayed neutral, a victorious Germany would punish Italian fence-sitting. If Italy joined Germany, it would share in the spoils. The logic was pure opportunism -- but the premise (German victory) proved correct only until 1941, not beyond.

### Declaration of War on France (10 June 1940)

With France collapsing under the German Blitzkrieg, Mussolini declared war. His statement to Marshal Badoglio: **"I need a few thousand dead so that I can sit at the peace conference as a man who has fought."**

The Italian offensive into southern France was a disaster despite French collapse -- Italian forces made negligible gains against a French army that was simultaneously fighting the Germans. This was performative war-making: Mussolini needed the *appearance* of having fought, not actual military results.

### Invasion of Greece (28 October 1940)

Mussolini launched the invasion without adequately informing his generals, with completely inadequate forces, in late autumn through mountain terrain, against an army that had been mobilized. Ciano wrote in his diary that Mussolini believed Greece would collapse in days and that "the whole thing will be over in two weeks."

The Greek army not only stopped the Italian advance but counterattacked into Albania (Italian-occupied since 1939). Germany had to intervene in April 1941 to rescue the Italian position. The Greek disaster exposed the emptiness of fascist military pretensions and marked Mussolini's permanent subordination to Hitler.

**Why did he do it?** Hitler had not consulted Mussolini about the occupation of Romania. Mussolini was "paying Hitler back in his own coin" -- asserting his independence. He chose Greece because Ciano had assured him Greek elites were pro-fascist and would not resist. Both premises were false.

## 3. Decision-Making Pattern

### The Personalization of Power

Mussolini held multiple ministries simultaneously: at various times he was simultaneously Prime Minister, Foreign Minister, War Minister, Navy Minister, and Air Minister. This was not efficiency -- it eliminated institutional checks. No one could tell Mussolini "no" because he was everyone's boss.

### "Announce First, Plan Later"

The performative dimension of fascist foreign policy systematically distorted military planning:
- Mussolini would announce a campaign before consulting his generals
- The generals, knowing Mussolini wanted war, would underestimate the requirements (they had learned that telling him the truth about military inadequacy was politically dangerous)
- The information bubble reinforced itself: Mussolini believed Italy was strong because his subordinates told him Italy was strong

### The Information Bubble

Fascism's propaganda apparatus -- press control, cult of personality, suppression of dissent -- created a feedback loop where Mussolini received systematically optimistic assessments. Bad news was suppressed or reinterpreted. The Italian military's deficiencies (outdated equipment, poor training, inadequate logistics) were known but could not be stated openly because acknowledging them would be "anti-fascist defeatism."

### Relationship with the Military

Mussolini distrusted his generals and they distrusted him:
- Marshal Badoglio (Chief of the Supreme General Staff) was competent but marginalized
- Mussolini frequently ignored military advice (Greece 1940, continued offensives despite inadequate forces)
- The military learned to tell Mussolini what he wanted to hear
- When things went wrong (Greece, North Africa), Mussolini blamed the generals

### Ciano and the Managed Dissent

Count Galeazzo Ciano, Mussolini's son-in-law and Foreign Minister (1936-1943), was one of the few who could express dissent. His diaries reveal a man increasingly horrified by Mussolini's decisions (the Pact of Steel, the declaration of war, the Greek invasion) but unable to stop them. When Ciano voted to depose Mussolini at the Fascist Grand Council (July 1943), Mussolini had him executed (January 1944).

## 4. The German Alliance: From Senior Partner to Puppet

### Initial Suspicion (1933-1935)

Mussolini initially viewed Hitler with aristocratic contempt. At their first meeting (Venice, 1934), Mussolini dressed in full fascist regalia while Hitler wore civilian clothes and a raincoat. The Stresa Front (April 1935) aligned Italy with Britain and France against German expansion.

### The Pivot (1936-1939)

Ethiopia was the turning point. Britain and France imposed sanctions; Germany did not. Hitler supported Mussolini's Ethiopian adventure, recognized the Italian conquest, and sent congratulations. Mussolini drew the obvious conclusion: Germany was Italy's natural ally, and the democracies would always obstruct Italy's imperial ambitions.

The Rome-Berlin Axis (announced November 1936) was initially informal. The Pact of Steel (1939) made it a binding military alliance. The Tripartite Pact (1940) added Japan.

### Subordination (1941-1943)

After the Greek disaster and the North African defeats, Mussolini was effectively Hitler's junior partner. German forces operated on Italian soil, German generals gave orders to Italian units, and Italy's war aims were subordinated to Germany's. The relationship Mussolini had sought -- equal partnership between two fascist powers -- had become a satellite relationship.

### The Racial Laws (1938)

Italy adopted anti-Semitic racial laws in 1938 under German pressure. There was almost no domestic Italian demand for these laws -- Italian fascism had not been significantly anti-Semitic before 1938, and Italian Jews had been integrated into Italian society. The laws were deeply unpopular and demonstrated that Mussolini was willing to sacrifice Italian interests and values for the German alliance.

## 5. Ideology vs. National Interest

The Mussolini case demonstrates that ideology and national interest **cannot be cleanly separated** when the ideology glorifies war as an end in itself.

| Factor | Evidence |
|--------|----------|
| **Ideology as self-reinforcing** | Mussolini's glorification of war led him to seek wars. Each "successful" war (Corfu, Ethiopia, Spain) reinforced the belief that war was the path to greatness. |
| **Prestige as national interest** | For Mussolini, Italy's prestige WAS its national interest. Looking weak was worse than being weak. This is similar to Franz Joseph's "decently perish" but driven by ideology rather than dynastic fatalism. |
| **National interest sacrificed to ideology** | The racial laws, the Pact of Steel, the declaration of war on France and Greece, and the continued fighting after 1941 all damaged Italian national interests while serving fascist ideological commitments. |
| **The Ciano dissent** | Ciano represents the "pragmatic nationalist" alternative -- the path Mussolini could have taken but did not. Ciano's execution symbolizes the elimination of pragmatic dissent within the fascist system. |

## 6. The Endgame (1943-1945)

### The Grand Council Vote (24-25 July 1943)

The Fascist Grand Council, the regime's highest body, voted 19-7 to depose Mussolini and restore King Victor Emmanuel III's constitutional authority. This was the one institutional mechanism that actually worked -- but only because the military disaster in North Africa and the Allied invasion of Sicily had made the war's outcome unmistakable even to fascist loyalists.

Mussolini was arrested. The King appointed Badoglio as Prime Minister, who negotiated an armistice with the Allies (September 1943).

### The Salo Republic (1943-1945)

German paratroopers rescued Mussolini from imprisonment. Hitler installed him as head of the "Italian Social Republic" -- a puppet state in German-occupied northern Italy. Mussolini was now a figurehead, issuing decrees that mattered only if the Germans enforced them. He had Ciano and other Grand Council "traitors" executed in January 1944.

### Death (28 April 1945)

Mussolini was captured by Italian partisans while attempting to flee to Switzerland disguised in a German soldier's coat. He was executed and his body, along with his mistress Clara Petacci and other fascist officials, was hung upside down at a Milan gas station -- the same square where fascist squads had displayed the bodies of their victims.

## 7. Calibration Notes for the ABM Model

| Parameter | Mussolini Value | Evidence |
|-----------|----------------|-----------|
| **Leader type** | `昏庸型` hybrid with `强权型` tendencies | The Conrad calibration (preventive war impulse, enemy underestimation, ally over-optimism, victory inertia, multi-front delusion) fits Mussolini remarkably well |
| **Prestige weighting** | Very high: prestige/performative considerations often override strategic rationality | "I need a few thousand dead so I can sit at the peace conference" |
| **Information distortion** | Severe: subordinates systematically underreport problems because honesty is political liability | The fascist propaganda feedback loop: no one could tell Il Duce the truth |
| **Risk tolerance** | Very high but miscalibrated: takes risks based on overestimated capabilities | Greece 1940, declaration of war on France 1940 |
| **Ally under-reliance** | Initially sees Germany as instrument for Italian goals, becomes increasingly dependent | From Stresa Front (anti-German) to Pact of Steel to de facto German protectorate |
| **Multi-front delusion** | Declared war on France AND invaded Greece simultaneously while fighting in North Africa | Three-front war with inadequate forces |
| **Victory inertia** | Each "successful" aggression (Corfu, Ethiopia) reinforced the belief that the next one would work too | Pattern continued until catastrophic failure in Greece exposed the illusion |
| **Domestic constraint** | Low: no parliament, no free press, no independent military leadership | The Grand Council vote was the ONLY institutional check, and it came too late |

**Mussolini's distinctiveness**: He represents a leader whose propaganda apparatus not only deceived the public but deceived the leader himself. Unlike Franz Joseph (who knew his army was inadequate but chose war anyway for dynastic honour), Mussolini genuinely believed Italy was a great military power because no one could tell him otherwise. This is the "information bubble" parameter that is distinct from both the prestige-seeking of `强权型` and the ineptitude of `昏庸型`. It suggests a possible additional dimension: **information-system integrity** -- the degree to which the leader receives accurate assessments of national capability.
