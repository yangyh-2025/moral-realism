# Woodrow Wilson: Moral Internationalism and Its Limits (1912-1924)

## Overview

Woodrow Wilson (1856-1924) is the archetypal "moral internationalist" leader in the 1914-1950 period -- and simultaneously the most revealing case study of the gap between universalist rhetoric and actual foreign policy. For the ABM model, he maps to the `王道型` (Moral-Rule) leader type, with its characteristic behavioral weights: respect for sovereignty (+0.2 bonus), disrespect penalty (-0.3), alliance cooperation (+0.15), and military deterrence as "last resort only." But Wilson's actual behavior was more complex than the ideal type, exhibiting tensions that illuminate the limits of moral-internationalist leadership in an anarchic international system.

---

## 1. Wilson's Moral Framework: Origins and Content

### Presbyterian Covenant Theology as Foreign Policy

Wilson was the son and grandson of Presbyterian ministers. His moral framework for international relations derived from covenant theology -- the idea that God establishes binding agreements (covenants) with peoples, and that breaking those covenants brings divine punishment. This translated directly into his conception of the League of Nations as a "covenant" that would bind nations to peace under the moral law.

His academic training as a political scientist (PhD from Johns Hopkins, professor at Princeton, president of Princeton 1902-1910) gave him a systematic approach but did not moderate his moral intensity. His 1889 book *The State* argued that democracy was the highest form of government because it embodied moral law.

### The 14 Points (8 January 1918)

Wilson's 14 Points speech to Congress was the most systematic articulation of his international vision:

1. Open covenants of peace, openly arrived at (no secret treaties)
2. Absolute freedom of navigation upon the seas
3. Removal of economic barriers and equality of trade conditions
4. Reduction of national armaments to the lowest point consistent with domestic safety
5. Impartial adjustment of colonial claims, taking account of native populations' interests
6. Evacuation of Russian territory, independent determination of Russia's political development
7. Evacuation and restoration of Belgium
8. Liberation of France, restoration of Alsace-Lorraine
9. Readjustment of Italian frontiers along lines of nationality
10. Autonomous development for the peoples of Austria-Hungary
11. Evacuation of Romania, Serbia, Montenegro; access to the sea for Serbia
12. Autonomous development for non-Turkish peoples of the Ottoman Empire; free passage through the Dardanelles
13. An independent Polish state with access to the sea
14. A general association of nations (the League of Nations) to afford mutual guarantees of political independence and territorial integrity

### "Making the World Safe for Democracy"

Wilson's war message to Congress (2 April 1917) contained his most famous phrase:

> "The world must be made safe for democracy. Its peace must be planted upon the tested foundations of political liberty. We have no selfish ends to serve. We desire no conquest, no dominion. We seek no indemnities for ourselves, no material compensation for the sacrifices we shall freely make."

He framed American entry as a moral obligation, not a strategic calculation:

> "Neutrality is no longer feasible or desirable where the peace of the world is involved and the freedom of its peoples, and the menace to that peace and freedom lies in the existence of autocratic governments backed by organized force which is controlled wholly by their will, not by the will of their people."

He concluded with a statement of almost religious self-abnegation:

> "It is a fearful thing to lead this great peaceful people into war... But the right is more precious than peace, and we shall fight for the things which we have always carried nearest our hearts."

## 2. The Decision for War (1914-1917)

### Neutrality (1914-1916)

Wilson maintained American neutrality for nearly three years. His 1916 re-election campaign slogan was "He Kept Us Out of War." But neutrality was increasingly strained:

- German unrestricted submarine warfare (sinking of the Lusitania, May 1915, 1,198 dead including 128 Americans)
- The Sussex Pledge (May 1916): Germany agreed to limit submarine warfare after Wilson threatened to sever diplomatic relations
- American economic entanglement: US trade with the Allies grew from $825 million (1914) to $3.2 billion (1916), while trade with the Central Powers collapsed from $169 million to $1 million

### The Zimmermann Telegram (February 1917)

British intelligence intercepted and shared a telegram from German Foreign Secretary Arthur Zimmermann to Mexico, proposing a military alliance against the United States. If the US entered the war, Germany would support Mexico in recovering Texas, New Mexico, and Arizona. The publication of the telegram in American newspapers on 1 March 1917 outraged public opinion.

### The Final Decision (March-April 1917)

Germany resumed unrestricted submarine warfare on 1 February 1917. Wilson severed diplomatic relations but still hesitated. The key cabinet meeting of 20 March 1917 was unanimous for war. Wilson asked Congress on 2 April.

His framing was careful: this was not war against the German people but against "autocratic government." The enemy was not Germany but Prussian militarism. This distinction was essential to the moral framework -- it allowed Wilson to frame American intervention as liberation, not conquest.

**Significance for the model**: Wilson's 30-month path from neutrality to war reveals a leader whose moral framework permitted war only when it could be framed as a moral crusade. The Zimmermann Telegram and unrestricted submarine warfare were the necessary triggers because they could be presented as intolerable violations of neutral rights and international law -- not as American strategic interests.

## 3. The League of Nations as Overriding Priority

### Why the League?

For Wilson, the League was not one policy among many. It was THE policy. All other points were subordinate to Article 14. His reasoning:

1. **Covenant theology**: A binding international covenant would replace the anarchic balance of power with a moral community
2. **Democratic peace theory**: If all nations were democracies overseen by a collective security organization, war would become impossible
3. **Redemption of the war's sacrifice**: Without the League, the war would have been "a meaningless slaughter"

### Concessions at Versailles

To save the League, Wilson made devastating concessions at the Paris Peace Conference:

- **Colonial mandates** (not independence): Point 5's "interests of native populations" was satisfied by the mandate system, which essentially repackaged imperialism under League supervision. Self-determination was for Europeans.
- **The war guilt clause (Article 231)** and massive reparations: Wilson opposed punitive terms but yielded to Clemenceau
- **The Rhineland occupation**: Wilson accepted a 15-year Allied occupation
- **The Shandong clause**: Wilson allowed Japan to retain German concessions in Shandong, China, outraging Chinese nationalists and validating the critique that self-determination was a European privilege

The Japanese proposed a racial equality clause for the League Covenant. Wilson -- a Virginia-born segregationist who had resegregated the federal civil service -- opposed it. The clause was defeated despite majority support (11 of 17 commission members). This is the single most revealing episode of the limits of Wilsonian universalism.

### The Senate Rejection (1919-1920)

The Treaty of Versailles required Senate ratification. The opposition:

- **The "Irreconcilables"** (Borah, Johnson, La Follette): ~16 senators who opposed the League under any conditions. William Borah argued the League would entangle America in European wars and violate the Constitution's war-making powers.
- **The "Reservationists"** (Lodge, Root): ~35 senators led by Henry Cabot Lodge, who would accept the League with amendments preserving American sovereignty -- particularly the right of Congress to declare war and control over the Monroe Doctrine.

Wilson refused to compromise with Lodge. He embarked on a nationwide speaking tour to rally public support for the unamended treaty.

### The Pueblo Speech (25 September 1919)

At Pueblo, Colorado, Wilson gave his most emotional defense of the League:

> "I wish that they [fallen American soldiers] could feel the moral obligation that rests upon us not to go back on those boys, but to see the thing through, to see it through to the end and make good their redemption of the world. For nothing less depends upon this decision, nothing less than the liberation and salvation of the world."

That night, Wilson collapsed. He returned to Washington and suffered a massive stroke on 2 October 1919. For the remaining 18 months of his presidency, he was incapacitated -- his wife Edith and his doctor effectively controlled access to him. The Senate voted on the treaty twice (November 1919, March 1920) and rejected it both times. The United States never joined the League of Nations.

**The tragedy of Wilson's inflexibility**: His refusal to compromise with Lodge's reservations was a direct expression of his moral framework. Compromise on the covenant would violate the covenant -- a mortal sin in covenant theology. Wilson would rather lose the League entirely than accept an imperfect one. In the ABM model, this is the `王道型` leader's tragic flaw: moral purity over political effectiveness.

## 4. The Tension Between Universalism and National Interest

### Wilson's Interventions in Latin America

Wilson intervened militarily in Latin America more than any previous president:

- **Mexico (1914, 1916-17)**: Occupation of Veracruz (1914); Pershing Expedition pursuing Pancho Villa (1916-17). Wilson justified these as promoting constitutional government.
- **Haiti (1915-1934)**: US Marines occupied Haiti, controlled its finances, and imposed a new constitution. Over 2,000 Haitians were killed in suppressing resistance.
- **Dominican Republic (1916-1924)**: Military occupation and imposition of US financial control.
- **Nicaragua (1912-1933)**: Continued the Taft-era occupation.

Wilson justified these interventions in paternalistic terms -- he was "teaching" Latin Americans to "elect good men." His Secretary of State William Jennings Bryan (a pacifist) defended the Haiti intervention as protecting American investments and preventing European intervention.

### Racial Policy at Home

Wilson was a segregationist. He resegregated the federal civil service, screened *The Birth of a Nation* (the pro-KKK film) at the White House -- the first film ever shown there -- and defended segregation as in the interest of both races. W.E.B. Du Bois, who had initially supported Wilson, became a sharp critic.

### The "Wilsonian Moment" (Erez Manela, 2007)

Erez Manela's book *The Wilsonian Moment* documents how colonial peoples around the world seized on Wilson's rhetoric of self-determination:

- **Ho Chi Minh** (then Nguyen Ai Quoc) rented a morning suit and presented a petition for Vietnamese independence at Versailles. Wilson ignored him.
- **Egyptian nationalists** led by Saad Zaghlul demanded independence, citing Wilson's principles. Britain arrested Zaghlul, triggering the 1919 Revolution.
- **Korean nationalists** issued a Declaration of Independence (1 March 1919) invoking Wilson. Japan crushed the movement.
- **Chinese students** protested the Shandong decision in the May Fourth Movement (1919), which transformed Chinese nationalism.

Manela's argument: Wilson's rhetoric had genuine global impact -- it raised expectations among colonized peoples that he could not (and, in many cases, did not intend to) fulfill. The disappointment of the "Wilsonian moment" radicalized anti-colonial movements and contributed to the appeal of Leninism as an alternative universalist ideology.

**Significance for the model**: This is the "credibility gap" dimension of `王道型` leadership. Moral-internationalist rhetoric creates expectations that reality cannot satisfy. When the gap becomes visible, it undermines the leader's credibility and radicalizes those who believed the rhetoric.

## 5. The Stroke and Its Consequences (October 1919 - March 1921)

Wilson's stroke on 2 October 1919 left him paralyzed on his left side and severely cognitively impaired. His wife Edith Wilson and his physician Cary Grayson controlled access to him for the remainder of his term. Key cabinet members (Secretary of State Lansing, who convened cabinet meetings in Wilson's absence) were fired or sidelined.

The stroke meant that:
1. Wilson could not negotiate with the Senate during the crucial ratification period
2. He could not receive or evaluate new intelligence about European conditions
3. His judgment, already compromised by his moral rigidity, became catastrophic

The 25th Amendment (presidential disability) was drafted partly in response to this episode. In the ABM model context, this is a reminder that individual leader capacity is not constant -- physical/mental decline can transform decision-making even within a single leader type.

## 7. Key Quotes

**On democracy and peace** (War Message, 2 April 1917):
> "A steadfast concert for peace can never be maintained except by a partnership of democratic nations. No autocratic government could be trusted to keep faith within it or observe its covenants."

**On self-determination** (Address to Congress, 11 February 1918):
> "Self-determination is not a mere phrase. It is an imperative principle of action, which statesmen will henceforth ignore at their peril."

**On the League** (Pueblo, Colorado, 25 September 1919):
> "There is one thing that the American people always rise to and extend their hand to, and that is the truth of justice and of liberty and of peace. We have accepted that truth and we are going to be led by it, and it is going to lead us, and through us the world, out into pastures of quietness and peace such as the world never dreamed of before."

**The racial equality clause rejection** (Wilson to the Japanese delegation):
Wilson did not publicly explain his reasoning, but his private view was that racial equality was a domestic impossibility for a segregationist president. The American South would reject any treaty that implied racial equality.

## 8. Calibration Notes for the ABM Model

| Model Parameter | Wilsonian Value | Evidence |
|-----------------|-----------------|----------|
| **Leader type** | `王道型` (Moral-Rule) | Consistent moral-internationalist framing of all actions |
| **Sovereignty respect bonus** | +0.2 (current value) | Core to Wilson's self-conception |
| **Sovereignty violation penalty** | -0.3 (current value) | BUT with a crucial exception: violations justified as "teaching democracy" / paternalistic intervention don't trigger the penalty in Wilson's calculus |
| **Military force as last resort** | High threshold, but not absolute: 30 months of neutrality, then full war | The "last resort" threshold can be cleared when framed as moral crusade |
| **Compromise tolerance** | Very low (0.0 to -0.1) -- moral rigidity | The Lodge reservation episode: Wilson would rather lose the League entirely than accept an imperfect one |
| **Domestic constraint** | High: Senate ratification requirement, 1918 midterms (Republicans took Congress), stroke | Democratic institutional constraints on Wilsonian internationalism |
| **Credibility gap** | Growing over time: rhetoric outstrips delivery | The "Wilsonian moment" phenomenon: raised expectations, disappointing reality |
| **Non-European self-determination** | Near-zero: Wilson did not meaningfully apply self-determination outside Europe | Shandong, Egypt, Korea, Vietnam, Haiti |

**Wilson's distinctive pattern**: He is the `王道型` leader who can authorize massive violence (WWI) and territorial intervention (Latin America) when those actions are framed as moral imperatives. His moral framework is NOT a constraint on action per se -- it is a constraint on the *justification* of action. Any action that can be framed as serving democracy, self-determination, or the rule of law is permissible. This makes Wilson, paradoxically, both the most morally constrained and the most morally self-authorizing leader type in the model.
