## Claim 1: "Sovereignty in the modern world cannot mean isolation"
### Supposed source: Ebert, Reichstag, June 1920

**Verdict: HIGHLY SUSPECT -- likely a secondary-source paraphrase, not a direct quote.**

**Search method:** Searched reichstagsprotokolle.de (site access failed -- possibly offline or restricted), FES digital archives, Muehlhausen critical edition indices, De Gruyter collections for "Souveraenitaet," "bedeuten/bedeutet/heisst," "Absonderung/Isolierung/Abkapselung" in Ebert's 1920 Reichstag speeches. Also searched English phrasing across JSTOR, Cambridge, and primary-source repositories.

**What was found -- and what was not:**
- No instance of this sentence in English or German surfaced in any primary-source database, parliamentary record, or critical edition.
- No German original found: "Souveraenitaet in der modernen Welt kann nicht Absonderung/Isolierung/Abkapselung bedeuten."
- Ebert's *actual* vocabulary when discussing sovereignty used: "Selbstbestimmungsrecht" (right to self-determination), "Gleichberechtigung" (equal rights), "Freiheit" (freedom), "national independence" (in a September 1920 letter to the Swedish PM).

**What Ebert DID say -- closest conceptual match (February 1919):**

Ebert's opening address to the National Assembly (6/11 February 1919) contains the closest thematic parallel. He argued that the German state must be founded "only on the free right of self-determination, internally and externally":

> Original German: *"Nur auf das freie Selbstbestimmungsrecht wollen wir unseren Staat gruenden, nach innen und nach aussen."*

He also warned against enforced isolation through the Versailles terms:
> "Germany must not again fall into the old misery of disintegration and confinement... Only a great united possibility of developing our economic life -- a politically capable, strong, single Germany -- can secure the future of our people."

And proclaimed:
> "We have solemnly renounced the principle of violence among peoples; there too we want law and freedom to prevail." (*"Dem Gewaltprinzip zwischen den Voelkern haben wir feierlich abgesagt; auch dort wollen wir, dass das Recht und die Freiheit zur Geltung komme."*)

**Why it matters that the phrasing is English and modern:**
The clean, modern English formulation -- "Sovereignty in the modern world cannot mean isolation" -- reads as a textbook or popular-history gloss on Ebert's thought rather than a direct quotation. The vocabulary is late-20th/21st-century international-relations idiom, not the language of 1920 German political discourse. Two plausible candidate sources:
1. Harmer (2008), *Friedrich Ebert: Germany* in the "Makers of the Modern World" series (Haus Publishing, ISBN 978-1-905791-63-7), which is aimed at a general English-language readership.
2. An American or British IR textbook summarizing Ebert's position as a foil to "realist" sovereignty theory.

**Flag: LIKELY PARAPHRASE. Do not attribute as direct quote without locating exact source. If used, cite as "[Author]'s characterization of Ebert's view" rather than as a verbatim Ebert quotation.**

## Claim 2: Ebert speech to Deutsche Friedensgesellschaft (German Peace Society), November 1923

**Verdict: UNCONFIRMED -- no primary evidence found; the speech may be misattributed, misdated, or confused with a different event.**

**Search method:** Searched FES archives, Scheer (1981) monograph, the Muehlhausen (2017) edition's contents/reviews, 1923 crisis document collections (kgparl.de), and broad web searches in both English and German for Ebert + "Deutsche Friedensgesellschaft," "Friedensbund," "Friedensgesellschaft," "German Peace Society."

**What was found:**
- Scheer's *Die deutsche Friedensgesellschaft (1892-1933)* (Frankfurt, 1981) is the standard monograph on the organized German peace movement. No passage or index entry linking Ebert to a November 1923 speech to the DFG surfaced.
- The document collection *Das Krisenjahr 1923* (kgparl.de, published 1980) -- a specialized scholarly collection on the crisis year -- contains NO Ebert DFG speech among its documents.
- The Muehlhausen (2017) critical edition of Ebert's collected presidential speeches -- which would be the natural location for any major address -- has no index entry for "Deutsche Friedensgesellschaft" or "Friedensbund" in reviews of the volume.
- The year 1923 was the absolute nadir of Ebert's presidency: Ruhr occupation, hyperinflation, Hitler's Beer Hall Putsch (8-9 November 1923), communist uprisings, and a declaration of a state of emergency. Ebert was consumed with crisis management (emergency decrees under Article 48, cabinet meetings on the passive resistance, military security). A speech to a peace society would have been extraordinarly notable -- and would certainly have been recorded.

**Alternative possibilities for confusion:**
1. **The Freiburg Peace Congress (August 1923):** The *Dritte Internationale Demokratische Friedenskongress* was held in Freiburg in August 1923, organized by the *Friedensbund Deutscher Katholiken* (Catholic peace league, distinct from the DFG), with French parliamentarian Marc Sangnier co-leading. Ebert is not documented as a speaker. The DFG under Harry Graf Kessler was involved as a participant, not the primary organizer.
2. **The Neujahrsaufruf (December 1923/January 1924):** Ebert's New Year address to German youth (see Claim 3) was a response to a declaration from the *Jugendverbaende* (youth associations), NOT the DFG. The content overlapped with peace-oriented themes, which may have led to conflation.
3. **Ebert's 1917 SPD Parteitag speech:** Contained the phrase "eine Politik der Voelkerverstaendigung" (a policy of international understanding). This earlier speech may have been misdated to 1923 by a secondary source.
4. **Confusion with Friedrich-Karl Scheer's book:** A source may cite Scheer's monograph on the DFG alongside Ebert's peace-related statements, and a subsequent reader may have elided the two references.

**Flag: VERY LIKELY MISATTRIBUTED. Verify the exact source of this claim -- who cites it, and what reference do they give? -- before using.**

## Claim 3: Ebert's New Year address, 1924

**Verdict: CONFIRMED. Authentic primary source exists with full text available.**

**Search method:** Searched FES digital archives, BBF (Bibliothek fuer Bildungsgeschichtliche Forschung) scripta repository, Arbeiter-Jugend periodical records, and German newspaper archives.

**Authenticated provenance:** The document is "Neujahrsaufruf des Reichspraesidenten an die deutsche Jugend" (New Year Appeal of the Reich President to German Youth), published in the journal *Arbeiter-Jugend*, 16. Jahrgang, 1924. It was Ebert's response to a declaration from the *Ausschuss der deutschen Jugendverbaende* (Committee of German Youth Associations), which had been delivered to him in November 1923 and contained a declaration of loyalty to the Reich's unity (*Bekenntnis zur Reichseinheit*).

**Authentic German text (full, from the BBF scan):**

> "Zur Jahreswende seufzt das deutsche Volk unter hartem Druck und quaelendem Elend. Die wirtschaftliche Not weiter Volkskreise wird erschwert durch die laehmende Ungewissheit ueber das zukuenftige Schicksal unseres Landes.
>
> Dennoch ist die Hoffnung im deutschen Lande noch nicht erstorben, die Hoffnung auf einen Zukunft, auf eine beßere Zukunft. Diese Hoffnung aber hat ihre besten und staerksten Wurzeln in dem Vertrauen auf den Willen und die Kraft der deutschen Jugend.
>
> Das junge Deutschland hat dieses Vertrauen bisher gerechtfertigt. Es hat seinerzeit die Grenzen unseres Landes schuetzen helfen, es hat erst in juengster Zeit mit erfreulicher Einmuetigkeit und Geschlossenheit sich fuer des Reiches Einheit und Geschlossenheit eingesetzt; es hat in Selbstzucht und in Erkenntnis der Notwendigkeit Einfachheit und Schlichtheit zum Grundsatz des persoenlichen Lebens erhoben; es hat in den letzten Wochen mit bescheidener Selbstverstaendlichkeit die Not im Volke zu lindern gesucht; und es hat inmitten eigener materieller Sorgen und Kuemmernisse doch nicht versaeumt, seine seelischen Erlebniskraefte zu staerken und heimatliche und Heimatkultur zu pflegen.
>
> Deutsche Jugend! Erhalte und staerke in dir Gemeinsinn und Idealismus! Das deutsche Volk kann nur leben und eine geachtete Stellung unter den Voelkern einnehmen, wenn es seine Geschichte in einer freien und friedlichen Demokratie selbst bestimmt. Diese Selbstbestimmung wird aber nur dann lebendige und dauernde Kraft des ganzen Volkes werden, wenn die junge Generation die dafuer noetigen Eigenschaften durch die deutsche Jugendbewegung in sich herausbildet: gesunden Nationalstolz, staatsbuergerliche Gesinnung, das Bewußtsein der Verantwortung vor der Gemeinschaft, soziales Mitempfinden und nicht zuletzt die Achtung vor der Weltanschauung und religioesen Lebensueberzeugung andersdenkender Volksgenossen.
>
> Deutsche Jugend! Wenn du in solcher Gesinnung und mit solchem Wollen die Schwelle des neuen Jahres ueberschreitest, wirst du selber am meisten mit dazu beitragen, daß aus den duesteren Jahren der Vergangenheit und aus der Schwere der Gegenwart heraus der Weg des deutschen Volkes allmaehlich wieder aufwaerts fuehrt zu freudiger Arbeit in einem geeinten und freien Deutschland."

**Key international-relations passage (emphasis added):**
> "Das deutsche Volk kann nur leben und **eine geachtete Stellung unter den Voelkern einnehmen**, wenn es seine Geschichte in einer freien und friedlichen Demokratie selbst bestimmt."

Translation: "The German people can only live and **take an honored place among the nations** if it determines its own history in a free and peaceful democracy."

**Scholarly note:** This is a genuine primary source with a traceable publication chain. However, three caveats:
1. The address is to *youth*, not to the electorate or the Reichstag. Its primary frame is moral-pedagogical, not foreign-policy.
2. While it contains the democracy-international-standing link, it is a domestic exhortation, not a foreign-policy manifesto.
3. The historical context is critical: Germany in January 1924 was still under the Versailles restrictions, the Ruhr occupation was ongoing (though the passive resistance had been abandoned), and the hyperinflation had just been stabilized by the Rentenmark (November 1923). The Dawes Plan was still being negotiated.

**Use with confidence, but contextualize as a youth address, not a foreign-policy speech.**

## Claim 4: "Old international order collapsed"
### Supposed source: Ebert, SPD Parteitag, Weimar, June 1919

**Verdict: PARTIALLY CONFIRMED -- the party congress protocol is real and accessible, but the specific phrasing is likely a scholarly English paraphrase.**

**Search method:** Searched FES archives for SPD party congress protocols, UPenn serial listings, HathiTrust, and H-Soz-Kult reviews. Searched for German equivalents of "old international order collapsed" ("Ordnung," "zusammenbrach," "zusammenbricht," "international," "alte Ordnung") in the 1919 protocol context.

**Authenticated provenance of the protocol:**
The full title is *Protokoll ueber die Verhandlungen des Parteitages der SPD, abgehalten in Weimar vom 10. bis 15. Juni 1919* (Berlin: Vorwaerts Paul Singer, 1919). It also includes the *Bericht ueber die 7. Frauenkonferenz abgehalten in Weimar am 15. und 16. Juni 1919*. The protocol is held in digitized form at the Friedrich-Ebert-Stiftung (FES) in Bonn, as part of their collection of SPD party congress reports from 1890-1931. HathiTrust and the Internet Archive also hold copies.

**Historical context of the congress:**
The SPD met in Weimar during the same period the National Assembly was sitting there (Weimar was chosen over Berlin for safety reasons). The dominant foreign-policy issue was the Versailles Treaty: the German government was under immense Allied pressure to sign, and the SPD was deeply divided. The government ultimately signed on 28 June 1919 -- just two weeks after the Parteitag ended.

**What Ebert's 1917 speech (an earlier, related source) contains:**
In a 1917 SPD Parteitag speech (held at the FES archive and cited in research), Ebert said:
> "Die Schrecken des Krieges, seine Opfer und Leiden, der Wiederaufbau des Zerstoerten werden gewaltige Antriebe zur Politik der Voelkerverstaendigung sein."
> (The horrors of the war, its sacrifices and sufferings, the reconstruction of what was destroyed will be powerful drivers toward a policy of international understanding.)

**On the specific phrasing:**
No direct German original of "the old international order collapsed" has been found in the indexed search results. The phrase reads as a modern English scholarly gloss summarizing what Ebert *argued* (i.e., that the war and the Versailles settlement had destroyed the pre-1914 system) rather than verbatim from the protocol. The "old international order" is an IR-historiographical concept closely associated with later scholarship (e.g., the "Westphalian system" literature, E.H. Carr's *Twenty Years' Crisis*, or the post-WWI "Wilsonian moment" scholarship).

**To verify the exact wording:** The FES digitized protocol should be consulted directly (via the FES library portal or HathiTrust). Search the protocol for Ebert's major speech, which would appear in the "Verhandlungen" (proceedings) section, not the administrative reports.

**Flag: SCHOLARLY GLOSS. Use as characterization, not as direct quote, until exact wording is retrieved from the protocol.**

## Claim 5: Ebert-Stresemann conversation, early 1925, on the League of Nations
### Supposed source: Stresemann's diaries (Vermaechtnis), as cited via Kolb

**Verdict: CORROBORATED -- the conversation window is real and documented in Stresemann's diaries; the source chain is correctly identified; but the conversation's context is narrower than sometimes claimed.**

**Search method:** Searched Stresemann's *Vermaechtnis* (Bernhard ed.), Sutton's English translation, Kolb's citations, NYT reviews of Sutton's edition, and the broader 1925 diplomatic chronology.

**The source chain is now precisely identified:**

1. **Primary (original):** Stresemann's handwritten diary, entry from January or February 1925. Stresemann kept detailed diaries throughout his political career.
2. **Primary (German edition):** Henry Bernhard (ed.), *Gustav Stresemann: Vermaechtnis. Der Nachlass in drei Baenden* (Berlin: Ullstein, 1932-1934). The relevant entry is in Volume 2, which covers the early-mid 1920s.
3. **Primary (English translation):** Eric Sutton (ed. and trans.), *Gustav Stresemann: His Diaries, Letters, and Papers*, Volume II (New York: Macmillan, 1937). The subtitle is "Locarno and Geneva." This volume covers the period from the beginning of 1925 to September 10, 1926 (the day Germany was admitted to the League of Nations). The volume records that Ebert and Stresemann were "on the friendliest terms" and that Ebert possessed "collected, well-informed, and steady judgment" on foreign-political questions.
4. **Secondary (citation via Kolb):** Eberhard Kolb, *The Weimar Republic* (English ed., Routledge, 2005), or the German original *Die Weimarer Republik* (Oldenbourg Grundriss), cites the Stresemann diary entry. Kolb's textbook is the standard English-language reference for Weimar history and the most plausible route by which this conversation entered Anglophone scholarship.

**Critical chronological correction:**
Ebert died on **28 February 1925**. This means:
- Any Ebert-Stresemann conversation in 1925 occurred in January or at most the first three weeks of February -- a window of less than 8 weeks.
- The major diplomatic activity on the League of Nations question occurred *after Ebert's death*:
  - Stresemann's 9 February 1925 memorandum on security (the Locarno precursor) was drafted while Ebert was alive but ill.
  - On 20 March 1925, *acting* President Simons wrote to the Chancellor about discussions on Germany's League of Nations membership.
  - The detailed diplomatic negotiations with Brockdorff-Rantzau (German ambassador to Moscow), Krestinsky, and Litvinov occurred in March-June 1925.
  - Germany was formally admitted to the League on 10 September 1926 (18 months after Ebert died).
- This means the conversation -- if it specifically concerned the League -- was necessarily about *preliminary strategic orientation*, not about the Locarno negotiations, the Soviet reaction, or the detailed terms of entry.

**What the Sutton volume confirms about the Ebert-Stresemann relationship:**
- They were on "the friendliest terms" (Sutton's phrasing).
- Ebert's death (28 February 1925) is recorded in the diary with Stresemann's reflection.
- Ebert died "worn out by the cares of office and relentless attacks from political enemies."

**What is NOT confirmed:**
- The specific *content* of the conversation -- whether it addressed the League of Nations specifically, or whether the discussion was broader (general foreign-policy strategy, the security question, the succession crisis that Ebert's health foreshadowed).
- Whether Kolb cites *this specific conversation* or cites the diary entry about Ebert's *death* for Stresemann's overall assessment of Ebert's foreign-policy judgment.

**Flag: The conversation almost certainly existed; the source chain is correctly identified. But the content has been triple-filtered (Stresemann's selective recording > Bernhard's editorial selection > Kolb's interpretive citation). The exact wording should be checked against Sutton Volume II or Bernhard Volume 2 before quoting.**

## Claim 6: Ebert as "Vernunftrepublikaner" in foreign policy
### Supposed source: Eberhard Kolb's characterization

**Verdict: NEEDS RECATEGORIZATION -- this is the most important correction in the report. Kolb does NOT characterize Ebert personally as a "Vernunftrepublikaner," because the term has a specific historiographical meaning that excludes Ebert. HOWEVER, Ebert's foreign-policy *style* is characterized as pragmatic and rational in ways that secondary sources may label "vernunftrepublikanisch" as an adjective.**

**Search method:** Searched the FES archives, the Weimar-research scholarly literature (Weimarforschung Jena, NASG, H-Soz-Kult), H-Net reviews of Muehlhausen (2006), the Meinecke coinage context, and Kolb's available texts for "Vernunftrepublikaner" + Ebert.

### The Term's Precise Meaning and Origin:

The term "Vernunftrepublikaner" was coined by the historian **Friedrich Meinecke** in 1919:

> *"Ich bleibe, der Vergangenheit zugewandt, Herzensmonarchist und werde, der Zukunft zugewandt, Vernunftrepublikaner."*
> (I remain, facing the past, a monarchist of the heart and shall become, facing the future, a republican of reason.)

This describes someone who:
- Was emotionally attached to the monarchy (a "Herzensmonarchist")
- BUT accepted the Weimar Republic as a rational necessity -- not from inner conviction, but from pragmatic calculation
- Had "sich mit der Republik als einer einstweilen unabanderlichen Tatsache abgefunden" (accommodated oneself to the Republic as a temporarily unalterable fact)

The term is *always* applied to **bourgeois-conservative** figures -- monarchists who made their peace with the Republic:
- **Ernst Troeltsch** (theologian/philosopher): argued that democracy "does not really suit us Germans, but we have nothing better"
- **Thomas Mann**: transformed from the author of *Betrachtungen eines Unpolitischen* (1918) to a Vernunftrepublikaner in his 1922 speech *Von deutscher Republik*
- **Hugo Preuss** (father of the Weimar Constitution): argued from "inner necessity and the teaching of experience," not from "doctrinaire infatuation" with parliamentarism
- **Gustav Stresemann** (DVP): a monarchist who became the Weimar Republic's foreign minister and a de facto Vernunftrepublikaner
- **Portions of the DNVP**: became Vernunftrepublikaner only *after* Hindenburg's election in 1925, which symbolized "ein Stueck Monarchie innerhalb der Republik" (a piece of monarchy within the Republic)

The term does NOT apply to those who were *convinced democrats* from the beginning, including Friedrich Ebert (SPD), Philipp Scheidemann (SPD), and other republican founders.

### Why Ebert Was Not a Vernunftrepublikaner:

Friedrich Ebert was:
- A founding father of the Republic (not someone who "accommodated himself" to it)
- An SPD Social Democrat and lifelong democrat (not a former monarchist)
- Someone who staked his presidency -- and ultimately his life -- on defending the Republic against enemies on both the far right and far left
- Described by Muehlhausen (2006) as a man whose entire political project was the "Primat der parlamentarischen Demokratie" (primacy of parliamentary democracy)

### Ebert's Actual Relationship to the Vernunftrepublikaner Concept:

The H-Net and sehepunkte reviews of Muehlhausen's definitive biography (2006) clarify Ebert's role: he was the **strategic integrator** whose goal was to **turn bourgeois conservatives INTO Vernunftrepublikaner**. His integration strategy included:
- Making the *Deutschlandlied* the national anthem (1922)
- A flag ordinance (1921) that accommodated the old imperial colors alongside the republican black-red-gold
- Cultivating Stresemann's DVP (German People's Party, a conservative-liberal party with monarchist roots)
- Using foreign-policy leverage -- especially the Dawes Plan (1924) and the USA orientation -- to force the DNVP (German National People's Party, right-wing conservative) into a Vernunftrepublikaner position
- Setting the condition that the DNVP could only enter government if they accepted the foreign-policy fundamentals (reparations, Western orientation)

Wolfram Pyta's research (published in Kolb-edited volumes, archived at FES) emphasizes that Ebert deployed his presidential powers -- especially in foreign policy -- precisely to *compel* the conservative right toward Vernunftrepublikanismus.

### What is TRUE about Kolb and Ebert's Foreign-Policy Characterization:

Kolb and the broader Weimar scholarship characterize Ebert's foreign policy as:
- **Pragmatic and rational** (nuechtern, pragmatisch -- not ideological or dogmatic)
- **Pro-Western** -- oriented toward the USA, Britain, and France, not Soviet Russia
- **Anti-Bolshevik** -- Ebert was "critical to hostile toward rapprochement with Soviet Russia," saw the Rapallo Treaty (1922) as a personal "Schlag ins Gesicht" (slap in the face) and feared it would lose Germany "credit in the West"
- **Focused on reparations** as the key to unlocking Germany's international reintegration -- the Dawes Plan (1924) was his major foreign-policy achievement
- **Using the presidency's foreign-policy powers actively** -- ambassadorial appointments (Adolf Koester, Otto Landsberg, Brockdorff-Rantzau in Moscow), cabinet-level foreign-policy intervention, and the threat of Reichstag dissolution to secure reparations votes

This pragmatic, anti-ideological, Western-oriented foreign policy *style* is what some secondary sources may collapse into the label "vernunftrepublikanisch" -- but the collapse is imprecise and misleading.

### The Kolb-Muehlhausen Tension:

The search results reveal a historiographical nuance worth noting:
- Muehlhausen (2006) treats Ebert's foreign policy "fast lakonisch" (almost laconically -- Elz in sehepunkte). Ebert's außenpolitisches engagement is described as receiving surprisingly brief treatment, with foreign policy "fast nur unter innenpolitischen Gesichtspunkten" (almost only from the perspective of domestic politics) except for Ebert's anti-Bolshevism.
- This suggests that Kolb, writing a general textbook rather than a detailed biography, may offer a *different* characterization of Ebert's foreign policy than Muehlhausen does -- and secondary sources citing "Kolb's characterization" need to be checked against Kolb's actual text.

**Flag: DO NOT call Ebert a "Vernunftrepublikaner" unless specifically meaning his pragmatic policy *style* (not his identity or regime-loyalty). If your source attributes the label to him, check whether the source (a) is Kolb or someone summarizing Kolb, (b) uses the term as an adjective describing policy rather than as a noun describing identity, and (c) understands the Meinecke origin of the term.**

## Cross-Cutting Observations

### 1. The English-Language Secondary-Source Problem
Claims 1, 2, 4, and 6 all show signs of English-language secondary sources smoothing Ebert's language into a more modern and idiomatic register. Ebert's actual German is more concrete, less conceptually abstract, and more embedded in the political struggles of his moment. When working with English quotations attributed to Ebert, always ask: "What is the German original, and where is it published?"

### 2. The Vernunftrepublikaner Correction Is the Most Significant Finding
Calling Ebert a "Vernunftrepublikaner" fundamentally misunderstands his political identity. The term describes those who were NOT founders of the Republic but who learned to *accept* it. Ebert was a founder and a convinced democrat. This correction matters not only for accuracy but because it changes how his foreign-policy motivations are understood: he was not a calculating pragmatist reluctantly accepting the Republic's foreign-policy constraints, but a committed democratic statesman who believed Germany's international rehabilitation depended on democratic legitimacy.

### 3. Three-Verification Triangulation
For Ebert's speeches: Muehlhausen (2017) critical edition + FES digital archive + the original periodical or protocol. If a quotation can't be located in at least one of these three, treat it as a paraphrase.

### 4. The Triple-Filter Problem (Claim 5)
The Ebert-Stresemann conversation passes through three filters: (1) Stresemann writing his diary from his own perspective and interests; (2) Bernhard editing the diaries for publication in the politically charged atmosphere of 1932-1934; (3) Kolb selecting and interpreting the passage for his textbook. The original diary entry, checked against the published versions, would be ideal.

### 5. Wolfram Pyta's Research as Bridge Literature
Pyta's essays on Ebert as Reichspraesident (published in Kolb-edited volumes and accessible through the FES archive) bridge the Kolb and Muehlhausen corpuses. His work on Ebert's foreign-policy leverage, the Rapallo crisis, and the Dawes Plan strategy is more detailed than Kolb's textbook treatment while being more focused than Muehlhausen's comprehensive biography. For English-language readers seeking more detail than Kolb's survey but less than Muehlhausen's 1064 pages, Pyta is the key source.

### Claim A: Ebert articulated a vision of "limited sovereignty" compatible with international cooperation

**Search method:** Searched for "beschraenkte Souveraenitaet" and "limited sovereignty" in connection with Ebert, Weimar, and Versailles across FES archives, De Gruyter, Vierteljahrshefte fuer Zeitgeschichte, and broader academic sources.

**What the sources show:**

1. The concept of "limited sovereignty" (*beschraenkte Souveraenitaet*) was a *structural reality* of the Weimar Republic -- not a normative vision Ebert advocated. The Versailles Treaty deprived Germany of territorial integrity (Rhineland demilitarized, Saar under League administration, Danzig a Free City), military autonomy (100,000-man army, no air force, no submarines), and economic self-determination (reparations overseen by the Reparations Commission).

2. A key contemporaneous source from *Weimar: A Jurisprudence of Crisis* states:
   > "The German Reich has at present been altogether deprived of a large part of its political substance, because crucial political issues are being dealt with from the outside... The Reich has not yet regained complete sovereignty, and this lack of political substance would relativize the importance of any constitution."

   This is a *diagnosis* of Germany's condition, not a prescriptive vision Ebert promoted.

3. Ebert's own language about sovereignty consistently used "Selbstbestimmungsrecht" (right to self-determination) and "Gleichberechtigung" (equal rights) -- he argued for Germany's *reintegration* into international society on equal terms, not for a normative theory of limited sovereignty. His February 1919 address stated:
   > "Nur auf das freie Selbstbestimmungsrecht wollen wir unseren Staat gruenden, nach innen und nach aussen."
   > (Only on the free right of self-determination do we want to found our state, internally and externally.)

4. The concept of sovereignty as "compatible with international cooperation" -- in the sense of pooled or shared sovereignty within a League of Nations framework -- is more characteristic of Stresemann's Locarno-era foreign policy (1925-1929) than of Ebert's presidency (1919-1925). Ebert's focus was on *recovering* sovereignty from Versailles, not on theorizing its limitation.

5. The Weimar Constitution (Article 4) did incorporate international law principles -- "the generally recognized rules of international law are binding constituent parts of German Reich law" -- which Hugo Preuss advocated as a conscious break with Wilhelmine nationalism. Ebert supported this constitutional framework, but Preuss was the intellectual architect.

**Verdict: CLAIM REQUIRES NUANCE.** Ebert operated within a condition of limited sovereignty and endorsed constitutional provisions binding Germany to international law. But he did not articulate a *normative theory* of limited sovereignty as a positive good. The claim may conflate (a) Ebert's pragmatic acceptance of international constraints, (b) Preuss's constitutional internationalism, and (c) Stresemann's later Locarno multilateralism.

### Claim B: Ebert argued Versailles must be revised through diplomatic means, not war

**Search method:** Searched for "Revisionspolitik," "friedliche Revision," "diplomatic means," and related terms in connection with Ebert's stated positions.

**What the sources show:**

1. This claim is broadly CORRECT but requires temporal and attributional precision.

2. Ebert's position on Versailles shifted across three phases:
   - **Phase 1 (May-June 1919):** He fiercely opposed the terms. In a May 14, 1919 *Vorwaerts* article, he called them "unreconcilable with conscience and reason" and demanded they be "drastically and fundamentally corrected." In a Lustgarten speech on May 23, 1919, he said Germany would "never sign the peace terms."
   - **Phase 2 (Post-signature, July 1919 onward):** After reluctantly signing, he issued a proclamation on "fulfillment" (*Erfuellungspolitik*): "So far as it is fulfillable, it must be fulfilled." This was pragmatic, not principled -- the goal was to demonstrate the treaty's impossibility through good-faith compliance, then seek revision.
   - **Phase 3 (1923-1925):** His focus shifted to the Dawes Plan (1924) as the key revision mechanism -- using American economic leverage to modify the reparations regime. This was diplomatic and economic, not military.

3. The *Vierteljahrshefte fuer Zeitgeschichte* (1974, Krueger) establishes the conceptual distinction: it was under *Stresemann* (after 1923) that "peaceful revision" (*friedliche Revision*) became a systematic policy, "putting a leash on revisionist policy" (*der Revisionspolitik Zuegel anzulegen*) and rejecting violent measures.

4. Ebert's *actual* contribution to revision-through-diplomacy was:
   - Accepting the treaty rather than resuming war (over the objections of military figures who favored rejection and renewed hostilities)
   - Supporting the Dawes Plan negotiations
   - Using the threat of Reichstag dissolution to secure DNVP votes for the Dawes legislation
   - Orienting foreign policy toward the USA and Western powers rather than Soviet Russia
   - Refusing to endorse Rapallo (1922) because he feared it would "lose credit in the West"

5. The claim that Ebert "argued" for diplomatic revision in explicit programmatic terms may overstate his role. Ebert was a *practitioner* of diplomatic revision, not a theorist of it. The systematic articulation of *friedliche Revision* as doctrine is primarily Stresemann's contribution.

**Verdict: SUBSTANTIALLY CORRECT but ATTRIBUTION SHOULD BE QUALIFIED.** Ebert's policy *was* revision-through-diplomacy, and this distinguished him from both the revanchist right (who wanted violent revision) and the Erfuellung purists (who wanted literal compliance without revision). But the *explicit doctrine* of peaceful revision is more Stresemann's legacy than Ebert's. Cite Ebert's specific actions (Dawes Plan, Western orientation, Rapallo rejection) rather than attributing a general revisionism doctrine to him.

### Claim C: Ebert committed Germany to international norms, the League, and collective security

**Search method:** Searched for Ebert and "Voelkerbund" (League of Nations), "kollektive Sicherheit" (collective security), "Voelkerrecht" (international law), and related terms.

**What the sources show:**

1. This claim is CORRECT in its broad thrust but needs disaggregation of the three components.

2. **International norms (Voelkerrecht):** Ebert supported the Weimar Constitution's Article 4, which incorporated "the generally recognized rules of international law" as binding Reich law. This was a major symbolic departure from Wilhelmine Germany's instrumental approach to international law. But Ebert was not the intellectual architect -- Hugo Preuss, the constitutional drafter, was. Ebert's role was political support.

3. **League of Nations (Voelkerbund):**
   - Ebert's February 1919 address expressed hope for "a Wilson peace, to which we have a claim" and demanded that Germany "enter with equal rights into the League of Nations."
   - This was aspirational, not programmatic -- Germany was *excluded* from the League until 1926 (18 months after Ebert's death).
   - Ebert did support Germany's application for membership (the February 1925 memorandum was drafted with his knowledge), but he died before the negotiation process began in earnest.
   - Stresemann's Locarno diplomacy and Germany's September 1926 admission to the League occurred under Hindenburg's presidency.

4. **Collective security (kollektive Sicherheit):**
   - The search results consistently show that "collective security" as a formal concept belongs to the *post-WWII* period (UN Charter, Chapter VII) and the post-hoc scholarly analysis of the League's failures.
   - The League of Nations did have collective security *elements* (Articles 10, 11, 16 of the Covenant), but they were non-binding and decentralized.
   - No evidence indicates Ebert used the concept or language of "collective security" (*kollektive Sicherheit*). The term is anachronistic for 1919-1925 German political discourse.
   - Ebert's interest in the League was primarily about (a) equal membership as a mark of Germany's return to international respectability, and (b) the League as a framework for peaceful revision of Versailles -- not about collective security as a system.

5. **The SPD's League policy** did contain more developed internationalist elements (Eduard Bernstein's demand for "equally applicable international law," the Goerlitz Program of 1921 calling for a "genuine working, legal, and cultural community"), and Ebert as the SPD's leading figure was aligned with this trajectory. But the specific doctrinal content came from party intellectuals, not from Ebert's own speeches.

**Verdict: CORRECT in general orientation but OVERLY MODERN in terminology.** Ebert supported (a) international law as binding, (b) German membership in the League, and (c) a peaceful international order. But "collective security" is an anachronistic term that belongs to post-1945 discourse, not to Ebert's vocabulary. The specific language Ebert used was "Gleichberechtigung" (equal rights), "Voelkerbund" (League of Nations), "das Recht" (law/right), and "friedliche Demokratie" (peaceful democracy).

### Claim D: Ebert believed democracy was the foundation for a stable peace order

**Search method:** Searched for Ebert and "Demokratie," "Friedensordnung," "peace order," "democratic peace" across FES, academic, and primary-source databases.

**What the sources show:**

1. This claim is STRONGLY SUPPORTED by Ebert's own words, and is the most defensible of the four claims.

2. Ebert's Neujahrsaufruf (1924) is the single best primary source:
   > "Das deutsche Volk kann nur leben und eine geachtete Stellung unter den Voelkern einnehmen, wenn es seine Geschichte in einer freien und friedlichen Demokratie selbst bestimmt."
   > (The German people can only live and take an honored place among the nations if it determines its own history in a free and peaceful democracy.)

   This directly links democracy to international standing and peace.

3. His February 1919 address to the National Assembly also spoke of a shift "from Imperialism to Idealism, from world power to spiritual greatness" and the renunciation of "the principle of violence among peoples" (*Dem Gewaltprinzip zwischen den Voelkern haben wir feierlich abgesagt*).

4. The conceptual link between democracy and peace in Ebert's thought had multiple dimensions:
   - **Domestic:** A democratic Germany would not be controlled by militarist elites or industrial interests with a stake in war (the "old forces" he blamed for 1914).
   - **International:** A democratic Germany could be trusted as a partner -- a necessary precondition for equal membership in the postwar order.
   - **Normative:** Democracy embodied the values of "justice and truth" (*Reich des Rechts und der Wahrheit*) that Ebert saw as the foundation of a legitimate international order.

5. Patrick O. Cohrs's 2022 Cambridge study (*The New Atlantic Order*) specifically credits Ebert with a "forward-looking Atlanticist" vision that sought to "break with Wilhelmine power politics" and anchor a republican Germany in a "reconfigured Atlantic order." This corroborates the claim.

6. **Caveat on terminology:** The modern "democratic peace theory" (DPT) literature -- which argues that democracies do not fight each other (Rummel, Doyle, Russett) -- was developed in the 1970s-1990s and is not attributable to Ebert. Ebert's formulation was less theoretical: democratic states are more peaceful *because democratic publics bear the costs of war* and *because democratic leaders are accountable*. This is closer to Kant's *Perpetual Peace* (1795), which Ebert may have known through SPD intellectual traditions, than to modern DPT.

7. **The Keynesian parallel:** Cohrs's framework also mentions that Ebert's approach shared affinities with what we would now call "commercial peace" theory -- the Dawes Plan strategy of creating American economic interests in German recovery was intended to make peaceful revision mutually beneficial, not zero-sum.

**Verdict: STRONGLY SUPPORTED.** This is the best-attested of the four claims. The Neujahrsaufruf (1924) provides a direct, verifiable primary-source quotation linking democracy to international peace and standing. However, attribute the concept using Ebert's own language ("freie und friedliche Demokratie," "geachtete Stellung unter den Voelkern") rather than imposing modern theoretical terminology ("democratic peace theory").

## Claim C1: "Conrad proposed a Serbian war 25 times between 1 January 1913 and 1 January 1914"

**From research file (line 65-68):** Hew Strachan statistic.

**Verification:**

The claim originates from Hew Strachan's *The First World War, Volume I: To Arms* (Oxford UP, 2001). **Verdict: VERIFIED.** The statistic is from Strachan (2001) and corroborated by the broader scholarship.
