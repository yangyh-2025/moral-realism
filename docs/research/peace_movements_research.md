# Peace Movements, Internationalism, and Moral Opposition to War (1914-1950)

## Overview

This report documents the historical peace movements and anti-war forces that operated as moral constraints on state decision-making during the period 1914-1950. For an ABM model of leader decision-making in international relations, these movements represent the "moral counterforce" -- the organized expression of values that oppose the prestige/honour/national-interest logic that drove leaders like Franz Joseph to war.

---

## Part I: The Second International and the Great Betrayal (1907-1914)

### 1. Pre-War Anti-War Resolutions

The Second International (founded 1889) was the umbrella organization of the world's socialist parties. By 1914, it represented millions of organized workers across Europe. Its pre-war congresses passed increasingly strong anti-war resolutions:

**Stuttgart Congress (1907)**: Unanimously adopted a resolution against war. Crucially, an amendment by Lenin, Martov, and Rosa Luxemburg was incorporated: if war broke out, socialists must "utilise the economic and political crisis created by the war to rouse the masses and thereby to hasten the downfall of capitalist class rule."

**Copenhagen Congress (1910)**: Reaffirmed anti-war commitments; called for international arbitration of disputes.

**Basel Congress (1912)**: As the Balkan Wars signaled growing tensions, the International convened an extraordinary congress. The Basel Manifesto, adopted unanimously, warned governments that war would create a revolutionary situation. It referenced the Paris Commune (1871) and the Russian Revolution of 1905 as precedents -- threatening that war would trigger proletarian revolution. Jean Jaures declared the resolution "serves notice upon the governments... that [by war] they would easily create a revolutionary situation."

**Jaures' Assassination (31 July 1914)**: The French socialist leader Jean Jaures -- the most powerful anti-war voice in Europe -- was assassinated in a Paris cafe by a nationalist fanatic. His murder removed the one figure who might have rallied international socialist opposition to the coming war.

### 2. The Collapse (August 1914)

Within one week, the Second International disintegrated:

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| **2 August** | Belgian socialists voted to align with their government |
| **3 August** | German SPD: 14 of 92 deputies opposed war credits in closed session, but party discipline suppressed dissent |
| **4 August** | German SPD voted **unanimously** for war credits. Hugo Haase -- a war opponent -- was forced to read the party's pro-war statement. French SFIO voted unanimously the same day. British Labour, Austrian, and Hungarian socialists all followed. |

**The Justifications**

Socialist parties across Europe offered nationalist rationalizations:
- **France**: Defense of the Republic and civilization against German absolutism (union sacree)
- **Germany**: Defense of Kultur against "Asiatic barbarism" (Tsarist Russia) -- the Burgfrieden (civil truce)
- **Both sides** claimed their country was the victim of aggression

**Lenin's Verdict**: "The Second International is dead. Long live the Third International!"

**The Scale of the Betrayal**

An estimated 12 million members of socialist parties and trade unions were mobilized to kill fellow workers across trenches. Rosa Luxemburg and Clara Zetkin reportedly contemplated suicide upon hearing the news of the SPD's vote for war credits.

### 3. Why Did International Working-Class Solidarity Fail?

Several factors explain the collapse:

1. **The primacy of national identity**: National identity proved far more powerful than class identity when war came. Workers identified as Frenchmen or Germans first, workers second.

2. **The logic of defensive war**: Each party could plausibly claim its country was defending itself against aggression, making the pre-war resolutions' call to "oppose imperialist war" ambiguous in application.

3. **Institutional capture**: Socialist parties had become large, bureaucratic organizations embedded in national political systems. Party leaders had more to lose from defying their governments than from betraying internationalist rhetoric.

4. **The Russian factor**: For German socialists, the Tsarist autocracy was a genuinely reactionary force. War against Russia could be framed as progressive.

5. **The failure of imagination**: No one in the socialist leadership had seriously planned for what to do if war actually broke out. The pre-war resolutions were rhetorical gestures, not operational plans.

**Significance for the ABM Model**: The collapse of 1914 is the definitive case study of moral/internationalist conviction being overwhelmed by national loyalty. It demonstrates that ideological commitment to peace, even when organized at mass scale, is fragile in the face of perceived national threat.

## Part II: The Anti-War Minority: Zimmerwald and Revolutionary Defeatism (1915-1917)

### 1. The Zimmerwald Conference (5-8 September 1915)

Not all socialists capitulated. A minority gathered in the Swiss village of Zimmerwald.

**Attendance**: 38 delegates from 10 countries, held in secret under the guise of an "ornithological society" meeting at the Hotel Beau Sejour.

**Key figures**: Lenin, Trotsky, Zinoviev, Radek (Russian); Ledebour (German); Merrheim (French); Serrati (Italian); Grimm and Platten (Swiss hosts).

**Two factions**:
- **Majority (Centrists/Pacifists)**: Wanted to revive the Second International, coordinate anti-war agitation, and push for "peace without annexations or reparations"
- **Zimmerwald Left (Lenin's faction, ~8 delegates)**: Argued the war was an imperialist conflict to be transformed into revolutionary civil war against the capitalist class. Called for a new Third International.

**Outcome**: A compromise manifesto was adopted condemning the war as imperialist, criticizing socialist parties for supporting it, and calling for withdrawal from the Burgfrieden and a "peace without annexations and war reparations." The International Socialist Commission (ISC) was established in Bern, led by Robert Grimm.

### 2. The Kienthal Conference (24-30 April 1916)

The Second Zimmerwald Conference, held at the Hotel Baren in Kienthal.

**Key shift**: By April 1916, war fatigue had grown, and the pro-war socialist majorities had lost credibility. Lenin's Zimmerwald Left gained ground -- their resolution attracted 19 votes (nearly half the delegates), though still short of a majority.

**Outcome**: A manifesto dealt more severely with the majority socialists ("social patriots"), criticized bourgeois pacifists, and declared that "the struggle for lasting peace can only be a struggle for the realization of socialism."

The conference still rejected Lenin's call for an immediate break with the Second International, but it represented the zenith of the anti-war socialist movement.

### 3. The Stockholm Conference (September 1917)

The Third Zimmerwald Conference, held in Stockholm, proved ineffectual. Delegates from Entente countries could not obtain passports. The Russian February Revolution had fundamentally changed the political landscape. The movement dissolved by 1919, superseded by the Communist International (Comintern).

**Legacy**: The Zimmerwald movement created the political space for the Bolshevik line to gain legitimacy and foreshadowed the permanent division of the international socialist movement into social democratic and communist wings -- a schism that defined 20th-century politics.

## Part III: Interwar Peace Movements (1919-1939)

### 1. The League of Nations Union (LNU)

**Scale**: The LNU became the largest and most influential peace organization in Britain, attracting over one million members at its peak with nearly 3,000 local branches.

**Leadership**: Lord Robert Cecil (a founding father of the League of Nations, LNU president from 1923) and Philip Noel-Baker (Quaker, Olympic athlete, later Labour MP).

**Philosophy**: The LNU was "internationalist" rather than strictly pacifist. It supported the League of Nations and collective security, including the potential use of force to deter aggression. This distinguished it from absolute pacifist groups.

**Historian's Observation**: There was a significant distinction between "pacifists" (who opposed all war on principle) and "internationalists" (who supported collective security through the League). The LNU deliberately blurred this line to maximize membership, which later caused confusion and internal tension. By 1933, only 388,255 paid-up subscribers existed out of the widely publicized "one million" members.

### 2. The Oxford Union "King and Country" Debate (9 February 1933)

The Oxford Union debating society passed the motion: "That this House will under no circumstances fight for its King and Country" by 275 to 153.

This caused shock waves across Britain. It was interpreted (and exaggerated) as evidence that the rising generation of the ruling classes had embraced pacifism. Some historians argue Hitler interpreted the vote as evidence Britain lacked the will to fight, though this claim is debated.

Scholars emphasize that the resolution reflected support for the League of Nations and collective security rather than absolute pacifism, and that it has often been oversimplified in historical accounts.

### 3. The Peace Ballot (1934-1935)

**What it was**: A nationwide house-to-house survey on the League of Nations, disarmament, and collective security, organized by the LNU under Lord Cecil.

**Scale**: Over 11.5 million adults completed the ballot -- roughly half the adult population. An estimated 500,000 volunteers distributed and collected ballot forms.

**The New Statesman called it**: "The most remarkable popular referendum ever initiated and carried through by private enterprise."

**Results showed**: Overwhelming support for remaining in the League of Nations and for collective security, though more ambivalence about embracing military measures.

**Historical Interpretation**: Winston Churchill saw it as evidence of public support for firm collective security against Hitler. Critics argued it reflected naive idealism. Martin Ceadel's nuanced analysis shows it was primarily an expression of internationalist, not absolutist pacifist, sentiment.

### 4. Absolute Pacifism: The Peace Pledge Union

Alongside the internationalist LNU, a more radical absolutist pacifism flourished:

- The **Peace Pledge Union (PPU)** was founded by Canon Dick Sheppard, attracting those who renounced all war unconditionally
- The **Fellowship of Reconciliation**, the **No-Conscription Fellowship** (from WWI), and the **Union of Democratic Control** were other influential groups
- The PPU and absolute pacifists refused to cooperate with the Peace Ballot because it implied endorsement of military force through collective security

### 5. The Kellogg-Briand Pact (1928)

Signed by 62 nations, the Pact "outlawed war as an instrument of national policy." It was the high-water mark of interwar legal pacifism. Its fatal weakness: no enforcement mechanism. It relied entirely on the good faith of signatories.

### 6. The Failure of Collective Security

The League of Nations' failure to stop aggression in the 1930s is the defining tragedy of interwar internationalism:

| Crisis | Year | What Happened |
|--------|------|---------------|
| **Manchuria** | 1931 | Japan invaded. The League condemned but took months to investigate. Japan withdrew from the League and continued. |
| **Ethiopia** | 1935 | Italy invaded. Weak sanctions (no oil). Hoare-Laval Pact attempted to accommodate Italian imperialism. |
| **Rhineland** | 1936 | Hitler remilitarized. The League did nothing. |
| **Spanish Civil War** | 1936-39 | Non-intervention policy failed to stop German and Italian support for Franco. |
| **Austria / Czechoslovakia** | 1938 | Anschluss and Munich -- appeasement outside the League framework entirely. |

**Why Collective Security Failed**:

1. **No enforcement mechanism**: The League had no standing army and no reliable means of coercing great powers.
2. **National interests over collective obligations**: Britain and France -- the "pillars" of the League -- prioritized European security and domestic concerns over distant conflicts.
3. **Absence of key powers**: The USA never joined. Germany and Japan withdrew in 1933. The USSR was intermittently absent.
4. **Unanimity requirement**: Aggressor states could effectively veto resolutions against themselves.
5. **Economic crisis**: The Great Depression diverted attention and resources from international crises to domestic troubles.
6. **The domino effect**: Failure to resist Japan encouraged Mussolini; failure to stop Italy emboldened Hitler.

### 7. The Pacifism-vs-Antifascism Tension (Late 1930s)

The rise of fascism created an acute dilemma for peace movements:

- **Absolute pacifists** maintained that all war was wrong, even war against Hitler
- **Collective security advocates** argued that force against fascist aggression was morally justified and practically necessary
- Many prominent pacifists (including Bertrand Russell, who had been imprisoned for anti-WWI activism) abandoned absolute pacifism in the face of Nazi Germany
- The PPU's membership declined from its peak as fascism's threat became undeniable
- The Spanish Civil War (1936-39) was a crucial dividing line: many on the left supported the Republican side militarily, breaking with pacifist orthodoxy

This tension mirrors a core dimension of the ABM model: the conflict between the moral conviction that war is wrong and the moral conviction that fascism must be stopped.

## Part IV: Vatican Diplomacy as Moral Authority Without Military Power

### 1. Pope Benedict XV (1914-1922)

**Elected 3 September 1914**, weeks after the war began. His prior experience in papal diplomacy made him a deliberate choice for neutrality.

**Guiding principles**:
1. Complete impartiality toward all belligerents
2. Constant effort to do good without national or religious distinctions
3. Assiduous commitment to hastening the end of the conflict

**Early peace efforts (1914-1916)**:
- November 1914: First encyclical *Ad Beatissimi Apostolorum* urged belligerents to negotiate
- December 1914: Called for a Christmas truce (rejected, particularly by the British)
- 1915: Attempted to mediate between Austria-Hungary and Italy to prevent Italy entering the war; failed
- Made the poignant statement "Nations do not die" in reference to occupied Belgium
- 1916-1917: Acted as intermediary between Entente powers and the new Austrian Emperor Karl I

**The 1917 Peace Note (1 August 1917)**

On the third anniversary of the war, Benedict issued his most ambitious peace proposal -- an open letter "to the Heads of the Belligerent Peoples." The seven proposals:

1. Moral force of international law to replace material force of arms
2. Simultaneous and reciprocal disarmament
3. International arbitration of disputes, with sanctions against states refusing arbitration
4. Freedom and common use of the seas
5. Reciprocal renunciation of war reparations
6. Evacuation and restoration of all occupied territories
7. Conciliatory negotiation of rival territorial claims, taking into account "the aspirations of peoples"

The pope famously described the war as a **"useless massacre"** (*inutile strage*).

**Why it failed**:
- Germany felt militarily strong and was unwilling to negotiate
- Austria-Hungary followed Germany's lead
- President Wilson gave the note a cool reception -- decisive because the Entente was increasingly dependent on American support
- Hostile reactions on both sides: in France, Benedict was "le pape boche" (the German pope); in Protestant Germany, "der franzosische Papst"; in Italy, Mussolini called him "Maledetto XV" (Accursed XV)

**Humanitarian efforts**: Established a Vatican "Office for Prisoners" handling 600,000 items of correspondence; arranged food relief convoys to starving children in Belgium, Lithuania, Montenegro, Poland, Russia, and Syria/Lebanon; protested directly to the Ottoman Sultan regarding the Armenian Genocide.

**Legacy**: Short-term failure but long-term significance. Preserved the unity of European Catholicism. Established the modern template of the pope as neutral arbiter and impartial mediator. Diplomatic relations doubled from 14 to nearly all great powers. Prophetically criticized Versailles' punitive treatment of Germany, warning it would lead to future conflict. The content and structure of Wilson's later Fourteen Points closely resembled Benedict's Peace Note.

### 2. Pope Pius XI (1922-1939)

- Lateran Treaties (1929): Resolved the "Roman Question" with Italy, establishing Vatican City as a sovereign state
- Condemned both communism (*Divini Redemptoris*, 1937) and Nazism (*Mit brennender Sorge*, 1937)
- Relations with fascist Italy were pragmatic but tense
- Died in February 1939, on the eve of WWII

### 3. Pope Pius XII (1939-1958) and the Controversy

- Elected March 1939; former papal nuncio to Germany
- Maintained Vatican neutrality during WWII
- Controversial "silence" regarding the Holocaust -- a debate that continues among historians
- Engaged in behind-the-scenes diplomacy and humanitarian relief
- Post-war: opposed communism, supported Christian Democracy in Western Europe

## Part V: The Failure of Internationalism and Its Implications for the Model

### Why Did the Peace Movements Fail to Prevent War?

| Factor | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|
| **National identity primacy** | When national security was perceived as at stake, internationalist ideology collapsed (August 1914) |
| **Institutional weakness** | The League had moral authority but no enforcement mechanism -- it could condemn but not coerce |
| **Great power defection** | Key powers (USA, USSR, Germany, Japan) were absent or withdrew, leaving the system hollow |
| **The fascist challenge** | Nazi Germany and fascist Italy were regimes that explicitly rejected internationalist norms -- they could not be reasoned with in League terms |
| **Domestic economic pressure** | The Great Depression turned attention inward; international cooperation declined |
| **The pacifism-antifascism tension** | Peace movements were divided between absolute pacifism and collective security, weakening both |
| **Timing of death** | Key internationalist leaders died at critical moments: Jaures (1914), Rathenau (1922), Stresemann (1929), Henderson (1935) before seeing their work vindicated |

### What Conditions Made Peace Movements Effective When They Were?

1. **Post-war exhaustion**: The 1920s peace movement flourished because of genuine popular revulsion at WWI's slaughter
2. **Elite support**: Aristide Briand, Gustav Stresemann, Austen Chamberlain -- elite statesmen who believed in the Locarno project
3. **Economic prosperity**: The Dawes Plan and American loans created conditions where peaceful cooperation seemed to "work"
4. **Absence of existential threat**: Before the Great Depression and Hitler's rise, the European order seemed manageable through negotiation

### Implications for the ABM Model

The peace movements provide historical grounding for several model parameters:

1. **Moral constraint** is real but fragile -- it operates when national security is not perceived as immediately threatened
2. **Internationalist ideology** can motivate genuine state behaviour (Locarno, League membership, disarmament negotiations) but collapses under severe pressure
3. **Reputation/prestige dynamics** cut both ways: leaders fear the reputational cost of appearing weak (Franz Joseph) but also the reputational cost of appearing warlike (interwar democratic leaders)
4. **The pacifism-antifascism tension** is a model-relevant moral dilemma: two genuine moral convictions (oppose war, oppose fascism) that came into direct conflict
5. **Institutional design matters**: The League's structural weaknesses (unanimity, no enforcement) were fatal; they were not inevitable but resulted from specific design choices
