# Arthur Henderson: Labour Internationalism, War, and Peace (1914-1935)

## Overview

Arthur Henderson (1863-1935) was a co-founder of the British Labour Party who rose from poverty as an iron moulder to become one of Britain's most prominent statesmen. His career embodies the central tension in early 20th-century socialist foreign policy: how to reconcile working-class internationalism with national loyalty in time of war. He served as Home Secretary (1924) and Foreign Secretary (1929-1931), and was awarded the Nobel Peace Prize in 1934 for his leadership of the World Disarmament Conference.

---

## 1. August 1914: From Anti-War to Pro-War

**The Initial Anti-War Position**

Before August 1914, Henderson was firmly in the camp of the Second International's anti-war socialism. He had summoned a conference of trade unions and socialist organizations for early August, intended "to supply a united proletarian response to the threat of war."

**The Shift**

By the time the conference met on 5 August 1914, Britain had already declared war on Germany (4 August). The meeting "wasted no time reflecting upon its original purpose" and instead established the War Emergency Workers' National Committee to safeguard working-class interests during the conflict.

In August 1914, Henderson "with the majority of the Labour members of the Commons, expressed support for the British effort in World War I." He took over the party's parliamentary leadership from **Ramsay MacDonald**, who headed the Labourites' pacifist minority and resigned from the leadership position.

**Significance for the Model**

Henderson's August 1914 shift is a textbook case of national loyalty overriding internationalist ideology. The Second International's pre-war resolutions (Stuttgart 1907, Basel 1912) had pledged socialist parties to oppose war -- but when war came, Henderson, like his German and French counterparts, chose the nation. His case differs from some others in that he was not personally a Marxian internationalist; his socialism was rooted in Methodism and trade unionism, and his support for the war was framed in terms of defending democracy and upholding treaty obligations to Belgium.

---

## 2. Wartime Government Roles (1915-1917)

Henderson's support for the war led to his inclusion in government:

- **May 1915**: Joined H.H. Asquith's wartime coalition government as President of the Board of Education
- **Later 1915**: Became Paymaster General and government adviser on labour matters
- **December 1916**: When Lloyd George became Prime Minister, Henderson became a **minister without portfolio in the five-man War Cabinet** -- the first Labour politician to serve at this level

His presence in the War Cabinet represented a historic breakthrough for Labour -- the party of the working class was now at the center of national decision-making. But it also created an acute tension: as War Cabinet member, Henderson shared responsibility for prosecuting a war that much of the labour movement increasingly opposed.

---

## 3. The Stockholm Conference and Resignation (1917)

This is the pivotal episode in Henderson's wartime career and the clearest illustration of the tension between socialist internationalism and national war policy.

**Background**

After the February Revolution in Russia (1917), the Russian Provisional Government and the Petrograd Soviet pushed for an international socialist conference to discuss peace terms. The proposed venue was Stockholm.

**Henderson's Russia Visit**

Henderson visited Russia in mid-1917 as part of a governmental mission. On his return, he stopped in Paris for an Allied Socialist Conference where he expressed support for British labour representation at Stockholm.

**The Labour Conference Vote**

On 10 August 1917, the Labour Party Conference voted by more than 3-to-1 to send delegates to Stockholm, provided the conference was consultative and not binding on governments. Henderson spoke in favour.

**Lloyd George's Opposition**

Lloyd George and the War Cabinet opposed any British participation in Stockholm. They -- along with the French, Italian, and American governments -- believed a conference with enemy socialist representatives would undermine the war effort at a critical moment.

**The Resignation (11 August 1917)**

Henderson tendered his resignation, which Lloyd George promptly accepted. The Prime Minister's public letter was scathing:

> "Your colleagues were taken completely by surprise by the attitude you adopted at the Labour Conference."

Lloyd George accused Henderson of:
- Having led Cabinet colleagues to believe he would oppose Stockholm, only to support it at the Labour gathering
- Failing to properly relay a telegram from Kerensky indicating Russia did not ardently desire the conference
- Allowing his dual role as Labour Party secretary and War Cabinet minister to create "embarrassing complications"

**Henderson's Defence**

In a speech to the House of Commons on 14 August 1917, Henderson complained of "unfair treatment." He alleged that Cabinet had prior knowledge of his Paris trip, that he was kept waiting outside a Cabinet meeting for a full hour (effectively being sidelined), that he had not received the Kerensky telegram in time, and that had he resigned before the Labour conference, it would have inflated the pro-Stockholm vote.

**Aftermath**

- George Barnes (another Labour figure) replaced Henderson in the War Cabinet
- The British government refused passports to would-be Stockholm delegates
- The Stockholm conference went ahead in diminished form but achieved nothing
- German newspapers gleefully but wrongly predicted the resignation signalled the "downfall of the Lloyd George Cabinet"

**Significance for the Model**

The Stockholm episode reveals the structural impossibility of serving two masters: the demands of national wartime cabinet solidarity and the demands of international socialist solidarity. When forced to choose, Henderson chose the labour movement over the government -- but only after months of trying to bridge the two. His resignation was not over the war itself (he continued to support the war effort) but over the right of workers' representatives to meet across battle lines to discuss peace.

## 4. League of Nations and Disarmament (1919-1935)

**Foreign Secretary (1929-1931)**

As Foreign Secretary in Ramsay MacDonald's second Labour government, Henderson was an outspoken advocate for the League of Nations. He was largely responsible for bringing the World Disarmament Conference into existence.

**World Disarmament Conference Chairman (1932-1935)**

Elected as president of the Geneva Disarmament Conference, Henderson worked tirelessly to keep negotiations alive despite strong opposition from major powers -- including, at times, his own government.

**Nobel Peace Prize (1934)**

Henderson was awarded the Nobel Peace Prize in 1934 "for his untiring struggle and his courageous efforts as Chairman of the League of Nations Disarmament Conference 1931-34."

In his Nobel Lecture on 11 December 1934 ("Essential Elements of a Universal and Enduring Peace"), he stated:

> "There can be no real disarmament except on the basis of the collective peace system of the League of Nations."

He warned of a "gigantic paradox" where nations were preparing for war despite being pledged to peace, and described the new arms race emerging among the very nations that had founded the Disarmament Conference.

**The Failure**

Despite Henderson's efforts, the disarmament negotiations collapsed. The major powers were unwilling to relinquish national control over armed forces. In autumn 1933, Hitler withdrew Germany from the League of Nations. Two years later, Germany violated Versailles by introducing compulsory military service.

Henderson died in October 1935, unaware that the Italo-Ethiopian conflict had already shattered his dream. As one colleague put it: "I wish his passing might induce statesmen of the world, even at this late hour, to call a halt in the mad race of armaments."

---

## 5. Views on Rearmament and Fascism (1930s)

Henderson's position on rearmament was complex:

- He remained committed to collective security through the League, not unilateral British rearmament
- He opposed the pacifist absolutism of the Peace Pledge Union -- he believed in the legitimacy of force under League authority
- He saw German rearmament under Hitler as a direct threat to the international order he had spent his career trying to build
- Yet his primary framework remained disarmament-by-agreement, not rearmament-for-deterrence
- The Labour Party under his influence opposed the National Government's limited rearmament program in the early 1930s, a position that would be reversed only after his death and under the pressure of events

---

## 6. Analysis: The Tension Between National Interest, Internationalism, and Peace

Henderson's career illustrates three distinct modes of managing the national/international tension:

| Period | Mode | Description |
|--------|------|-------------|
| **Aug 1914** | National capture | Internationalist ideology yields to national war effort, rationalized as defense of democracy |
| **1917 (Stockholm)** | Attempted synthesis | Seeks to reconcile cabinet loyalty with labour internationalism; fails, chooses labour movement |
| **1929-1935** | Institutional internationalism | Channels internationalism through League institutions; genuine conviction that collective security can replace balance-of-power politics |

His Nobel Prize represented recognition not of success (his disarmament conference failed) but of persistent, courageous commitment to an ideal -- the model of a politician who kept faith with internationalist principles even as the international order collapsed around him.
