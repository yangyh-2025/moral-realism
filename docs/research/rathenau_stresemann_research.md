# Walther Rathenau and Gustav Stresemann: German Foreign Policy Between Nationalism and Internationalism (1914-1929)

## Part I: Walther Rathenau (1867-1922)

### Overview

Walther Rathenau was a German-Jewish industrialist (head of the AEG electrical conglomerate), writer, philosopher, and statesman. He served as Foreign Minister of the Weimar Republic in 1922, the first Jew to hold a German cabinet post. His life and death embody the tragic tensions of early 20th-century Germany: liberal internationalist and German patriot, Jew and German nationalist, industrial capitalist and advocate of worker participation, architect of Germany's WWI war economy and architect of its post-war diplomatic rehabilitation.

---

### 1. The War Economy: Organizing German Industry for Total War (1914-1915)

**The Genesis of the KRA (Kriegsrohstoffabteilung)**

Three days after Britain declared war (4 August 1914), Rathenau grew deeply anxious about Germany's supply situation. Britain's naval blockade had transformed Germany into what Rathenau called a "besieged fortress."

On 8 August 1914, he met with Colonel Scheuch at the War Ministry. The following day, War Minister Erich von Falkenhayn summoned him and unexpectedly asked Rathenau to lead a new organization for managing raw materials. The **Kriegsrohstoffabteilung (War Raw Materials Department, or KRA)** was formally established by ministerial decree on 13 August 1914.

**Methods**

Rathenau pursued four strategies:
1. **Compulsory control** of all domestic raw materials -- nothing diverted to non-essential uses
2. **Procurement from abroad** -- purchases in neutral countries, requisitioning in occupied territories (Belgium, northern France)
3. **Expansion of domestic production** -- developing new manufacturing methods
4. **Substitution (Ersatz)** -- replacing scarce materials with alternatives

Within a fortnight, his team assembled national coverage statistics. Starting with fewer than a dozen critical materials, the list grew to over one hundred. The KRA expanded from five people in four small rooms to over 500 staff occupying entire streets of office buildings, with some 36 auxiliary bodies (Kriegsrohstoff-Gesellschaften) organized by material type.

**Significance**

Rathenau characterized his work as an unprecedented experiment that "touches closely on the methods of socialism and communism" -- not in theoretical form, but as a pragmatic "Gemeinwirtschaft" (mixed economy) blending free market and state control. A Jewish industrialist and intellectual became the single individual most responsible for keeping the German war machine supplied with materials. This contradiction -- the Jewish patriot organizing Germany's war economy while increasingly doubting the war's purpose -- defines Rathenau's wartime experience.

---

### 2. Views on the War and War Aims

Rathenau was not a militarist. His views on the war exhibit a profound ambivalence:

- **Strategic pessimism**: He described Germany's geographic position in stark terms -- three "oceans" that were really "inland lakes" (Baltic, North Sea, Mediterranean), all controllable by Britain. Germany was a "needy country with a low supply of essential materials."

- **Economic cost**: In his December 1916 lecture "Probleme der Friedenswirtschaft" (Problems of the Peace-Time Economy), he calculated the war was destroying up to one-fifth of national wealth per year, causing massive wealth redistribution and social upheaval.

- **War aims**: He did not advocate annexationist war aims of the kind championed by the Pan-German League. His vision was of a Germany that could trade freely and compete economically, not one that conquered territory.

- **1918 desperation**: As the Western Front collapsed, Rathenau proposed a desperate levée en masse ("call to arms") to turn defeat into victory -- suggesting his patriotism, however complex, remained engaged to the end.

---

### 3. Political Philosophy

Rathenau's writings reveal a complex, often paradoxical thinker:

- **Economic philosophy**: In *Die neue Wirtschaft* (1918, "The New Economy"), he proposed a "decentralized, democratic social order" with greater worker participation in management (a precursor to modern co-determination), industrial self-government, and effective state oversight -- but not wholesale nationalization. He believed laissez-faire capitalism was obsolete but rejected both state socialism and Marxism.

- **Critique of mechanization**: He warned that technological change was pushing civilization toward extreme mechanization where "the human soul would be lost" -- a theme in *In Days to Come* (1921) and *The New Society* (1921).

- **Jewish identity**: In his controversial 1897 essay *Hore, Israel!* ("Hear, O Israel!"), he called for the radical assimilation of German Jews, arguing they must consciously "cast off" tribal qualities "demonstrably hateful to fellow Germans." He later moderated these views. His famous self-description captures his dilemma: "I am a German of Jewish origin. My people are the German people, my homeland is Germany, my faith is German faith, which stands above denominations."

- **On Bolshevism**: Despite later signing the Rapallo Treaty with Soviet Russia, he was deeply skeptical of Bolshevism, writing that the Soviet idea was one of "compulsory happiness" comparable to the Inquisition, willing to sacrifice "ten million people" to destroy the bourgeoisie.

---

### 4. Foreign Minister and the Treaty of Rapallo (1922)

**Fulfillment Policy**

Rathenau advocated fulfilling Treaty of Versailles obligations in good faith -- not as capitulation, but as a strategy to demonstrate their impossibility and win revision through negotiation. This "Erfullungspolitik" was the foundation of Weimar foreign policy under both Rathenau and his successor Stresemann.

**The Rapallo Treaty (16 April 1922)**

Rathenau's greatest diplomatic achievement -- and the act that sealed his fate -- was the Treaty of Rapallo with Soviet Russia, signed on the sidelines of the Genoa Conference. The treaty:
- Normalized diplomatic relations between Germany and the USSR (broken since 1918)
- Strengthened economic ties and renounced mutual territorial and financial claims from WWI
- Broke Germany's postwar diplomatic isolation -- the first time Germany had asserted itself independently in international affairs
- Infuriated the Western Allies (especially France), who saw it as upsetting the European balance of power

The treaty was deeply controversial domestically. Right-wing nationalists viewed it as collaboration with Bolshevik communism, reinforcing false claims of a "Jewish-communist conspiracy."

---

### 5. Assassination (24 June 1922)

Less than six months into his tenure as foreign minister, Rathenau was murdered.

**The Attack**: Members of the Organisation Consul, an ultra-nationalist, antisemitic paramilitary terrorist group led by former naval officer Hermann Ehrhardt, ambushed his car in Berlin's Grunewald district. Erwin Kern fired a submachine gun at close range; Hermann Fischer threw a hand grenade.

**The Motive**: The assassins believed Rathenau was literally one of the "Elders of Zion" from the antisemitic forgery *The Protocols of the Elders of Zion*. They sought to provoke a civil war to topple the Weimar Republic and establish a military dictatorship.

**Aftermath**: Millions took to the streets in grief. Chancellor Joseph Wirth famously declared in the Reichstag: "The enemy is on the right!" The assassination prompted the Law for the Protection of the Republic (July 1922), establishing special courts for political violence. The Nazis later reversed this legacy -- they destroyed Rathenau memorials and erected a monument to his murderers in 1933.

---

### 6. The Central Tension

Rathenau embodied a specific tragic paradox of early 20th-century Germany:

| Identity | Manifestation |
|----------|---------------|
| **German patriot** | Organized the war economy in 1914-15, proposed levée en masse in 1918 |
| **Liberal internationalist** | Advocated fulfillment of Versailles, negotiated Germany's diplomatic rehabilitation |
| **Jewish German** | Insisted on his German identity in an environment of rising antisemitism |
| **Industrial capitalist** | Head of AEG, one of Europe's largest corporations |
| **Social reformer** | Advocated worker participation, criticized laissez-faire capitalism |
| **Martyr for democracy** | Murdered by the far right, mourned by millions, demonized by the Nazis |

He was killed not for any specific policy but for what he represented: a Germany that could be both national and liberal, both powerful and cooperative, both German and Jewish.

## Part II: Gustav Stresemann (1878-1929)

### Overview

Gustav Stresemann served as Germany's Foreign Minister from 1923 until his death in 1929. He was jointly awarded the Nobel Peace Prize in 1926 alongside French Foreign Minister Aristide Briand. His policy of Erfullungspolitik ("fulfillment policy") was the most successful period of Weimar diplomacy.

---

### 1. From Nationalist to European Statesman

Stresemann began his political career as a monarchist and annexationist during WWI. He supported unrestricted submarine warfare and opposed the Treaty of Versailles. His transformation from nationalist hardliner to great European conciliator is one of the most significant political evolutions of the interwar period.

The key shift came with the Ruhr Crisis (1923). France and Belgium occupied the Ruhr industrial region to enforce reparations. German passive resistance collapsed, hyperinflation destroyed the currency, and Stresemann -- briefly Chancellor in 1923 -- recognized that confrontation could only destroy Germany. From this point, he pursued accommodation.

---

### 2. The Fulfillment Policy (Erfullungspolitik)

Stresemann's strategy: comply with Versailles obligations not as submission but as a tactical investment in trust, creating conditions for peaceful revision of the treaty's harshest terms.

Key achievements:

| Year | Achievement | Significance |
|------|------------|--------------|
| **1924** | Dawes Plan | U.S.-brokered reparations restructuring; American loans stabilized the German economy |
| **1925** | Locarno Treaties | Germany voluntarily accepted its western borders (while leaving eastern borders open to revision) |
| **1926** | League of Nations membership | Germany admitted with a permanent Council seat -- restored to great-power status |
| **1926** | Treaty of Berlin with USSR | Mutual neutrality agreement; maintained the Rapallo relationship while reassuring the West |
| **1928** | Kellogg-Briand Pact | Germany joined the pact outlawing war as an instrument of national policy |
| **1929** | Young Plan | Further reduced reparations; Allied promise to evacuate the Rhineland by 1930 (5 years early) |

---

### 3. The Locarno Strategy

The Locarno Treaties (1925) were Stresemann's masterstroke:

- Germany, France, and Belgium mutually guaranteed their existing western borders
- Britain and Italy acted as guarantors
- Germany signed arbitration conventions with France, Belgium, Poland, and Czechoslovakia
- Crucially, Germany did **not** recognize its eastern borders as permanent -- Stresemann's deliberate design to leave open peaceful revision regarding Danzig, the Polish Corridor, and Upper Silesia

Stresemann himself described Locarno as having "ended the policy of opposition and conflicting aims."

---

### 4. Peaceful Revisionism

Stresemann's ultimate aims extended beyond restoring Germany's pre-war status:

- Evacuation of the Rhineland (achieved by 1930)
- **Anschluss with Austria** (union of German-speaking peoples)
- Revision of eastern borders -- return of Danzig, the Polish Corridor, and Upper Silesia from Poland
- Restoration of Germany's position as a European great power

Crucially, Stresemann sought these changes **through negotiation, not war**. He believed economic pressure and diplomatic leverage could achieve territorial revisions peacefully. As scholarship notes: "Stresemann did not anticipate the use of force or the revival of Germany's extreme war aims."

His private papers (discovered in the 1950s) revealed a more committed nationalist than his public persona suggested -- but one who genuinely believed peaceful methods were the only viable path. He was a "pragmatic conservative" who used accommodation as a tactical tool to dismantle Versailles piece by piece.

---

### 5. Death and Legacy

Stresemann died suddenly on 3 October 1929, just weeks before the Wall Street Crash. His death removed the one German statesman who might have navigated the coming storms of the Great Depression. Without him, Weimar foreign policy shifted toward more confrontational, unilateral approaches under Bruning, Papen, and Schleicher -- anticipating the policies of the Third Reich.

**Historical Debate**

| View | Characterization |
|------|-----------------|
| Traditional (post-1945) | Stresemann as "good European," champion of reconciliation, foreshadowing European integration |
| Revisionist (1950s+) | Flexible but committed nationalist; accommodation as tactical tool to dismantle Versailles |
| Modern consensus | Pragmatic conservative; genuinely believed in peaceful methods but pursued traditional German nationalist goals |

## Part III: Comparative Analysis for the ABM Model

The Rathenau-Stresemann comparison reveals distinct paths within German internationalism:

| Dimension | Rathenau | Stresemann |
|-----------|----------|------------|
| **Starting point** | Liberal intellectual, never a militarist | Monarchist annexationist during WWI |
| **Mode of internationalism** | Philosophical and economic -- believed in interdependence | Tactical and diplomatic -- accommodation as strategy |
| **Relationship to nationalism** | "I am a German of Jewish origin" -- German identity was existential, contested | Uncomplicated German nationalism; peaceful means to nationalist ends |
| **Relationship to the West** | Fulfillment as good faith -- to demonstrate impossibility of Versailles terms | Fulfillment as leverage -- to build trust for eventual revision |
| **Relationship to the East** | Rapallo: broke isolation by working with the USSR | Treaty of Berlin: maintained eastern option while pursuing western integration |
| **How they died** | Assassinated by right-wing extremists for being who he was | Died naturally; his legacy contested between "good European" and "clever nationalist" |
| **Model dimension** | Moral conviction + identity vulnerability | Strategic calculation + nationalist goals by peaceful means |

Both men believed Germany's future lay in international cooperation, not conquest. Both were willing to work with the Weimar Republic's democratic institutions. Both were ultimately destroyed by forces that rejected everything they represented -- Rathenau literally, Stresemann in historical memory. Their careers demonstrate that internationalism in early 20th-century Germany was possible, effective, and existentially dangerous.
