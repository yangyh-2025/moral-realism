# Georges Clemenceau and Aristide Briand: French Security and French Internationalism (1917-1940)

## Overview

France presents the most compressed case study of the tension between security-driven nationalism and cooperative internationalism in the 1914-1950 period. Two figures embody the poles: Georges Clemenceau ("The Tiger"), the radical republican who led France to victory in 1918 and demanded maximal security at Versailles; and Aristide Briand, the great conciliator who sought Franco-German reconciliation through Locarno, the League of Nations, and European integration. France was simultaneously the power that had suffered most from German aggression (1.4 million dead, industrial northeast devastated) and the power whose security demands were impossible for any international system to fully satisfy. This structural position -- the insecurity of the victor -- is a key variable for the ABM model.

---

## Part I: Georges Clemenceau (1841-1929)

### 1. "The Tiger" and "Pere la Victoire"

Clemenceau became Prime Minister for the second time in November 1917, at age 76, with France near collapse. The Nivelle Offensive (April 1917) had failed catastrophically, triggering widespread mutinies in the French army. Russia was collapsing. The United States had entered the war but its army had not yet arrived in strength.

**His First Speech as Premier (20 November 1917)**:

> "I wage war. Domestic policy -- I wage war. Foreign policy -- I wage war. Always, everywhere, I wage war."

This was not rhetoric. Clemenceau centralized decision-making, imposed strict press censorship to suppress defeatism, arrested suspected defeatists (including former Prime Minister Joseph Caillaux, whom he had tried for treason), and made frequent visits to the front to maintain contact with soldiers. He was called "Pere la Victoire" not affectionately but in recognition that he embodied the national will to fight.

**Civilian Control of the Military**

Clemenceau's most famous aphorism is often misquoted. The actual formulation:

> "War is too serious a matter to be left to the generals." (Original: "La guerre! C'est une chose trop grave pour la confier a des militaires.")

He maintained firm civilian control over Foch, Petain, and the military, refusing to let them dictate political terms. This distinguishes him from Franz Joseph (who deferred to Conrad), Nicholas II (who deferred to the generals and ultimately took personal command), and even Lloyd George (who struggled continuously with Haig and Robertson).

### 2. French Security Demands at Versailles

France approached the peace conference from a position of profound demographic and strategic anxiety:

| France | Germany |
|--------|---------|
| 39 million population | 60+ million population |
| 1.4 million military dead | 2 million military dead |
| Industrial northeast devastated (10 departements) | German territory essentially untouched by war |
| Birth rate declining since 1850s | Higher birth rate |
| Won the war | Lost the war |

French security demands were maximal because French insecurity was structural:

1. **Alsace-Lorraine**: Return without plebiscite (achieved)
2. **Rhineland**: Separation from Germany or permanent Allied occupation (compromised: 15-year occupation, permanent demilitarization)
3. **Saar coal**: Compensation for destroyed French mines (achieved: 15-year League administration, French economic control)
4. **Reparations**: Germany pays the full cost of reconstruction (achieved in principle through Article 231 "war guilt clause" and the Reparations Commission, but never fully collected)
5. **Anglo-American security guarantee**: A formal treaty promising military support if Germany attacked again (negotiated but the US Senate rejected it, causing the British guarantee to lapse)
6. **German disarmament**: 100,000-man army, no air force, no submarines, no heavy artillery, no general staff (achieved in the treaty text but enforcement was weak)
7. **Polish independence and a strong Poland**: As an eastern counterweight to Germany (achieved)

### 3. Clemenceau at Versailles: The Clash with Wilson

The Clemenceau-Wilson dynamic was the central drama of the Paris Peace Conference. Their worldviews were in fundamental opposition:

| Clemenceau | Wilson |
|-----------|--------|
| France had been invaded twice by Germany in his lifetime (1870, 1914) | America was protected by two oceans |
| Security through power: German weakness | Security through law: the League of Nations |
| "Germany will always be a menace" | "The German people, once freed from autocracy, will be peaceful" |
| The League is useless without military force | The League's moral authority will be sufficient |
| France bleeds; America lectures | America sacrifices; Europe squabbles |

**The Famous Exchange**:

Clemenceau's alleged quip -- "Wilson bores me with his 14 Points; God only needed 10" -- is widely cited but its provenance is uncertain. What is documented is Clemenceau's comment to his aide Andre Tardieu:

> "Mr. Wilson talks like Jesus Christ and acts like Lloyd George."

And his more substantive criticism in the Council of Four:

> "Do not believe that the League of Nations is capable of guaranteeing the security of France. The only guarantee of security is that Germany shall be in a position where she cannot renew the war. I do not believe in the League of Nations as an effective instrument for the prevention of war."

Raymond Poincare (French President during the war: 1913-1920) was even more skeptical of Wilson, privately recording that Wilson "speaks like a secular prophet who has received a direct mandate from God."

### 4. What Clemenceau Won and Lost

**Won**:
- Alsace-Lorraine (his overriding priority, shared by all French)
- 15-year Rhineland occupation with staged evacuation
- Permanent demilitarization of the Rhineland
- Saar coal under French economic control for 15 years
- War guilt clause and reparations principle
- German disarmament provisions
- French mandates in Syria and Lebanon

**Lost**:
- Permanent separation of the Rhineland from Germany (blocked by Wilson and Lloyd George)
- Formal Anglo-American military guarantee (signed but the US Senate killed it in March 1920; British guarantee then lapsed)
- The degree of German disarmament Foch demanded (Foch: "This is not a peace, it is an armistice for twenty years")
- Full reparations collection (Germany never paid the full amount; the Dawes and Young Plans reduced the burden)

**Foch's Prophecy**:

When Clemenceau accepted the compromise on the Rhineland, Marshal Foch -- who had demanded permanent occupation of the Rhine as France's military frontier -- reportedly said:

> "This is not peace. It is an armistice for twenty years."

World War II began 20 years and 65 days later.

### 5. Clemenceau's Defeat and Retirement

In January 1920, Clemenceau was defeated in the presidential election by Paul Deschanel (who was himself incapacitated by mental illness months later). Clemenceau retired, traveled, and wrote. He died in November 1929, weeks after the Wall Street Crash and a month after the death of his great antagonist Gustav Stresemann.

His final years were spent warning that Germany would rise again. His 1920 observation:

> "The northern part of France is destroyed, but Germany remains. Germany is still there with 60 million inhabitants. And remember what I'm telling you: twenty years from now, the Germans will take their revenge."

### 6. Clemenceau's Worldview: Radical Patriotism

Clemenceau was not a reactionary monarchist like Franz Joseph or an erratic autocrat like Nicholas II. He was a radical republican -- anti-clerical (he had famously fought to separate church and state in 1905), fiercely committed to the Republic, a veteran of the Paris Commune era, and an intellectual (he was a friend of Monet and a significant journalist). His nationalism was not dynastic or ethnic but civic-republican: France was worth dying for because it was the Republic.

This makes his security maximalism not a matter of personal prestige (Franz Joseph) or erratic conviction (Nicholas II) but of rational threat assessment: Germany was larger, richer, more populous, and had invaded twice in living memory. Clemenceau did not trust Germans to become peaceful because he did not trust any state to be peaceful without the means of coercion.

**Significance for the model**: Clemenceau represents a rational security-maximizing leader type (`霸权型` or `强权型` without the prestige/emotional distortion). His demands followed from strategic calculation, not dynastic honour or personal vanity. This is a different version of "national interest" than Franz Joseph's -- one grounded in objective threat assessment rather than dynastic prestige preservation.

## Part II: Aristide Briand (1862-1932)

### 1. The Internationalist Counterpoint

Aristide Briand served as Prime Minister of France 11 times between 1909 and 1929, and as Foreign Minister continuously from 1925 until his death in 1932. If Clemenceau embodied French security nationalism, Briand embodied the attempt to escape the security dilemma through international cooperation.

**Briand's journey**: He had been a socialist, a syndicalist, and a fierce anti-militarist in his youth. During WWI, he served as Prime Minister (1915-1917), navigating the war's middle years. His transformation into the great conciliator of the 1920s was not a repudiation of his wartime service but a recognition that only Franco-German reconciliation could prevent the next war.

### 2. The Locarno Treaties (1925)

Briand's greatest achievement, shared with Stresemann and British Foreign Secretary Austen Chamberlain. The Locarno Treaties:

- Germany, France, and Belgium mutually guaranteed their western borders
- Britain and Italy acted as guarantors
- Germany signed arbitration conventions with Poland and Czechoslovakia
- Germany did NOT recognize its eastern borders as permanent -- Stresemann's deliberate ambiguity

For Briand, Locarno was the first step in a process that would eventually include the eastern borders. He believed -- or hoped -- that the "Locarno spirit" of cooperation would make territorial revision unnecessary or irrelevant.

**Briand, Chamberlain, and Stresemann** shared the 1926 Nobel Peace Prize.

### 3. The Briand-Kellogg Pact (1928)

Briand proposed to American Secretary of State Frank Kellogg a bilateral treaty renouncing war between France and the United States. Kellogg expanded it into a multilateral pact. Eventually 62 nations signed the Kellogg-Briand Pact, which "outlawed war as an instrument of national policy."

The fatal weakness: no enforcement mechanism. It was a moral declaration, not an operational treaty. Briand knew this but believed the normative effect was valuable in itself.

### 4. The European Federal Union Proposal (1929)

In September 1929, Briand proposed to the League of Nations Assembly a "federal link" among European states -- the first formal proposal for European integration from a major power leader. His memorandum of May 1930 ("Memorandum on the Organization of a Regime of European Federal Union") proposed:

- A European Conference with regular meetings
- A permanent political committee as executive organ
- Economic cooperation to precede political union
- Preservation of national sovereignty within the federal framework
- Open to all European states including Germany and the USSR

The proposal failed. The Great Depression had begun. Britain was hostile (the Empire came first). Germany was in political crisis. Mussolini's Italy was uninterested. The USSR saw it as a capitalist encirclement scheme.

### 5. Why Briand's Internationalism Failed

The structural reasons Briand's project failed are instructive for the model:

1. **French insecurity couldn't be resolved by treaties alone.** Every German concession (Locarno, League entry, Kellogg-Briand) was met with French demands for more security -- not because France was greedy but because the underlying asymmetry (German demographic and industrial superiority) remained.

2. **German revisionism could not be permanently satisfied through peaceful accommodation.** Stresemann's peaceful revisionism was genuine, but Stresemann died in 1929. His successors were not Stresemann. And even Stresemann had wanted the Polish Corridor back.

3. **The Depression destroyed the economic basis of reconciliation.** The Dawes and Young Plans depended on American loans. When those loans dried up after 1929, both the German economy and Franco-German reconciliation collapsed.

4. **Anglo-American absence.** Britain prioritized imperial trade preferences over European federalism. The United States never joined the League. A European security system without the Atlantic powers was inherently incomplete.

5. **Briand died in March 1932.** Like Stresemann (1929), Henderson (1935), and Rathenau (assassinated 1922), the internationalist leaders kept dying before their work was complete. By 1933, Hitler was Chancellor.

### 6. Briand's Vision vs. Political Reality

Briand's most revealing statement came in a private conversation with a German diplomat shortly before his death:

> "So long as I am here, there will be no war between France and Germany. But I cannot live forever."

He understood that his project depended on personal relationships (with Stresemann, with Chamberlain) and that those relationships were mortal. He could not institutionalize the "spirit of Locarno" before he died.

## Part III: Comparison and Model Implications

### Clemenceau and Briand as Complementary French Types

| Dimension | Clemenceau | Briand |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| **Core fear** | German power will always menace France | The next war will destroy European civilization |
| **Solution** | Permanent German weakness through enforced disarmament, occupation, and alliances | Permanent peace through institutionalized cooperation and economic integration |
| **Relationship to Germany** | Distrust -- deter and constrain | Conditional trust -- integrate and transform |
| **Relationship to the League** | Useful only if backed by military force | Valuable as a normative framework, even without enforcement |
| **Model leader type** | `霸权型` / `强权型` (rational security maximizer) | `王道型` (internationalist, cooperation-focused) |
| **Historical vindication?** | Foch's 20-year prediction was accurate. But Clemenceau's maximalist program was politically impossible. | Briand's vision anticipated the European Union. But it failed to prevent WWII. |

**Both were right about different things.** Clemenceau was right that Germany would menace peace again; his proposed solutions were politically impossible because the Anglo-American powers would not guarantee France permanently. Briand was right that Franco-German reconciliation was the only lasting solution; his program was chronologically impossible because he died before it could be institutionalized.

### The French Security Dilemma as a Model Parameter

France's structural position generates a generalizable pattern for the ABM model:

1. **Victorious powers can be existentially insecure.** Winning a war does not guarantee feeling safe. France won WWI and felt more threatened than ever.
2. **Security demands are infinite in an anarchic system.** There is no natural stopping point. Clemenceau wanted the Rhineland. Foch wanted permanent occupation. The right wing wanted annexation. Where does "enough security" begin?
3. **Internationalism from weakness looks different from internationalism from strength.** Wilson's internationalism came from a position of American safety. Briand's internationalism came from French vulnerability. The same policy (support the League, seek disarmament, promote cooperation) had different meanings in different structural positions.
4. **The peace settlement's legitimacy depends on enforcement.** When the enforcers (USA, Britain) withdraw, the settlement becomes a piece of paper. France was left alone to enforce Versailles, which it could not do.
